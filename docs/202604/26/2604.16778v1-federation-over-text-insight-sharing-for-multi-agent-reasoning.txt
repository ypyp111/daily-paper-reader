## ABSTRACT 

LLM-powered agents often reason from scratch when presented with a new problem instance and lack automatic mechanisms to transfer learned skills to other agents. We propose a federated learning-like framework, Federation over Text (FoT), that enables multiple agents solving differ-ent tasks to collectively generate a shared library of metacognitive insights by iteratively federating their local reasoning processes. Instead of federation over gradients (e.g., as in distributed train-ing), FoT operates at the semantic level without any gradient optimization or supervision signal. Iteratively, each agent does local thinking and self-improvement on their specific tasks indepen-dently, and shares reasoning traces with a central server, which aggregates and distills them into a cross-task (and cross-domain) insight library that existing and future agents can leverage to improve performance on related tasks. Experiments show that FoT improves reasoning effectiveness and efficiency across a wide range of challenging applications, including mathematical problem solv-ing, cross-domain collaboration, and machine learning research insight discovery. Specifically, it improves average accuracies of downstream tasks by 24% while reducing the reasoning tokens by 28% across the first two applications. In the research insight discovery application, FoT is able to generate insights that cover over 90% of the major contributions in the subsequent papers. 

## 1 Introduction 

LLM-powered agents are increasingly deployed for complex, multi-step tasks (e.g., Yoran et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024; Schmidgall et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Zhang, 2026). However, they can suffer from two major limitations. First, reasoning can be inefficient: agents often reason from scratch given an input instance, repeatedly solving similar problems, which can be computationally expensive and slow. Second, reasoning in multi-agent systems typically occurs in an isolated manner, where agents are chained by passing prompts or intermediate outputs (e.g., Ke et al., 2025). While agents exchange task solutions within a session, their internal thinking processes and full reasoning histories are not explicitly stored or communicated. As a result, these hard-won thinking processes are lost and cannot be reused or shared across agents. It remains underexplored how to automatically transfer, reuse, or distill independent reasoning traces to help guide and improve future reasoning processes for similar agents or across domains. At the same time, classical distributed or federated paradigms for knowledge sharing (McMahan et al., 2017) may not be suitable for learning generalizable insights from agents’ reasoning traces. Averaging the gradients or weights of entire (or part of) large generative models can be computationally prohibitive, impossible for closed-source LLMs, and more importantly, not interpretable as parameter aggregation is not explicitly equivalent to sharing abstract, high-level reasoning summaries .To this end, we propose federation over text (FoT), a federated-like framework that enables a set of agents to col-laboratively and iteratively build a shared, evolving library of reusable insights from independent local reasoning traces (expressed in natural language) without communicating actual problem instances. Unlike traditional multi-agent systems that decompose a single complex task into subtasks, FoT operates across multiple independent agents over diverse but related tasks. Each agent solves some task with a base language model, and our goal is to generate an insight library—a high-level aggregation of the common principles and ideas shared across the agents’ tasks.

Unlike traditional distributed learning, which averages gradients or model updates in a high-dimensional, uninter-pretable space, FoT operates at the semantic , text level without any parameter optimization, fine-tuning, or reinforce-ment learning. In particular, at each round, agents transmit concise metacognitive summaries of their problem-solving processes to a central server (can be physical or logically virtual). The server, acting as an aggregator, identifies and distills recurring reasoning fragments from these summaries into new, explicit insights and sends the insight library back to the agents for the next round. We note that FoT is a general framework that allows each agent to use any local reasoning model combined with any local self-improvement workflows for their individual tasks. Our framework only requires reasoning traces to be shared at each iteration. Meanwhile, we note that our proposed FoT approach is analogous to the classic federated/distributed learning setting, where these two regimes can be interpreted within the same conceptual framework (for instance, see side-by-side com-parisons in Table 1). Additionally, FoT offers similar privacy benefits in that only abstract reasoning traces are shared instead of raw problem instances. By making these connections and drawing inspiration from federated optimization, FoT enables a range of potential design choices regarding multi-agent reasoning evaluation, service personalization, and how to execute better joint reasoning. We discuss these implications in more detail in Section 3.1. In this work, we apply FoT to challenging settings including mathematical problem solving, multi-domain collabora-tion (scientific question-answering and coding), and research insight discovery (Section 4). We compare our frame-work with a range of baselines, such as agent-independent problem-solving and other forms of cross-agent reasoning trace sharing. Across applications of mathematical problem solving and multi-domain collaboration, FoT improves the accuracy of the downstream tasks by 24% on average while reducing the reasoning tokens by 28%. For research insight discovery, we demonstrate that an insight library learned from ICLR papers in one year can cover the core technical contributions of over 90% of accepted papers in the next year, where we guarantee that the base LLM model has not accessed the papers in the next year. Moreover, we highlight that FoT can improve over the state-of-the-art ap-proach on benchmark datasets. For instance, on GPQA-Diamond, Gemini-3.0-Pro-Preview (as the base model) agents using FoT achieve 94.42%, surpassing the previous mark of 94.1% achieved by Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview alone. 

## 2 Related Work 

Multi-Agent Collaboration. Several existing multi-agent works focus on enabling agents to solve a given complicated task in a collaborative way. A common paradigm involves decomposing a task into smaller subtasks, assigning each to an individual agent, and aggregating the partial results (Zhang et al., 2024b; Tran et al., 2025; Dang et al., 2025; Chung & Du, 2025; Lyu et al., 2026). Another popular approach is to allow multiple agents to repeatedly perform the same task and vote on a solution (Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2025). Other forms of multi-agent collaboration include debate (Liang et al., 2024), which allows agents to engage in structured dialogue to produce better answers for a specific problem. Previous works also study large-scale agentic knowledge exchange by building concrete workflows for agent laboratories (Schmidgall et al., 2025), or simulating research communities (Yu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a). Rather than using multiple agents to handle different components of a single task or building end-to-end engineering workflows to simulate complicated systems, FoT focuses on aggregating shared, high-level insights from indepen-dent local problem-solving processes across multiple agents. This shifts the focus from task-level coordination to knowledge-level aggregation. We primarily focus on the intrinsic reasoning capabilities of LLMs and do not consider integrating external tools, though FoT is compatible with tool-augmented agents and can be extended to larger-scale agentic systems. 

Collaborative and Federated Learning. Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) aggregates model parame-ters from multiple clients while keeping data local. Existing literature on federated agents primarily focuses on training language models in the classical parameter or gradient-level aggregation framework (Li et al., 2025), which can be expensive and insufficient for capturing high-level abstract reasoning strategies. Social learning (Mohtashami et al., 2023) allows teacher agents to generate synthetic examples or abstract prompts that are aggregated to train a student model in a single-task setting. FoT and FL share interesting connections (Section 3.1); FoT operates in the semantic space while being compatible with heterogeneous agent models and task domains. 

LLM Skills and Recursive Self-Improvement. To embed past experience into LLM-based agents, existing ap-proaches such as Anthropic Skills (Anthropic, n.d.), Agents.md 2, vibe research (Zhang, 2026), and scientific skills (K-Dense Inc., 2026) rely on human-authored or AI-generated content, often with human guidance via prompt engineer-ing, to explicitly package such knowledge or engineered instructions as part of the prompt. There is extensive research on improving agent reasoning through self-reflection, self-improvement, or context engineering (Yoran et al., 2023;

## 3 Federation over Text 

In this section, we propose our federation over text framework and discuss its connections with federated learning (i.e., gradient aggregation) (McMahan et al., 2017). Federated learning (FL) focuses on training a shared global model to serve existing and future clients, whereas FoT aims to build an insight library that helps both existing and new agents to better solve a variety of tasks. In the most common FL workflow, the server broadcasts the current global model to each client, who then performs local training on their local data and transmits the local model updates back to the server. The server aggregates the model updates to form a new global model. This iterative process repeats until convergence. At a high level, in FoT, at each round, each agent solves its local task via any base LLM model with the help of the current insight library, and sends the reasoning traces to the server. The server merges them to update the library and distributes it to all agents for the next round. In the workflow of FoT (the right subfigure of Figure 1) , each of the (potentially many) decentralized agents solves a specific problem or task, using a selected base LLM. These tasks can span different domains such as mathematical reasoning, coding, and scientific question-answering. Given a task (e.g., a collection of math questions), an agent first solves the task (e.g., generates solutions), then reflects on whether the reasoning process contains reusable techniques and what key skills can be extracted. Instead of uploading raw reasoning histories, each agent generates a reasoning trace for each problem based on its solution and reflections, specifying when and how to apply the extracted skills via step-by-step instructions. Agents then share these summarized traces with the server, while keeping problem instances and raw histories local. In each round, after collecting traces, the server needs to aggregate them to update the insight library. We develop one specific aggregation technique that distills high-level insights rather than simply concatenating reasoning traces. In particular, the server first clusters reasoning traces that share similar skills, and then uncovers connections among traces that solve the same problem using different skills and can potentially be merged. Finally, the server broadcasts the updated insights library to all agents for the next iteration. The aggregated library can be re-used as an external tool to improve the reasoning of either existing agents or future agents that are not in the current network. We provide all the designed prompts that we use in Appendix D. 

Local Reasoning and Global Aggregation Strategies. We note that many self-improvement methods proposed by prior works (e.g., Yoran et al., 2023; Didolkar et al., 2025; Zhuang et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026, 2025b) can be plugged into FoT to generate the reasoning traces sent to the server. In our experiments (Section 5.1), we demonstrate the alternative methodology of Didolkar et al. (2025) as the local thinking method. FoT also allows for the decoupling 3Federation over Text: Insight Sharing for Multi-Agent Reasoning A P REPRINT 

of the LLM used to create the insight library and the model used to solve the tasks (Section 4.1). In Section 5, we empirically study the effects of heterogeneous base LLMs and other important FoT parameters such as the library size.

When examining incorrect reasoning processes (e.g., Reasoning Processes 1 and 3 in the Appendix), an interesting phenomenon occurs: there are steps that are totally irrelevant to the final answer. 4.3 Research Insight Discovery 

In this application, we aim to collectively discover insights from different areas of machine learning research using FoT via a proxy of published research papers. Another line of work focuses on building end-to-end agentic workflows for executing the entire scientific pipeline, producing submission-ready research papers (Schmidgall et al., 2025; Jansen et al., 2025; Weng et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2026; Lyu et al., 2026; Song et al., 2026), which is not the goal of FoT. In our setting, each agent is assigned to one (or multiple) paper(s) and tasked with identifying the primary novel contributions. FoT aims to curate a library that contains actionable and generalizable research insights. We then evaluate how many future papers could be guided by these insights in the library as their core contributions. Such evaluation judges is based on four aspects: concrete methodology usage, how methods are presented, whether the paper’s core contribution would fundamentally differ without the insight, and whether the insight is novel; see details in Prompt 5 in the appendix. ## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we have proposed the first federated-like multi-agent reasoning framework, Federation over Text (FoT), which enables multiple agents (each solving a task) to collaboratively learn an insight library by sharing and aggre-gating local reasoning traces iteratively. Such insight libraries capture high-level, reusable insights, allowing agents to learn from past experience and generalize to future cross-domain tasks. Operating purely in the semantic, text space without additional training or gradient optimization, FoT is compatible with heterogeneous agent models, varying local reasoning and self-improvement approaches, and different global aggregation strategies. We have demonstrated the effectiveness of FoT across diverse applications, including mathematical reasoning, cross-domain collaboration, and research insight discovery. Further analyses of reasoning traces, extracted insights, and ablation studies reveal how insights guide performance gains and highlight the flexibility of FoT with different local reasoning strategies and server-side aggregation methods. Our work establishes connections between federated learn-ing and multi-agent systems so that we can explore other design choices in distributed optimization for multi-agent reasoning. In future work, we aim to automate prompt design for FoT and extend FoT to multimodal inputs.

## A Usage of AI in Writing

Reasoning Trace 2: Directional Skill Evolution 

This technique involves systematically transforming specific, verified solutions into more general and reusable tools (skills) to enrich a knowledge base. Instead of just storing a specific answer, the system actively ’evolves’ it to cover broader cases. When to use: When building a knowledge base or skill library from specific examples, particularly in domains like mathematics or coding where a specific solution (e.g., for 2D vectors) often hints at a general principle (e.g., for N-dimensions). It is used to proactively expand the capabilities of an AI agent without requiring new external problem sets. Step-by-step: 1) Select a specific, verified skill or lemma from the existing library (the seed) 2) Choose one of four specific evolution heuristics: ’Parameterize’ (replace specific constants like numbers with variables), ’Extend Dimensions’ (generalize from specific dimensions to n-dimensions), ’Scale Complexity’ (create a more complex version of the problem), or ’Identify Key Concepts’ (extract and formalize the core underlying principle) 3) Retrieve relevant unsolved problems or requests to guide the evolution context, ensuring the new skill aims at actual gaps 4) Prompt the model to rewrite the seed skill according to the chosen heuristic 5) Formally verify the generated skill; if valid and distinct from existing skills, add it to the library.
