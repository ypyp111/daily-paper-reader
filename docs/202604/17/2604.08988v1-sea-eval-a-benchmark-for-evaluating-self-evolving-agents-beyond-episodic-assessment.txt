# SEA-Eval: A Benchmark for Evaluating Self-Evolving Agents Beyond Episodic Assessment 

## Sihang Jiang, Lipeng Ma, Zhonghua Hong, Keyi Wang, Zhiyu Lu, Shisong Chen, Jinghao Zhang, Tianjun Pan, Weijia Zhou, Jiaqing Liang, Yanghua Xiao 

## Fudan University 

## Abstract 

Current LLM-based agents demonstrate strong performance in episodic task execution but remain constrained by static toolsets and episodic amnesia, failing to accumulate experi-ence or optimize strategies across task bound-aries. While the Self-Evolving Agent (SEA) paradigm has been previously proposed, this pa-per contributes a new formal definition of SEA grounded in digital embodiment and continu-ous cross-task evolution, and introduces SEA-Eval, the first benchmark designed to evalu-ate SEA characteristics across two dimensions, intra-task execution reliability and long-term evolutionary performance. By organizing tasks into sequential streams and analyzing Success Rate and Token Consumption over time, SEA-Eval quantifies evolutionary gain and structural stability in ways that existing episodic bench-marks cannot. Empirical evaluations reveal a significant evolutionary bottleneck in current state-of-the-art frameworks, where identical success rates mask up to 31.2× differences in token consumption and divergent evolutionary trajectories under sequential analysis. SEA-Eval provides a rigorous scientific foundation for advancing agents from mere task executors toward genuinely self-evolving digital entities. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Model (LLM) agents have emerged as a pivotal paradigm for translating generative intelligence into tangible productivity. Since the inception of tool-augmented reasoning, agents have demonstrated substantial industrial utility across domains such as autonomous soft-ware engineering, scientific discovery, and complex workflow orchestration. Recent advancements in system-native architectures, exemplified by frame-works like OpenClaw 1, have propelled agents be-yond the confines of text-based reasoning engines.

By achieving deep integration with operating sys-tems and native applications, these agents are tran-sitioning toward a state of digital embodiment : no longer mere semantic processors, but functional ac-tors capable of sustaining high-load industrial tasks directly upon the underlying digital infrastructure. This evolutionary trajectory points toward a new agentic paradigm: the Self-Evolving Agent (SEA) .The core characteristics of an SEA are defined by two properties. First, complex interaction in open-ended environments : the agent operates di-rectly upon raw GUI pixels, dynamic DOM trees, and low-level system interfaces, unconstrained by predefined API boundaries. Second, continuous, multi-turn evolution : by maintaining persistent memory across task boundaries, the agent trans-forms historical interaction experience into cogni-tive priors that reconfigure the execution of subse-quent tasks. Architecturally, the SEA is centered on the Evolutionary Flywheel —a closed-loop mech-anism formed by the symbiotic coupling of an Exe-cution Hub and a Cognition Hub . The Execution Hub acts as the agent’s digital limb, performing perception and action while obtaining unmediated feedback from the environment; the Cognition Hub serves as the cognitive core, providing real-time guidance during execution and distilling raw in-teraction trajectories into persistent cognitive as-sets. Through this dual-hub coupling, the SEA overcomes the episodic amnesia (Luo et al., 2025) inherent in traditional agent lifecycles, achieving a fundamental transition from fragmented task pro-cessing to continuous capability evolution. However, the novel characteristics of SEAs ex-pose a fundamental mismatch with existing evalu-ation frameworks. Current benchmarks adopt the isolated task episode as the basic unit of evaluation, whereas the SEA paradigm requires cross-task be-havioral continuity—prior experience is systemat-ically discarded at every task boundary, making the assessment of cross-task knowledge accumula-1

2.1 LLM Agents and Episodic Amnesia 

The development of autonomous agents has pro-gressed from restricted, rule-based systems to in-creasingly open-ended and adaptive entities (Wang et al., 2024; ang Gao et al., 2026; Xi et al., 2025). The emergence of LLMs has catalyzed a new gen-eration of agents capable of interleaving reasoning, planning, and tool use, forming iterative reasoning– action loops that enable interaction with complex environments (Yehudai et al., 2025; Rahman et al., 2025). However, current LLM-based agents re-main fundamentally constrained by episodic am-nesia. We extend this concept from cognitive sci-ence to the agent lifecycle: just as patients with episodic amnesia retain procedural skills yet cannot encode specific experiences into persistent mem-ory, LLM agents demonstrate strong within-task reasoning yet reset their cognitive state at every task boundary, failing to transform interaction experi-ence into reusable cross-task cognitive assets (Luo et al., 2025; Du, 2026). This limitation has been studied in the continual learning literature, where methods such as EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) mitigate forgetting through parameter-level update mechanisms (Cao et al., 2025). However, these ap-proaches define evolution at the algorithm level and presuppose specific update mechanisms, whereas SEA defines evolution at the architecture level with-out constraining implementation. SEA further op-erates over open-ended digital environments rather than closed task distributions, and grounds evolu-tion in external memory rather than model parame-ters. 

2.2 Technical Foundations of Self-Evolving Agents 

Prior work has advanced the technical capabili-ties relevant to SEA along two lines correspond-ing to its dual-hub architecture. On the Execu-tion Hub side, CREATOR (Qian et al., 2023), Voyager (Wang et al., 2023), and ToolLLM (Qin et al., 2024) have explored tool creation, skill li-brary construction, and large-scale tool selection respectively, while embodied AI research (Brohan et al., 2023; Wahle et al., 2022) has extended the perception–action paradigm to physical and digital environments. On the Cognition Hub side, Reflex-ion (Shinn et al., 2023), ExpeL (Zhao et al., 2024), and MemGPT (Packer et al., 2023) have advanced memory system construction; CoT (Wei et al., 2022) and ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) have improved planning and reasoning; and Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) and CRITIC (Gou et al., 2024) have strengthened self-reflection mechanisms. Nevertheless, these works each advance isolated capabilities without recognizing that the underlying dimensions are structurally interdependent: mem-ory quality depends on reflection, reflection signals derive from execution, and execution efficiency re-flects memory effectiveness. No prior work has formalized this interdependence as a closed-loop system whose coupling conditions must be jointly satisfied. To the best of our knowledge, this paper provides the first formal characterization of this closed-loop structure. 

2.3 Limitations of Existing Evaluation Frameworks 

Because prior work treats these dimensions as in-dependent techniques rather than a closed-loop system, existing benchmarks have not been de-signed to capture the quality of closed-loop evo-lution. Mainstream benchmarks including We-bArena (Zhou et al., 2024), OSWorld (Xie et al., 2024), SWE-bench (Jimenez et al., 2024), τ -bench (Yao et al., 2024), and AgentBench (Liu et al., 2025) all adopt the single task episode as the basic evaluation unit, effectively measuring im-mediate execution competence while remaining blind to cross-task evolutionary dynamics. Re-cent works including LifelongAgentBench (Zheng et al., 2025), MetaTool (Huang et al., 2023), Ham-merBench (Wang et al., 2025), and EverMem-Bench (Hu et al., 2026) have begun to explore expe-rience reuse and efficiency dimensions, but treat ef-ficiency as a static single-task property rather than a dynamic trend across sequential task streams. If an agent’s token consumption fails to converge af-ter repeated execution of structurally similar tasks, this is the true signal of absent evolutionary capabil-ity, yet it remains entirely invisible under existing frameworks. Among available efficiency proxies, token consumption most faithfully reflects this dy-namic: execution step count may remain stable even as per-step reasoning depth increases, and wall-clock latency introduces hardware-dependent variance that compromises cross-environment re-producibility. This gap manifests across three dimensions that directly motivate SEA-Eval’s design. First, on eval-uation unit, SEA-Eval replaces isolated episodes with cross-task behavioral trajectories. Second, 3on efficiency metrics, SEA-Eval tracks the con-vergence trajectory of token consumption rather than static single-task measurements. Third, on sequential design, SEA-Eval introduces persistent memory configurations and ordered task streams, enabling for the first time the independent quan-tification of evolutionary gain and structural stabil-ity (ang Gao et al., 2026; Cai et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025). 

## 3 The SEA Paradigm: Characteristics, Consequences, and Structural Necessity 

3.1 Foundational Characteristics of the SEA Paradigm 

We formally define a Self-Evolving Agent as a tu-ple SEA = ( E, A, M, Φ, Ψ) , where E denotes an open-ended digital environment characterized by dynamic state spaces, heterogeneous interac-tion modalities, and the absence of pre-enumerated action constraints; A an open and extensible ac-tion space subject to autonomous expansion; M =

Mp ∪ M e ∪ M d a persistent stratified memory maintained across task boundaries, encompass-ing procedural knowledge Mp (reusable execu-tion strategies), episodic knowledge Me (histori-cal trajectories and failure attributions), and declar-ative knowledge Md (environmental constraints and user preference models); Φ : τ → M an experience distillation function mapping raw inter-action trajectories to structured memory updates; and Ψ : Mt → M t+1 a selective cross-task mem-ory transfer function. The defining departure from prior agent architectures lies in the existence of 

Φ and Ψ: in episodic agents, M is reset at every task boundary and A remains static; in an SEA, both are subject to continuous expansion and re-finement. An agent that resets its cognitive state at every task boundary is, by definition, not evolving— regardless of the sophistication of its within-task reasoning. This definition is grounded in two non-negotiable foundational characteristics. 

Complex Interaction in Open-ended Environ-ments. An SEA operates within open-ended digital ecosystems that are not constrained to application-specific domains or pre-enumerated ac-tion schemas. Unlike benchmarks that evaluate agents within simplified synthetic environments or sandboxes restricted to specific applications, the environments in which SEAs are deployed exhibit dynamic state spaces, heterogeneous interaction modalities, and persistent stochasticity that cannot be fully anticipated at design time. Under these con-ditions, the agent must engage directly with raw GUI pixels, dynamic DOM trees, and low-level system interfaces—a mode of operation we term 

digital embodiment : the capacity for unmediated, system-native interaction with the underlying com-putational substrate, unconstrained by predefined interface abstractions. 

Continuous, Multi-turn Evolution. The second characteristic defines the temporal boundary of the SEA paradigm. While episodic agents reset M

upon task completion, an SEA maintains state per-sistence across unbounded deployment cycles via 

Ψ. The outcomes of prior tasks—potentially oc-curring hours or days earlier—are retained in M

and actively reconfigure the execution path of sub-sequent, structurally unrelated interactions. This property overcomes the episodic amnesia inher-ent in context-window-bounded systems and estab-lishes cross-task behavioral continuity as a defini-tional requirement: an agent that resets its cogni-tive state at every task boundary is, by definition, not evolving, regardless of the sophistication of its within-task reasoning. 

3.2 Necessary Consequences of the SEA Characteristics 

The two foundational characteristics defined above are not merely descriptive—they entail necessary behavioral consequences that any SEA must ex-hibit in open-ended deployment. We identify two such derived properties, each following by logical necessity from one of the foundational characteris-tics. 

Autonomous Tool Synthesis. The first characteristic—complex interaction in open-ended environments—directly entails the necessity of autonomous tool synthesis. In environments where action constraints are not pre-enumerated, pre-defined tool schemas will inevitably encounter unhandled states: the agent will face interaction requirements that no existing tool in A is equipped to satisfy. Under these conditions, the agent must autonomously synthesize new functional primitives via Code-as-Policies, generating interaction scripts or toolchains in situ and registering validated outputs as persistent tools. This process directly implements the expansion of A: the cardinality 

|A| grows monotonically over deployment time as a function of environmental engagement rather 4Figure 1: The Self-Evolving Agent (SEA) framework. 

than pre-programmed capability. Autonomous tool synthesis is therefore not an optional capability enhancement but a structural necessity imposed by the open-ended nature of E.

Implicit User Alignment. The second characteristic—continuous, multi-turn evolution— directly entails the progressive alignment of the agent’s behavior with user-specific preferences. Over extended deployment cycles, the SEA accumulates interaction data with a specific user, including corrective signals such as rollbacks, revisions, and explicit feedback. These signals constitute a high-dimensional reward source that, through repeated application of Φ, drives the progressive encoding of user-specific workflows and implicit preferences into Md. The result is a longitudinal alignment process in which the agent’s execution strategies increasingly mirror the user’s latent intent—not through one-time calibration, but through continuous distillation of accumulated interaction friction. This emergent alignment is a necessary consequence of sustained multi-turn evolution with a consistent user, rather than a separately engineered component. 

3.3 Structural Necessity: The Evolutionary Flywheel 

To instantiate the characteristics defined in Sec-tion 3.1, an SEA requires a structural backbone capable of closing the loop between raw environ-mental interaction and the persistent updates to M

governed by Φ and Ψ. We argue that this struc-tural backbone is not an arbitrary design choice but a logical necessity : any system satisfying the 

SEA definition must implement mechanisms corre-sponding to the following two functional hubs and their coupling. The dual-hub architecture described below therefore represents a structural reference de-rived from the SEA definition, rather than a novel algorithmic contribution in itself, as shown in Fig-ure 1. 

3.3.1 Execution Hub: The Digital Limb 

The Execution Hub serves as the agent’s sensory-motor interface with E, implementing the opera-tional requirements of digital embodiment through two functional components. 

Multimodal Perception. The hub continuously aligns heterogeneous environmental signals— including raw screen visuals, DOM structures, sys-tem logs, and terminal outputs—into structured state representations. This perceptual grounding is the prerequisite for system-native interaction: without the capacity to interpret unstructured en-vironmental feedback across modalities, the agent cannot operate beyond the confines of predefined API interfaces. 

Action Execution and Micro-feedback. The hub executes actions within A and manages the re-sulting environmental feedback through a localized validation ring. This micro-feedback mechanism triggers sub-second retries or execution bypasses at the action layer in response to environmental per-turbations, preventing low-level execution failures from propagating upward into the reasoning chain. Concurrently, when existing tools in A prove in-sufficient for the current environmental state, the hub synthesizes temporary interaction scripts, sub-mits them to a sandboxed validation environment, and upon successful verification, registers them as persistent tools—directly instantiating the expan-sion of A identified as a necessary consequence in Section 3.2. 

3.3.2 Cognition Hub: The Cognitive Core 

The Cognition Hub implements the Φ and Ψ func-tions that constitute the SEA’s evolutionary capac-ity. It operates across two temporal modes: real-time guidance of the Execution Hub during task execution, and offline distillation of accumulated experience into persistent memory updates after task completion. 

Real-time Execution Guidance. During task ex-ecution, the Cognition Hub provides continuous decision support to the Execution Hub by maintain-ing a structured representation of the current task state, retrieved context from M, and an actively updated execution plan. This real-time guidance integrates prior experience from Mp and Md into the current execution context, enabling the agent 5to draw on consolidated strategies rather than rea-soning from scratch at each decision step. When execution anomalies are detected, the Cognition Hub performs online self-reflection to revise the current plan, maintaining closed-loop execution stability under environmental perturbation. 

Experience Distillation. Following task comple-tion, the Cognition Hub instantiates Φ : τ → M 

by performing causal attribution on the raw interac-tion trajectory τ . It identifies the critical decision subsequence τ ∗ ⊂ τ that causally produced the ob-served outcome, then abstracts τ ∗ into structured updates across the three memory layers: successful execution paths are abstracted into parameterized strategies committed to Mp; failure attributions and diagnostic records are retained in Me; and inferred environmental constraints and user pref-erence signals are encoded in Md. This tripartite distillation ensures that Φ captures the full spec-trum of reusable knowledge derivable from a trajec-tory, rather than reducing experience to executable procedures alone. The selective cross-task trans-fer governed by Ψ is an inherent property of this distillation process: only knowledge assessed as sufficiently generalizable is propagated to Mt+1 ,preserving evolutionary stability while enabling continued growth. 

3.3.3 The Evolutionary Flywheel and Its Failure Modes 

The symbiotic coupling of the Execution Hub and Cognition Hub constitutes the Evolutionary Fly-wheel : a closed-loop mechanism through which raw environmental interaction is progressively transformed into persistent cognitive assets. The flywheel operates through three sequential phases: 1. Execution : The Execution Hub engages E,generating a raw interaction trajectory τ that captures the full sequence of perceptual inputs, actions, environmental responses, and error recovery events. 2. Distillation : The Cognition Hub applies Φ to 

τ , performing causal attribution to extract τ ∗

and committing structured knowledge updates to the appropriate layers of M via Ψ.3. Augmented Execution : In subsequent tasks, the Execution Hub retrieves relevant entries from M—particularly consolidated strategies from Mp—replacing exhaustive zero-shot reasoning with experience-guided execution. The resulting trajectory is of higher quality and lower cost than its predecessor, producing richer material for the next distillation cycle. These three phases form a self-reinforcing loop: each execution cycle produces experience that, once distilled, reduces the cost and increases the quality of future execution. The flywheel generates a net evolutionary gain when the reduction in ex-ecution cost achieved through experience-guided execution exceeds the overhead introduced by dis-tillation and retrieval. This condition is met when Φ

successfully abstracts generalizable strategies from instance-specific trajectories—transforming task-level successes into reusable cross-task knowledge. The flywheel can degrade into pseudo-evolution 

under two theoretically distinct failure modes. In 

distillation failure , Φ cannot perform effective causal attribution—due to trajectory noise or in-sufficient abstraction capacity—and the resulting memory update fails to generalize beyond the spe-cific instance, leaving Mp inert despite apparent task success. In retrieval failure , the Execution Hub cannot identify structural similarity between a new task and existing entries in M—particularly under environmental drift—and defaults to exhaus-tive zero-shot reasoning, bypassing the flywheel entirely. Critically, both failure modes produce an identical behavioral signature: execution cost fails to decrease across sequential task variants and scales linearly with task frequency rather than con-verging. 

## 4 SEA-Eval: Evaluation Framework and Benchmark Design 

4.1 Evaluation Principles 

The theoretical analysis in Section 3 established that genuine evolution in SEAs produces a dis-tinguishable behavioral signature: as the Evolu-tionary Flywheel operates above the distillation-yield threshold, execution cost converges mono-tonically across sequential task variants while task success is maintained. Conversely, pseudo-evolution—arising from distillation or retrieval failure—produces no such convergence, with ex-ecution cost scaling linearly with task frequency regardless of accumulated experience. These pre-dictions jointly motivate an evaluation design that must simultaneously track task success and exe-cution overhead across temporally connected task sequences, rather than assessing isolated episodes. two complementary dimensions, as shown in Fig-ure 2. 

4.1.1 Intra-task Execution Reliability 

This dimension directly operationalizes the first foundational characteristic of the SEA paradigm: complex interaction in open-ended environments. It evaluates the agent’s capacity to navigate high-complexity, non-stationary tasks without relying on prior accumulated experience, assessing immediate execution competence under environmental uncer-tainty independent of long-term memory evolution. 

Complex Scenario Adaptation. The agent must maintain coherent state representations across multi-step decision chains and long-horizon rea-soning sequences, sustaining task progress despite the dynamic and unpredictable nature of the de-ployment environment. 

Error Recovery and Robustness. When encoun-tering execution failures or unexpected environ-mental perturbations, the agent must sustain closed-loop execution through real-time self-reflection and self-correction, avoiding logical deadlocks or cas-cading system failures. 

4.1.2 Long-term Evolutionary Performance 

This dimension directly operationalizes the second foundational characteristic of the SEA paradigm: continuous, multi-turn evolution. It assesses whether the agent’s performance systematically improves over successive task deployments and whether these improvements remain stable across task boundaries. 

Evolutionary Growth. The agent should exhibit measurable performance gains correlated with task frequency N , manifested as monotonically improv-ing Success Rates and continuously decreasing ex-ecution overhead. This growth constitutes the em-pirical signature of a functioning Evolutionary Fly-wheel. 

Evolutionary Stability. While acquiring new ca-pabilities or transitioning across task domains, the agent must preserve the integrity of previously con-solidated knowledge in Mp. A robust SEA must suppress catastrophic forgetting, ensuring that the expansion of M does not introduce negative inter-ference into existing procedural knowledge. 

Implicit Alignment Convergence. Over ex-tended deployment with a consistent user, the agent should exhibit a monotonically decreasing user intervention rate, reflecting the progressive inter-nalization of user-specific preferences into Md.This convergence operationalizes the implicit user alignment property identified as a necessary conse-quence of continuous multi-turn evolution in Sec-tion 3. 74.2 Metric Design 

The two evaluation dimensions defined above jointly motivate the selection of Success Rate (SR) and Token Consumption (T) as the primary evalua-tive primitives of SEA-Eval. SR directly measures the task success requirement of intra-task reliability, while T serves as the primary signal for detecting the convergence predicted by the flywheel theory: when Φ successfully abstracts generalizable strate-gies from prior trajectories, subsequent executions bypass exhaustive reasoning chains, manifesting directly as a reduction in T. All remaining metrics in SEA-Eval are auxiliary, providing diagnostic granularity for interpreting the patterns observed in SR and T. 

4.2.1 Primary Metrics Success Rate (SR). SR quantifies the accuracy of goal achievement, serving as the baseline mea-sure of task execution capability and the minimum reliability guarantee for deployment viability. 

Token Consumption (T). T measures the com-putational overhead of task execution and serves as the primary signal for detecting the transition from exhaustive zero-shot reasoning to experience-guided execution. This selection is theoretically motivated: the causal relationship between distil-lation quality and token cost is more direct than execution step count S, which may remain sta-ble even as reasoning depth increases within each step, and more tractable than wall-clock latency, which introduces hardware-dependent variance that compromises cross-environment reproducibility. T therefore serves as the most faithful proxy for the Evolutionary Flywheel’s operational state among available efficiency primitives. 

4.2.2 Intra-task Auxiliary Metrics 

Within a single task execution cycle, the following metrics provide diagnostic granularity beyond the primary SR and T measurements. 

Execution Steps (S). S records the number of decision-making steps required to complete a task, providing a coarse-grained indicator of action plan-ning efficiency that complements T in characteriz-ing intra-task execution paths. 

Self-Correction Frequency ( Nfix ). Nfix records the frequency of autonomous error identification and recovery within a single task, reflecting the agent’s real-time resilience under environmental perturbation and complementing SR as a measure of execution robustness. 

User Intervention Rate ( Nundo ). Nundo records the frequency of manual corrections, command roll-backs, or user-initiated interventions during task execution, quantifying the immediate friction be-tween the agent’s execution logic and user intent. While this metric captures instantaneous alignment deviation at the intra-task level, its longitudinal be-havior across task sequences forms the basis for the alignment convergence metric defined in Sec-tion 4.2.3.

4.3 Dataset Design 

Following a metric-driven design philosophy, the SEA-Eval dataset is constructed to generate the task sequences necessary for computing the evo-lutionary metrics defined in Section 4.2. The con-struction proceeds in two phases: the design of high-complexity atomic tasks that instantiate the intra-task reliability requirements, and their sys-tematic organization into sequential streams that enable longitudinal evolutionary analysis. 

4.3.1 Atomic Task Construction 

Atomic tasks are designed to simulate realistic open-ended deployment environments, directly in-stantiating the complex interaction conditions re-quired by the first foundational characteristic of the SEA paradigm. 

Heterogeneous Capability Coverage. Tasks span two primary interaction ecosystems to ensure balanced coverage across digital embodiment sce-narios: Web Automation (16 tasks), encompass-ing multi-step navigation, complex form interac-tion, dynamic DOM parsing, deep data scraping, and pagination traversal; and Local System Opera-tions (14 tasks), covering file I/O, script execution, environment configuration, API invocation, and PDF/OCR processing. All tasks are designed by human experts using publicly available materials to ensure baseline solvability and ground-truth ver-ifiability. 

Execution Complexity Filtering. To ensure that atomic tasks possess sufficient depth for evolution-ary sensitivity analysis, tasks are filtered according to logical execution sequence length as measured by baseline agent trajectories. Tasks are designed to span a range of sequence lengths, ensuring that the benchmark captures evolutionary dynamics across varying levels of task complexity rather than col-lapsing to a single difficulty regime. 

Skill Bloat Simulation. Environmental pertur-bations are introduced and the agent’s memory is pre-loaded with redundant or irrelevant tools, eval-uating retrieval precision under conditions of mem-ory congestion. This directly stresses the retrieval component of the Evolutionary Flywheel, testing whether retrieval failure is induced when M con-tains high-noise content alongside genuinely appli-cable knowledge. 

4.3.2 Sequential Stream Construction 

Atomic tasks are organized into longitudinal streams designed to generate the temporal se-quences required for computing Keff , KSR , and 

Kalign . Two complementary sequence structures are employed, each corresponding to a distinct eval-uation target established in Section 3. 

Correlated Task Sequences. Correlated se-quences instantiate the primary scenario for ob-serving evolutionary growth. By presenting struc-turally similar or semantically equivalent task vari-ants in succession, they create the conditions under which Φ can produce generalizable updates to Mp

and the Evolutionary Flywheel can operate above the distillation-yield threshold. For each of the 30 atomic tasks, three semantically equivalent variants are generated by prompting an LLM to rewrite the task while preserving the original intent, atomic ca-pabilities, and execution environment. All variants are manually reviewed by domain experts, yielding 90 additional tasks that, together with the original 30, comprise a streaming task pool of 120 tasks. These correlated sequences constitute the primary data source for computing Keff and KSR .

Orthogonal Task Sequences. Orthogonal se-quences serve as the stress test for evolutionary sta-bility. By interleaving structurally unrelated tasks, they expose the agent to the risk of catastrophic forgetting—the degradation of previously consoli-dated entries in Mp due to interference from new task trajectories. A robust SEA must maintain sta-ble SR on established task types while concurrently acquiring new capabilities, directly testing whether 

Ψ successfully suppresses negative transfer across 9task boundaries. 

Dynamic Step-Change Design. To evaluate self-healing capacity and cross-domain knowledge transfer under environmental discontinuity, abrupt environmental changes—such as underlying library updates or task type switches—are manually in-troduced into task flows. For each atomic task, an LLM agent first analyzes its environmental de-pendencies and subsequently injects a conflicting change that invalidates the original execution path. After expert verification, 45 environmental step-changes are incorporated into the benchmark, dis-tributed across the atomic task set to simulate real-istic deployment drift. 

Human Feedback Simulation. To generate data for Nundo and Kalign measurement, tasks are equipped with interactive feedback nodes. Fuzzy intentions or specific preference constraints are implanted into task specifications, and a simula-tor records agent actions during execution. When the agent deviates from preset user preferences, a simulated Undo action is triggered, providing raw interaction data for alignment convergence anal-ysis. This yields 30 preference-constrained task instances, one per original atomic task. 

4.3.3 Dataset Statistics 

The complete SEA-Eval dataset comprises: 30 core atomic tasks across web automation (16) and lo-cal system operations (14); 90 streaming tasks (30 original + 60 variants) organized into correlated sequences for evolutionary growth measurement; 45 environmental step-changes distributed across the atomic task set for dynamic adaptation assess-ment; and 30 preference-constrained instances for alignment convergence measurement. This staged construction ensures that SEA-Eval provides both the task diversity required for intra-task reliabil-ity assessment and the temporal depth required for longitudinal evolutionary analysis, jointly opera-tionalizing the evaluation framework defined in Sections 4.1 and 4.2.

5.1 Experimental Setup 

To rigorously evaluate the evolutionary capabilities of the agents across our proposed SEA-Eval bench-mark, we establish a controlled and reproducible experimental environment. The setup is configured as follows: • Agent Harnesses: We deploy two represen-tative agent frameworks to instantiate the system-native interactions: OpenClaw and 

GenericAgent . 5.3 Evolutionary Analysis 

A fundamental premise of the SEA-Eval frame-work is that a truly self-evolving agent should not perpetually rely on the robust zero-shot reason-ing capabilities of the underlying LLMs to brute-force its way through tasks. To validate this, we conduct a evolutionary evaluation across 12 high-complexity task groups, comparing the evolution-ary trajectories of OpenClaw (OC) and GenericA-gent (GA). The results shown in Figure 3 reveal a stark contrast in experience distillation capabilities. 

OpenClaw exhibits classic symptoms of pseudo-evolution. Across the majority of the task streams, OpenClaw demonstrates exorbitant baseline token consumption and fails to achieve a stable conver-gence rate ( Kconv ). For instance, in the complex web navigation and data integration task (Task 1), OpenClaw consumes a massive 4,615k tokens on the initial run. While it shows a transient decrease in the first variant (Task 1-B: 868.7k tokens), it immediately regresses when faced with the subse-quent variant (Task 1-C: 1,524.9k tokens). This erratic trajectory indicates that OpenClaw suffers from "episodic amnesia." Rather than extracting generalizable strategies from previous successes, it treats every variant as a novel problem, rely-ing entirely on the LLM’s zero-shot capacity to navigate the state space from scratch. This brute-force approach not only leads to unsustainably high "metabolic energy" costs (e.g., consuming up to 5,950k tokens in Task 11) but also results in sys-tematic fragility, as evidenced by its complete fail-ure (null output) in later variants of prolonged se-quences like Task 5-C and Task 9-B. Conversely, GenericAgent successfully demon-strates the characteristics of a self-evolving system. GenericAgent consistently initiates tasks with a sig-nificantly lower token overhead and exhibits a pro-nounced, monotonic negative slope across sequen-tial variants. A prime example of this logic collapse is observed in the academic database retrieval task (Task 5). GenericAgent initiates the task sequence utilizing 520k tokens. However, by abstracting the successful retrieval and filtering logic into a persis-tent SOP, it dramatically reduces its consumption in the subsequent variants to 253.8k (Task 5-B) and further to 117.4k (Task 5-C). A similar pat-tern of structural optimization is observed in the gaming metadata aggregation sequence (Task 8), where GenericAgent’s token consumption decays from 170k → 91k → 33.2k. Furthermore, Gener-icAgent demonstrates robust self-correction mecha-nisms devoid of brute-force escalation. In the travel planning task (Task 2), GenericAgent initially ex-periences an execution error (hallucination) costing 961.45k tokens. However, the system utilizes this failure as a negative reward signal, restructuring its execution path to successfully and efficiently com-plete the subsequent variants (270.6k and 231.0k tokens, respectively). The empirical data explicitly validates that re-lying on scale-driven, zero-shot reasoning is in-sufficient for continuous deployment. Systems trapped in pseudo-evolution scale linearly in com-putational cost relative to task frequency. In con-trast, architectures equipped with effective experi-ence engines transform high-entropy exploration into low-entropy skills, verifying our hypothesis that the convergence of token cost is the primary indicator of authentic machine evolution. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper presents SEA-Eval, a systematic eval-uation framework for Self-Evolving Agents that integrates intra-task execution reliability and long-11 Figure 3: The results of different agent frameworks (with Claude Opus 4.6 ) when executing similar tasks. 

term evolutionary performance. We contribute a formal definition of the SEA paradigm grounded in digital embodiment and continuous cross-task evolution, and establish the Evolutionary Flywheel as its structural necessity. Building on this theo-retical foundation, SEA-Eval moves beyond per-formance snapshots to measure evolutionary tra-jectories directly, supported by a multidimensional metric system centered on Success Rate and To-ken Consumption, and a standardized dataset or-ganized into sequential task streams for observing agent behavior in complex, non-stationary envi-ronments. Empirical results reveal a fundamental gap between apparent capability and genuine evolu-tion: frameworks achieving identical success rates exhibit up to 31.2× divergence in token consump-tion, and only architectures equipped with effective experience distillation demonstrate the monotonic convergence that distinguishes true evolution from pseudo-evolution. These findings suggest that self-evolving agents, as a form of continuously adap-tive digital entities, represent a meaningful step toward more capable and efficient autonomous sys-tems. Challenges remain in areas such as fully autonomous bootstrapping, subjective preference alignment, and systemic governance, and the eval-uation principles established in this work provide a concrete foundation for addressing them in future research. 12 7 Discussion and Future Work 

SEA-Eval establishes a rigorous foundation for evaluating the evolutionary dynamics of single-agent systems across intra-task reliability and long-term performance dimensions. However, the SEA paradigm as formalized in Section 3 opens several directions that extend beyond the current bench-mark’s scope. We organize these directions across three frontiers: the capability frontier, concerning what SEAs can do and how efficiently they do it; the evaluation frontier, concerning what aspects of SEA behavior remain difficult to measure; and the deployment frontier, concerning how SEAs can be safely and broadly instantiated in the real world. 

7.1 Expanding the Capability Frontier 7.1.1 Toward Fully Autonomous Bootstrapping 

Current SEA deployments rely on human-provided task specifications to initialize the first execution cycle, from which the Evolutionary Flywheel be-gins distillation. A fully autonomous SEA must eventually initiate its own evolutionary cycle with-out human scaffolding—identifying performance bottlenecks, generating synthetic experiences, and triggering self-iteration in the absence of explicit external prompts. Solving this cold-start problem is a prerequisite for SEAs to achieve genuine gen-eralization in unknown domains, and represents a capability dimension not currently assessed by SEA-Eval. 

7.1.2 Balancing Reasoning Depth and Execution Efficiency 

The current SEA architecture relies on large lan-guage models as the cognitive core of the Cog-nition Hub, whose inference process introduces non-negligible latency overhead. For tasks re-quiring immediate feedback—such as real-time interaction, high-frequency operations, or latency-sensitive workflows—the output efficiency of cur-rent models falls short of practical deployment demands. This gap is particularly pronounced in industrial-grade scenarios, where model inference latency and task execution requirements diverge significantly, limiting the applicability of SEAs in such contexts. One promising direction is het-erogeneous multi-model orchestration: delegating latency-sensitive, high-frequency execution tasks to lightweight models while reserving large mod-els for offline experience distillation and strategic planning. How to appropriately delineate the re-sponsibility boundary between these two classes of models, and how to introduce latency-aware eval-uation dimensions into the SEA-Eval framework, remain open problems for future work. 

7.1.3 Toward Collective Evolution: Multi-agent Co-evolution 

The SEA formalization characterizes the evolution-ary dynamics of a single agent operating within a private memory state M. However, the bounded capacity of a single agent’s M imposes an upper limit on achievable evolutionary gains, and expand-ing productivity beyond this ceiling naturally mo-tivates multi-agent coordination. When multiple SEAs operate within shared or overlapping envi-ronments, new questions emerge: how distilled knowledge in Mp should be selectively shared across agents without introducing noise from agent-specific execution contexts; how coupled Evolu-tionary Flywheels interact to produce system-level evolutionary gains that may exceed the sum of in-dividual contributions; and how functional special-ization emerges across agents over extended co-deployment. These questions require both theoreti-cal extensions to the SEA formalization and new system-level evaluation primitives beyond the indi-vidual Keff and KSR metrics defined in SEA-Eval. 

7.2 Expanding the Evaluation Frontier 7.2.1 Subjective Preference and Deep Alignment 

SEA-Eval operationalizes user alignment through 

Kalign , which tracks the longitudinal reduction in user intervention rate as a behavioral proxy for preference internalization. However, this metric captures only the observable behavioral layer of alignment. A large class of alignment phenomena— such as the aesthetic quality of a generated presen-tation or the stylistic appropriateness of produced content—involve subjective, individualized judg-ments whose ground truth cannot be formalized as intervention frequencies or task success labels. Fu-ture research should explore evaluation paradigms that move beyond behavioral proxies toward richer preference elicitation methods, capturing the con-vergence between an agent’s evolved strategies and a user’s latent aesthetic and functional intent. 13 7.3 Expanding the Deployment Frontier 7.3.1 Safety and Systemic Governance 

As SEAs autonomously expand A and update M,safety and governance become non-negotiable de-ployment requirements. Two structural insights frame this challenge. First, capability-permission parity: broader system permissions enhance evolu-tionary potential but proportionally scale the risk profile of autonomous self-modification, and no principled framework currently exists for calibrat-ing permission scope to evolutionary stage. Sec-ond, safety as system engineering: agent-level safeguards are inherently vulnerable to the same distillation failures that produce pseudo-evolution, making it necessary to enforce evolutionary bound-aries through underlying permission management frameworks and sandboxed execution environ-ments rather than relying on agent-level constraints alone. Future research should investigate how the Evolutionary Flywheel can be integrated into gov-erned scaffolding architectures that keep evolved assets in M auditable and controllable within mission-critical workflows. 

7.3.2 From Digital Embodiment to Embodied AI 

The SEA architecture is built around a modality-agnostic closed loop: the Execution Hub perceives environmental states, acts upon them, and returns feedback; the Cognition Hub distills the resulting trajectory into persistent memory updates via Φ and 

Ψ. This loop is instantiated in digital environments through screen pixels and system logs feeding into keyboard and mouse actions, but its structural logic is isomorphic to the perception-action-feedback cy-cles of physical robotic systems. Since Φ : τ → M 

and Ψ : Mt → M t+1 are modality-independent by definition, the transition from digital to physi-cal embodiment is primarily a challenge of modal-ity adaptation rather than architectural reinvention. Digital environments therefore offer a high-fidelity, low-cost testbed for developing the evolutionary mechanisms that physical robotic systems will ulti-mately require. 

## 8 Ethics Statement 

Data Source and Privacy All tasks in SEA-Eval are constructed by human experts based on pub-licly available web resources and local system op-eration scenarios. No personal privacy data or user-sensitive information is involved. All web automa-tion tasks target publicly accessible websites, and all local system operation tasks are executed within isolated sandbox environments, ensuring that no real user data is collected or stored during evalu-ation. The benchmark dataset and code will be publicly released upon publication to support re-producibility. 

Experimental Safety Since SEA operates with autonomous system-level capabilities including code execution, file manipulation, and environment configuration, all experiments in this paper are con-ducted strictly within containerized sandbox en-vironments. This ensures that agent operations produce no unintended effects on host systems or external environments.

All research design, experimental implementation, data analy-sis, and core arguments were completed indepen-dently by the authors. The use of these AI tools was limited to improving the fluency and academic conventions of English expression and the visual presentation of diagrams, and did not involve sub-stantive contributions to the research content.
