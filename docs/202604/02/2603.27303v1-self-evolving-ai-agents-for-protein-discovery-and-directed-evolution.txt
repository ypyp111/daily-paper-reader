Abstract 

Protein scientific discovery is bottlenecked by the manual orchestration of infor-mation and algorithms, while general agents are insufficient in complex domain projects. VenusFactory2 provides an autonomous framework that shifts from static tool usage to dynamic workflow synthesis via a self-evolving multi-agent infrastructure to address protein-related demands. It outperforms a set of well-known agents on the VenusAgentEval benchmark, and autonomously organizes the discovery and optimization of proteins from a single natural language prompt. 

Scientific discovery is propelled by the synergistic co-evolution of tools and intelli-gence [1], while protein serves as the primary substrate for this technological evolution within life sciences [2]. In this context, the integration of deep learning into pro-tein science has yielded substantial improvements in specific tasks, such as structure 1

While these models have achieved unprecedented accuracy, they are typically deployed as isolated, static command-line interfaces with incompatible data formats [9]. This dis-connection between conceptual biological intent and low-level programmatic execution forces researchers to dedicate disproportionate cognitive resources to low-level pipeline orchestration and software dependency resolution, thereby decoupling algorithmic intelligence from high-level biological intuition. Recent efforts such as Jupyter notebooks [9, 10] or graphical user interfaces [4, 11] have simplified the execution of individual tools; however, they primarily simplify the execution of individual tools rather than resolving the fundamental chal-lenge of knowledge-driven orchestration (Fig. 1a). Protein engineering campaigns are rarely isolated algorithmic tasks; rather, they necessitate the seamless synthesis of 2domain knowledge —mined from vast literature —with heterogeneous computational predictions (e.g., using AlphaFold prediction for ESM designed mutations) [12]. In existing frameworks, bridging the gap between conceptual biological reasoning and technical execution demands extensive manual intervention [13, 14]. Researchers are burdened not only with data wrangling but also with the cognitive load of contextually aligning algorithmic outputs with biological hypotheses. Consequently, scalability is constrained not by computational power, but by the temporal overhead and cognitive burden of orchestrating these disjointed workflows [15, 16]. To address these limitations, we introduce VenusFactory2, an autonomous frame-work that elevates the interaction paradigm from rigid tool execution to collaborative scientific reasoning specifically tailored for protein engineering and discovery. Venus-Factory2 mimics a digital research laboratory (Fig. 1b) populated by specialized agents acting as Principal Investigator , Machine Learning Specialist , Computational Biolo-gist , and Scientific Critic [12]. By simulating the collaboration in real human research teams, the system does not merely execute pipelines but proactively translates abstract biological objectives into rigorous experimental plans. Crucially, VenusFac-tory2 achieves self-evolving capability expansion: it detects methodological gaps and autonomously synthesizes —then deploys —executable computational tools to resolve unforeseen challenges, enabling open-ended discovery without manual intervention. As illustrated in Figure 1c, we simplify the research workflow of VenusFactory2 into a four-phase protocol orchestrated by role-specific agents. (1) Objective : The pro-cess initiates with the human researcher specifying high-level biological objectives and optional dataset constraints. (2) Research : The Principal Investigator agent con-ducts a feasibility analysis by cross-referencing external literature with internal tool capabilities, engaging in iterative dialogue to deeply research the experimental design. (3) Implementation : This phase is synergized by the Machine Learning Specialist 

and Computational Biologist . The former focuses on code review, execution, and new computational tools development, while the latter selects appropriate utilities to check and assemble the analysis pipeline. (4) Summary : Finally, the Scientific Critic agent aggregates outputs, execution logs, and reasoning traces to audit biological validity and generate the final report. To systematically assess procedural planning and reasoning capabilities within the domains of protein discovery and directed evolution, we established VenusAgentEval (Fig. 1d), an expert-reviewed benchmark comprising 148 validated instances stratified into Question-, Task-, and Project-level complexities. Formally, each evaluation task is defined as a tuple T = ( Q, P, C), where the query Q is paired with a multi-modal prompt context P —encompassing amino acid sequences, local file paths, and database identifiers —and a set of ground-truth constraints C for objective scoring. Comparative evaluation on the stratified VenusAgentEval benchmark reveals dis-tinct failure modes in existing paradigms (Fig. 1d). VenusFactory2 demonstrates superior robustness across all complexity tiers, achieving a weighted Project-level score of 78.8%. In sharp contrast, generalist models such as DeepSeek-V3.1, while compet-itive in atomic reasoning tasks (Task-level: 67.2%), suffer a catastrophic performance collapse in long-horizon workflows (plummeting to 18.2% at Project-level). The workflow is anchored by the Research module, which implements retrieval-augmented generation across major academic and biological databases [18, 19] to ground experimental hypotheses. For candidate identification, the Protein Discovery quadrant orchestrates structural or embedding alignment algorithms with biophysical filters [11, 20], while the Directed Evolution quadrant employs generative mutation models to optimize functional prop-erties under epistatic constraints [8, 21]. Complementing these pre-defined capabilities, the Auto-ML Infrastructure serves as an adaptive engine [22], enabling the runtime encapsulation of user-specific datasets into deployable tools when standard methods are insufficient. The operational logic of VenusFactory2 initiates with the Research module (Fig. 2b), where the Principal Investigator agent translates high-level biological objec-tives into structured execution protocols. Upon receiving project constraints, the agent decomposes the objective into specific retrieval sub-tasks, executing a hierar-chical search strategy across web content, academic literature, and patent databases to ground the scientific hypothesis. To guarantee experimental feasibility, the module implements a constraint verification loop: the agent engages in structured dialogue to resolve ambiguities and performs a resource feasibility analysis by cross-referencing retrieved methodologies against the system’s internal tool library. This process cul-minates in a formalized research plan —comprising current status, methodological workflow, and tool invocation sequences —that serves as a deterministic blueprint to minimize hallucinatory deviations during downstream execution. The framework overcomes the limitations of static libraries by enabling open-ended adaptability through autonomous tool synthesis (Fig. 2c). When the Research module identifies a missing function, exemplified by a requirement for allergenicity prediction, it aggregates training data from external repositories, user uploads, or conversation history. The agents then synthesize reproducible scripts to fine-tune foundation models using parameter-efficient methods applied to architectural templates (Methods). This workflow culminates in the automatic encapsulation of the new model as a persistent and shareable Model Context Protocol utility. Consequently, the system dynamically expands its operational scope to address specific and evolving research requirements. To validate the utility for protein discovery, we deployed VenusFactory2 on a tar-geted polyethylene terephthalate hydrolase retrieval campaign (Fig. 2d). Leveraging the VenusMine [20], the system maps natural language functional descriptors including thermostable PETase directly to protein sequence space via high-dimensional embed-ding retrieval. In cases where template sequences are absent, the Research module initiates a literature-based search to establish a seed profile. The subsequent workflow executes a hierarchical filtration protocol wherein candidates are mined from metage-nomic databases and subjected to multiparametric profiling by the Computational Biologist agent. This agent dynamically invokes predictive assays to screen for physic-ochemical constraints including melting temperature ( Tm) and pH stability. In a blind validation benchmark, VenusFactory2 successfully recapitulated the identification of KbPETase from a vast space of unannotated candidates. This confirms the system’s 5ability to execute precision retrieval by synthesizing semantic intent with biophysical property filtering. For complex optimization tasks, we evaluated VenusFactory2 in a directed evolu-tion scenario targeting a VHH antibody (Fig. 2e). The objective involved enhancing binding affinity through combinatorial mutagenesis. The system orchestrated a multi-stage pipeline, integrating AlphaFold2 [3] for structural resolution and VenusREM [8] for zero-shot mutational scanning. Crucially, the system demonstrated adaptive reasoning to bridge a methodological gap: while the initial tool output was limited to single-point fitness scores, the project required multi-site predictions. Identifying this discrepancy, the Machine Learning Specialist agent autonomously synthesized and executed a Ridge Regression [23] algorithm, utilizing single-point priors to model combinatorial epistatic effects. The resulting top-ranked variants, including double and quadruple mutants, exhibited high correlation with ground-truth wet-lab data. This case validates the system’s ability to expand beyond static tool definitions by dynamically constructing computational logic for specific biological objectives. Collectively, VenusFactory2 advances the transition from static tool execution to intent-centric protein engineering. By resolving the cognitive complexity of workflow orchestration and enabling runtime capability expansion, the framework empowers experimentalists to deploy sophisticated computational strategies without requiring specialized programming expertise. This work establishes a foundation for self-evolving scientific agents capable of driving autonomous and open-ended discovery in biomedicine.

## Research Tool Ecosystem 

Federated Web Intelligence. To synthesize multi-modal scientific information, the agent employs a federated search interface that interrogates four distinct infor-mation domains. (1) Academic Literature: We integrate programmatic access to 

PubMed , Web of Science , and Semantic Scholar to retrieve peer-reviewed articles, complemented by arXiv and bioRxiv for preprints, ensuring coverage of the latest algorithmic advances. (2) Technical Repositories: To bridge theoretical concepts with implementation, the system queries GitHub for codebases, and Hugging Face 

and Kaggle for benchmarks and datasets. (3) Intellectual Property: Industrial relevance is assessed by mining the Google Patents Public Datasets via Google BigQuery , allowing the agent to verify novelty and freedom-to-operate. (4) Open-Web Context: For unstructured content (e.g., industry news, documentation), the system leverages the Tavily and DuckDuckGo Search API, optimized for LLM-centric retrieval, to capture high-precision contextual intelligence. 

Database Interrogation Engine. To ensure comprehensive coverage of the protein universe, VenusFactory2 integrates a high-throughput data collection engine spanning nine canonical repositories across sequence, structure, enzymology, and functional networks. (1) Sequence and Function: UniProt [19] serves as the cen-tral repository for amino acid sequences and curated annotations ( > 250 million entries). This is supplemented by NCBI [18] databases (GenBank, RefSeq) to capture non-canonical entries. Additionally, InterPro [24] is leveraged to assign functional signatures, classifying proteins into families and domains ( ∼ 41 , 000 entries). (2) 

Structural Data: RCSB PDB [25] provides over 200 , 000 experimentally determined 3D structures. For targets lacking experimental data, the system retrieves predicted structures from the AlphaFold Database [26] via UniProt ID mapping. (3) Bio-chemical and Interaction Networks: To contextualize proteins within complex biological systems, the engine queries BRENDA [27] for comprehensive enzyme kinetics and functional parameters. Protein-ligand binding affinities and small-molecule bioac-tivity are retrieved via ChEMBL [28]. Furthermore, KEGG [29] is integrated to map targets onto higher-order metabolic and signaling pathways, while the STRING database [30] provides probabilistic profiling of protein-protein interactions. Technically, the module implements an asynchronous, multithreaded architecture to maximize data acquisition throughput (see Supplementary Table A1 ). 

## Directed Evolution Tools 

Mutational Scanning. For directed evolution prediction, we deployed a compre-hensive suite of protein language models (PLMs), prioritizing architectures validated through wet-lab experiments [31, 32] or superior benchmark performance [33, 34]. To capture sequence-based evolutionary patterns, the system integrates the evolu-tionary scale modeling suite, including ESM2 [35], ESM1v [36], and ESM1b [37]. For structure-conditioned predictions, we deployed hybrid architectures to leverage 9geometric constraints: VenusREM [8] integrates homologous sequences with struc-tural information. ProSST [38] utilizes discrete attention mechanisms. ProtSSN [39], a sequence-structure serial network. SaProt [21] employs Foldseek-derived [11] token encoding. The inverse folding model ESM-IF [40] and MIF-ST [41], combining graph neural networks. Furthermore, for “Low-N” scenarios involving iterative wet-lab feed-back, we implemented a sample-efficient Ridge Regression protocol [23] which is implemented by VenusFactory2 itself. This supervised module augments one-hot amino acid encoding with probability density features derived from unsupervised PLMs, demonstrating superior generalization in data-scarce regimes. 

Functional Sites Detection. To constrain the mutational search space and pre-vent the disruption of critical biological functions, we developed the VenusX [42] module, an ensemble of residue-level predictors designed to identify and mask essen-tial sites. Built upon PLMs, these models were trained on datasets partitioned at a strict 90 % sequence identity threshold to minimize data leakage. The suite comprises specialized predictors for catalytic active sites, ligand-binding interfaces, evolutionary conserved residues, functional motifs, and domain boundaries. Ground-truth labels were derived from curated InterPro entries [24], supporting binary classification of functionally relevant residues. (see Supplementary Table A2 )

## Protein Discovery Module 

Homology Mining Engine. To enable comprehensive protein discovery, we curated and consolidated four high-quality protein sequence datasets derived from the Global Ocean Protein Catalog (GOPC) [43], UniProt UniRef100 [19], the MGnify metage-nomic database [44], and the Open MetaGenomic (OMG) corpus [45], all deduplicated at 100 % sequence identity to remove redundancy. To navigate these vast sequence spaces, the system integrates a multi-modal retrieval architecture. Complement-ing Foldseek [11] structural alignment and sequence homology tools (BLAST [46] and MMseqs2 [47]), we deployed VenusMine [20], an advanced embedding-based retrieval engine. By synergizing sequence and structural representations through multi-round iterative searches, VenusMine facilitates the identification of remote homologs beyond the reach of local alignment, a capability validated by the discovery of high-performance PET hydrolases. 

Biophysical Filtration Suite. To facilitate the rigorous down-selection of protein candidates, we established a comprehensive filtration suite within the Venus-Factory2 platform, synergizing deep learning with classical biophysics. ## Auto Machine Learning 

Template-based Model Adaptation. To adapt foundation PLMs for specialized downstream tasks, VenusFactory2 implements a rigorous optimization framework that bridges general-purpose representations with specific scientific objectives. This infrastructure enables a continuum of tasks —from continuous property regression to discrete classification —governed by strict configuration schemas. By encapsulating model specifications within static .config definitions (Appendix Table B6), the sys-tem constrains the generative action space, thereby ensuring the syntactic validity and executability of autonomously synthesized code. To balance computational efficiency with feature alignment, the agent supports a spectrum of training strategies, ranging from full-parameter updates to parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) [60]. The PEFT infrastructure is integrated into Venus-Factory2 to support the LoRA family, optimizing resource consumption without compromising expressivity. Specifically, the system supports: (1) standard LoRA [61] and weight-decomposed LoRA (DoRA) [62] for maximizing learning capacity; (2) adaptive budgeting via AdaLoRA [63] for sparsity control; and (3) quantization-aware QLoRA [64] and IA3 [65] for deployment in resource-constrained environments. For structure-dependent tasks, the agent additionally incorporates the SES-Adapter [54], which employs cross-attention to explicitly fuse sequence representations with structural embeddings derived from Foldseek [11] or DSSP [66]. For the projection phase, the architecture bifurcates its processing logic based on the granularity of the biological objective. ## Supported Trainable PLMs 

To ensure broad adaptability across diverse biological tasks and computational environments, VenusFactory2 integrates a heterogeneous repository of pre-trained foundation models (see Supplementary Table B5 ). This collection encompasses architectures ranging from lightweight encoders (8M parameters) to large-scale gen-erative models (15B parameters), enabling deployment on hardware ranging from consumer GPUs to cluster nodes. For general-purpose sequence representation, the platform supports the full evolutionary scale modeling (ESM) family [35–37] and BERT-style architectures [67, 68], alongside Encoder-Decoder frameworks such as ProtT5 [67] and Ankh series [69, 70]. To address domain-specific engineering cam-paigns, the library includes antibody-centric models like IgBert and IgT5 [71]. Crucially, to transcend the limitations of 1D sequence modeling, the system incor-porates structure-informed architectures —including SaProt [21], ProSST [38], and ProtSSN [39] —which fuse geometric priors into the embedding space via structure-aware vocabularies or disentangled attention mechanisms, facilitating the optimization of folding stability and epistatic interactions.

## Agentic Built-in Tools Explore 

To evaluate the autonomous exploration capabilities of our framework, we analyzed its architectural and hyperparameter search process across diverse downstream protein property prediction tasks. Normalizing the primary evaluation metrics (Appendix Figure E1) reveals substantial variance in performance distributions across datasets. This highlights that model efficacy depends heavily on specific combinations of PLMs and fine-tuning strategies. Despite these significant performance variations, the agent consistently identified the optimal configuration. To analyze the temporal dynamics of this optimization, we tracked the agent’s search trajectories on a representative subset of datasets (DLKcat, DeepLoc2Multi, TAPE_Fluorescence, and DeepSoluE) that encompass a broad spectrum of biological properties and task complexities. As depicted in Appendix Figure E2, the agent exhibits a directed and sample-efficient convergence. It progressively refines its configurations and systematically outperforms static baselines derived from standard human-selected heuristics within a constrained trial budget. This optimization efficiency is directly related to the agent’s learned exploration preferences (Appendix Figure E3). Rather than sampling the configuration space uniformly, the agent disproportionately allocated its trial budget toward specific PLM architectures, notably the esm2_t30_150M and ankh-base models, while pre-dominantly utilizing the parameter-efficient ses-adapter tuning method. This skewed empirical distribution indicates an implicit cost-aware optimization strategy. By autonomously prioritizing parameter-efficient fine-tuning over full-model freezing and favoring medium-sized foundation models over their larger counterparts, the agent effectively balances computational overhead with predictive accuracy. It directs its search budget toward configurations with the highest empirical yield, demonstrating a systematic approach to resource allocation in protein representation learning. 

## Construction of VenusAgentEval 

Benchmark Rationale and Positioning. To systematically evaluate the special-ized capabilities of VenusFactory2 in protein engineering, we established VenusAgen-tEval. Distinct from general-domain reasoning benchmarks [73, 74] and broad scientific tool-use frameworks [16], VenusAgentEval stands as the first vertical agen-tic benchmark dedicated to protein science, specifically targeting the sub-domains of protein discovery and directed evolution. 13 Hierarchical Construction Strategy. Addressing the challenge of synthesizing tasks with both scientific depth and quantifiable metrics, we adopted a hierarchi-cal three-stage construction strategy comprising multi-agent generation, adversarial dialectical verification, and expert adjudication. 

Heterogeneous Multi-Agent Generation. To mitigate single-model knowl-edge bias [75], we assembled a consortium of four advanced LLMs (GPT-4 [76], Claude-3.7 [77], Gemini-2.5-Pro [78], and DeepSeek-R1 [79]). Configured with a uni-fied prompt simulating a senior computational biologist, this ensemble engaged in a parallel role-playing protocol. Over 100 interaction rounds, the system generated a pre-liminary corpus of 400 candidate questions, leveraging distinct reasoning architectures to enhance stylistic diversity and domain coverage.

## Agents Evaluation Strategy 

Pointwise Reasoning for Pairwise Evaluation. To address the stochastic nature of biological tool invocation and the limitations of standard LLM-based evaluation 

—specifically calibration errors in pointwise scoring and verbosity bias in pairwise comparisons —we implemented a decoupled evaluation architecture termed Pointwise Reasoning for Pairwise Evaluation . This framework separates evidence extraction from final adjudication to ensure objectivity. In the analytic phase, an Agent Ana-lyst (based on GPT-4o-mini) decomposes response pairs based on strict criteria: scientific validity, evidence sufficiency, and logical consistency. Crucially, this agent 14 produces structured qualitative critiques rather than numerical scores, isolating fac-tual accuracy from stylistic presentation. Subsequently, an independent judge instance synthesizes these evidence-based audits to determine a winner , thereby grounding the adjudication in verified content rather than surface-level fluency. 

Rank-Weighted Scoring Protocol. To quantify performance across the model cohort, we employed a scoring metric derived from exhaustive pairwise interactions. For a given query, models are ranked according to their total victory frequency. To penalize mediocrity while rewarding consistent superiority, we apply a linear rank-weighting scheme. Let N denote the total number of competing models, Wm the total win count for model m, and rm the specific rank of the model derived from Wm (where 

r = 1 indicates the highest win count). The final normalized capability score Sm is calculated as: 

Sm = Wm × (N − rm + 1) 

N (4) The reported performance metric represents the mean Sm across the test corpus, providing a robust indicator of relative scientific reasoning capability that accounts for both win rate and comparative standing. 

## Selection of the Reasoning Backbone 

To identify the optimal reasoning engine for VenusFactory2, we evaluated four state-of-the-art foundation models: Claude-3.7-Sonnet [77], DeepSeek-Chat [79], GPT-4o-mini [82], and Gemini-2.5-Pro [78]. All models were assessed on the VenusAgentEval benchmark using official API configurations. The evaluation protocol focused on two critical dimensions: 

Strategic Planning Assessment. This dimension evaluates the generative qual-ity of the Principal Investigator module, focusing on (1) Feasibility , ensuring the proposed scheme forms a closed execution loop without invoking non-existent tools; (2) 

Logical Adequacy , verifying coherent sequencing and the absence of step-skipping; and (3) Efficiency , identifying and penalizing redundant subtasks such as superfluous structural retrieval for sequence-based predictions. 

Scientific Response Assessment. This dimension evaluates the output of the 

Scientific Critic , prioritizing (1) Correctness , gauging the accuracy of reasoning-based task completion; (2) Causal Completeness , measuring the integrity of inference chains; (3) Hallucination Control , scrutinizing outputs for fabricated bio-logical entities; and (4) Constraint Adherence , ensuring strict compliance with prompt instructions. We employed a two-stage evaluation strategy where qualitative profiles from the initial analysis were fed into a second-stage judge model for pairwise comparison. Overview. VenusFactory2 operates as a closed-loop multi-agent system designed to automate protein engineering workflows. The architecture transforms abstract natural language queries into executable bioinformatics pipelines through a centralized orches-tration mechanism. The workflow follows a sequential logic: (1) task decomposition and planning, (2) code generation and tool execution, (3) autonomous error correc-tion, and (4) result synthesis and reporting. This process is collaboratively executed by four specialized agents: the Principal Investigator, the Machine Learning Specialist, the Computational Biologist, and the Scientific Critic. The specific implementation and functional responsibilities of each agent are detailed below. 

Principal Investigator. The Principal Investigator serves as the central plan-ner, responsible for interpreting research intent and architectural task decomposition. Upon receiving a user query, this agent performs three critical functions: (1) Intent Analysis: It parses the input to extract key entities, such as target sequences or database identifiers, and determines the necessary analysis paradigms. (2) Feasibil-ity Assessment: By cross-referencing the internal tool library, it constructs a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of analysis steps, defining input-output dependencies and execu-tion priorities. (3) Context Management: To support iterative optimization, the agent implements a two-level memory mechanism. At the interaction level, it retains conver-sational context to resolve ambiguities in multi-turn dialogue. At the execution level, it serializes and stores intermediate results and parameter configurations, allowing the system to bypass redundant computations in subsequent steps. 

Machine Learning Specialist. The Machine Learning Specialist functions as the algorithmic engine, tasked with the lifecycle management of predictive models. Its core responsibilities include: (1) Dynamic Code Generation: It translates the PI’s abstract requirements into executable Python scripts, implementing architectures ranging from lightweight regression models for multi-site variant scoring to fine-tuning large protein language models on domain-specific datasets. (2) Automated Debugging: The agent monitors runtime logs in real-time. Upon detecting exceptions or convergence failures, it triggers a self-correction loop to diagnose error sources, adjust hyperparameters, or revise data preprocessing logic without human intervention. (3) Tool Registration: Post-training, the agent encapsulates model weights and inference interfaces into stan-dardized tools, registering them into the system library for immediate invocation by other agents. 

Computational Biologist. The Computational Biologist acts as the interface for biological data acquisition and standard bioinformatics tool execution. Guided by the central plan, this agent executes the following tasks: (1) Multimodal Data Retrieval: It queries external repositories —such as sequence databases, structural archives, and literature indices —to curate training data or reference sets. (2) Tool Orchestration: It manages the execution of standard bioinformatics utilities, includ-ing sequence alignment, structural folding, and functional annotation tools, ensuring proper parameterization and resource allocation. (3) Data Standardization: To address format inconsistencies common in biological data, the agent implements adaptive pars-ing logic, converting heterogeneous outputs into unified data structures compatible with downstream machine learning modules. 17 Scientific Critic. The Scientific Critic serves as the quality assurance and syn-thesis module. Its primary function is to transform raw computational outputs into coherent scientific insights. This involves: (1) Logical Verification: During execution, the agent audits intermediate results against the initial research objectives, flag-ging inconsistencies, missing data, or biological implausibility for re-evaluation. (2) Result Integration: It aggregates disparate data streams —including model metrics, structural files, and functional predictions —into a consolidated analysis record. (3) Narrative Reconstruction: Finally, the agent synthesizes a structured report that sum-marizes methodology, interprets key findings with supporting evidence, and proposes data-driven directions for subsequent experimental validation.

Developing task-specific predictive tools in protein engineering traditionally demands profound cross-disciplinary expertise. Specifically, fine-tuning modern protein lan-guage models presents significant operational bottlenecks due to the necessity of navigating high-dimensional hyperparameter spaces, configuring training pipelines, and managing heterogeneous datasets. Consequently, optimal predictive performance is frequently inaccessible to researchers lacking specialized algorithmic knowledge. To overcome these barriers, VenusFactory2 features a closed-loop, self-evolving workflow that empowers its multi-agent system to autonomously orchestrate the end-to-end model development lifecycle. Upon encountering requests beyond the scope of its existing tool library, the system avoids execution failure by dynami-cally standardizing input data, selecting appropriate foundational architectures, and formulating optimized training scripts. By abstracting away these engineering com-plexities, VenusFactory2 transforms static tool libraries into a dynamic repository, consistently yielding customized predictors and packaging them as standard Model Context Protocol (MCP) services for future reuse. To empirically validate this workflow, we conducted a case study in de novo 

allergenicity predictor synthesis. The campaign was initiated with the following prompt: 

> “Please evaluate the allergenicity potential for these modified food enzymes and the protein sequence is in the uploaded file.”

Upon receiving the request, the Principal Investigator identified the intent as protein allergenicity analysis. After cross-referencing the tool library, it recognized a methodological gap: existing modules handled general functional annotation but lacked a dedicated allergenicity predictor. To resolve this, the agent autonomously decided to synthesize a novel tool and generated a complete execution plan: 

> Step 1: Search papers related to allergenicity prediction. Step 2: Use web search to find datasets for allergen / non-allergen prediction. Step 3: Split the downloaded training data into an 8:2 training and validation set. Step 4: Generate the training configuration file for the ESM2-8M model with LoRA. Step 5: Train the model using the generated configuration and report performance. Step 6: Package the trained checkpoint as a new MCP service and register it into the VenusFactory2 tool registry. Step 7: Use the newly minted MCP tool to predict the sequences in the provided FASTA file.

Following this blueprint, the Computational Biologist and Machine Learning Spe-cialist collaborated to execute the plan. They began with keyword searches across PubMed and arXiv, utilizing the summarized findings to guide dataset retrieval from open platforms such as Hugging Face. Upon processing the dataset into the requi-site 8:2 split, the Machine Learning Specialist selected the ESM2-8M architecture and autonomously fine-tuned it via LoRA for two epochs, monitoring convergence metrics without human intervention. Once training concluded, VenusFactory2 reported an accuracy of 0.92 and an AUROC of 0.96 on the independent test set. The agent subsequently packaged the 19 model checkpoint, inference schemas, and metadata into a new MCP tool, register-ing it within the platform library. Finally, VenusFactory2 deployed this synthesized tool on the user’s uploaded FASTA sequence, while the Scientific Critic trans-lated the technical metrics into an accessible final report. Through this process, a transient experimental requirement was transformed into a permanent, discoverable computational asset. 

## Adaptive Directed Evolution of a VHH Antibody 

Protein directed evolution constitutes a formidable challenge in protein engineer-ing, historically bottlenecked by the exponential growth of the mutational landscape. Even screening single-point mutations demands rigorous evaluation of structural constraints, functional effects, and experimental feasibility. To demonstrate how VenusFactory2 supports iterative combinatorial optimization, we deployed the system in a directed evolution campaign aimed at enhancing the binding affinity of a VHH antibody. The process was initiated via a natural-language directive specifying the wild-type target: 

> “I have a specific VHH antibody with the protein sequence MQVQLVESGGGLAQAGGSLR LSCAVSGMPEFARAMGWFRQAPGKERELLAAIEGIGATTYYADSVKGRFTISRDD AANTVLLQMNSLKPDDTAVYYCAAAFSVTIPTRARHWVDWGPGTLVTVSSDDDD KSGGGGSHHHHHH. The current goal is to improve the binding affinity of this antibody.”

Upon receiving this request, the Principal Investigator interpreted the objective as a structure-guided affinity maturation task and formulated the following execution protocol:   

> Step 1: Predict the three-dimensional conformation of the provided VHH sequence using AlphaFold2 to establish a structural baseline. Step 2: Perform structural homology mining across comprehensive databases using Fold-Seek to curate an evolutionary profile. Step 3: Execute a zero-shot, in silico saturated single-point mutational scan utilizing the VenusREM architecture.

Orchestrating this blueprint, the Computational Biologist first resolved the struc-ture via AlphaFold2 [3] and retrieved structural homologs from databases including AFDB and PDB via FoldSeek [11]. These multimodal inputs —sequence, predicted structure, and homology alignments —were autonomously routed to VenusREM [8]. The Scientific Critic then analyzed the resulting zero-shot fitness landscape, isolating high-potential single-point mutation sites to guide the first round of wet-lab validation. Following the acquisition of first-round experimental fitness data, the human researcher uploaded the results to the platform to initiate the combinatorial optimiza-tion phase: 

> “For this VHH, I have completed single-point mutation experiments. The experimental data are in the uploaded folder. Please recommend higher-order mutation sites based on these results.”

20 Recognizing that standard zero-shot tools lack the capacity to natively model com-plex epistatic interactions from user-provided empirical data, the Principal Investiga-tor autonomously pivoted the computational strategy to bridge this methodological gap: 

> Step 1: Dynamically synthesize and train a regularized Ridge Regression model using the uploaded empirical data to predict combinatorial epistatic effects. Step 2: Batch-evaluate higher-order mutants and rank the top-performing combinatorial candidates.

Tasked with this objective, the Machine Learning Specialist autonomously parsed the uploaded wet-lab data, applied one-hot encoding to the mutant sequences, and trained a regularized Ridge Regression model [23]. This supervised architecture effectively mapped single-point empirical priors to epistatic contributions, mitigat-ing overfitting. Leveraging this autonomously generated model, the Scientific Critic 

executed high-throughput batch inference across the combinatorial sequence space, ranking and recommending the top-performing double, triple, and quadruple mutants. Retrospective analysis against ground-truth wet-lab binding data confirmed that the agent-synthesized regression model achieved significantly higher experimental correlation for multi-point variants than a naive linear summation of single-point effects. This campaign underscores VenusFactory2’s capacity to autonomously rec-ognize methodological limitations and dynamically synthesize bespoke algorithmic solutions, functioning as an adaptive computational co-scientist for iterative biological optimization. 

## End-to-End Protein Discovery and Precision Mining 

Navigating billion-scale metagenomic spaces to discover novel, industrially rele-vant enzymes traditionally requires assembling disjointed bioinformatics pipelines and manually configuring complex filtration thresholds. To enable comprehensive discovery, VenusFactory2 integrates a massive, deduplicated sequence repository —

spanning databases like GOPC [43], UniProt [19], MGnify [44], and OMG [45] —with an agent-orchestrated screening infrastructure powered by VenusMine [20]. Rather than relying on static filters, VenusFactory2 empowers its agents to dynamically assemble structure-aware search and biophysical screening protocols directly from natural-language objectives. To validate this end-to-end capability, we conducted a blind retrieval campaign for polyethylene terephthalate (PET) hydrolases. The discovery process was initiated with a purely functional description rather than a traditional template sequence: 

> “Please search the metagenomic databases to discover a novel PET hydrolase that exhibits significantly higher thermostability and catalytic activity than the standard IsPETase.”

Upon receiving this prompt, the Principal Investigator recognized the intent as a de novo enzyme discovery task requiring both homology mining and stringent biophysical filtration. It subsequently formulated a comprehensive execution strategy: 

> Step 1: Search for scientific literature on PETase to provide an overview and background information.

21 Step 2: Retrieve the protein sequence and information for the well-studied PETase from Ideonella sakaiensis using its UniProt ID . Step 3: Download the predicted protein structure for PETase from the AlphaFold database using its UniProt ID. Step 4: Conduct enzyme mining using VenusMine. Step 5: Screen candidates for thermostability. Step 6: Screen candidates for solubility. Step 7: Rank the surviving candidates and compile the final enzyme discovery report. 

Following this blueprint, the agents autonomously executed the multi-stage pipeline. The Research module first identified a reliable seed PETase from the lit-erature. Transitioning to sequence space expansion, the Computational Biologist 

employed FoldSeek [11] for structural alignment against the AlphaFold Database, fol-lowed by a cascade expansion into the million-scale database using MMseqs2 [47] to construct a non-redundant library. To distill this vast pool, the system leveraged ProstT5 [84]. Operating in sequence-to-structure translation mode, it encoded candi-dates into high-dimensional embeddings, allowing the agent to cluster sequences based on conserved catalytic core architectures while discarding structurally divergent hits. Crucially, during the in silico screening phase, the Computational Biologist 

departed from static retrieval protocols. Interpreting the user’s explicit requirement for high thermostability and activity, it dynamically selected predictive assays from the Biophysical Filtration Suite, deploying fine-tuned ESM-2 [35] for melting tem-perature ( Tm), ProtSolM [49] for solubility, and AlphaFold2 [3] to verify structural fidelity. Through this agent-mediated orchestration, VenusFactory2 successfully narrowed the vast candidate space down to KbPETase (APET-14) [20], a novel enzyme from 

Kibdelosporangium banguiense . The discovered enzyme exhibited exceptional exper-imental metrics: a melting temperature ( Tm) of 80.1°C, exceeding IsPETase [85] by 32°C; a catalytic activity at 50°C that is 97x that of IsPETase and 5.7x that of the ther-mophilic benchmark LCC; and a catalytic efficiency ( kcat /Km) 1.87x and 1.3x higher than those of the engineered FastPETase [86] and LCC, respectively. This success-ful recapitulation demonstrates how VenusFactory2 translates a declarative natural language prompt into the precision retrieval of high-performance enzymes.
