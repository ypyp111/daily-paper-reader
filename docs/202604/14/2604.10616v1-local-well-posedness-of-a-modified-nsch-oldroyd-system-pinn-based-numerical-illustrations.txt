LOCAL WELL-POSEDNESS OF A MODIFIED NSCH–OLDROYD SYSTEM: PINN-BASED NUMERICAL ILLUSTRATIONS  

> WOOJEONG KIM 11DEPARTMENT OF MATHEMATICS AND INSTITUTE FOR SCIENTIFIC COMPUTING AND APPLIED MATHEMATICS, INDIANA UNIVERSITY, BLOOMINGTON, IN 47405, USA
> Abstract. Motivated by thrombus modeling, we study a modified Navier–Stokes–Cahn–Hilliard–Oldroyd system and consider PINN-based numerical illustrations for the modified system. To enable the analysis, we introduce a diffusion-enhanced system for the deformation variable while preserving the associated dissipative energy structure. We prove local well-posedness for this new system. We also present PINN-based numerical illustrations for representative thrombus cases and report resid-ual losses and benchmark errors obtained with Metropolis–Hastings sampling based on the energy decay.

Mathematics Subject Classification: 35D35, 35G31, 35Q35, 76T30 

Keywords: Navier–Stokes–Cahn–Hilliard system, Oldroyd model, local well-posedness, strong so-lutions, uniqueness, energy dissipation, PINNs. 1. Introduction 

Recently, fluid–structure interaction (FSI) models have been developed in increasingly detailed forms to describe the interaction between deformable structures and surrounding fluids. Among such models, diffuse-interface Navier–Stokes–Cahn–Hilliard (NSCH) systems have been widely used as continuum models for binary-fluid dynamics [27]. In [24], a fully Eulerian formulation was intro-duced for the velocity field. Moreover, NSCH thrombus model [26, 25] incorporated additional Oldroyd-B-type equations to represent elastic stress. Based on these developments, more detailed NSCH-type systems have been proposed for thrombus dynamics using physical reference data. However, in those works, the simulations are guided by external data, while rigorous mathematical results such as well-posedness or strong-solution theory are not established. By contrast, the same thrombus model of [6] established local well-posedness and uniqueness of strong solutions for the governing system under suitable initial data. This paper proved a mi-crostructural energy-dissipation property, which provides a useful analytical framework for un-derstanding deformation dynamics. However, from the viewpoint of stabilization and simulation, the governing system in the paper lacks a diffusion term in the deformation variable F . This ab-sence makes the system less robust both analytically and computationally, since diffusion terms are technically useful for controlling higher-order terms in the a priori estimates and for simulating stabilized systems. To address this issue, we modify the system in [6] by adding a small diffusion term to the defor-mation equation while preserving the underlying physical and analytical stability of the model. One theoretical contribution of this paper is therefore the construction of a modified governing system together with a diffusion term for the deformation variable F . This framework provides a more stable basis for analysis and for future data-assimilation-type applications. The main analytical contribution of this paper is the proof of local well-posedness for the resulting system.

Motivated by this analytical development, we also perform numerical simulations for the coupled NSCH system using physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). As pointed out in [31, 30], one of the main difficulties in PINN simulation is the accurate resolution of regions in which the solution exhibits sharp gradients. In phase-field models, this difficulty is concentrated near the interface of the phase variable, where rapid spatial variation occurs. Nevertheless, for a governing system of complexity comparable to that in [30], we obtain stable PINN simulations without relying on the reference-data setting used there for bubble dynamics. This serves as a supplementary numerical component of the paper. Therefore, the main objective of this paper is to develop and analyze a diffusion-enhanced NSCH– Oldroyd-type thrombus model and establish its local well-posedness. As a secondary component, we present PINN-based numerical illustrations showing that the modified system supports stable simulations in representative thrombus cases, including challenging interfacial regimes. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we derive the energy-dissipation law and establish the a priori estimates for the governing system of the proposed model. In particu-lar, the stabilization effect of the new model is realized through the diffusion term by demonstrating higher order of strong solution in the main well-posedness theorem. In Section 3, we prove the local well-posedness of the governing system. In Section 4, we present PINN-based numerical illustrations for the modified system. For technical description of the neural network architecture, we used 10 layers as width and 128 neurons as depth on ( x, y, t )inputs of two-dimensional spatial variables and time variable to output ( u1, u 2, ϕ, F 11 , F 12 , F 21 , F 22 , p )as in the governing system (1.2). Sampling method is Latin hypercube sampling(LHS) on the spatial domain and uniform distribution sampling on the time domain. We used Adam optimizer and tangent hyperbolic activation function. For number of epochs as-signed with learning rate, 250 epochs for learning rate 10 −3, 200 epochs for learning rate 10 −4,100 epochs for each of learning rates 10 −5, 10 −6, 10 −7 are set. This Appendix also contain the chasing the energy value for along time variable. 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we studied a modified viscoelastic NSCH-type thrombus model together with a diffusion-enhanced approximating system designed to stabilize the governing system. From the theoretical analysis of the approximating system, we derived a local well-posedness time interval for the governing equation. As a supplementary numerical component, we presented PINN-based numerical illustrations for representative thrombus cases. In the numerical experiments, the sweeping-window PINN training together with Metropolis– Hastings energy-adaptive sampling improved accuracy in challenging phase-field interfacial cases, where sharp transitions are commonly difficult to simulate. Several directions for future work remain. In particular, parameter selection remains challenging, since physically calibrated parameter regimes and robust hyperparameter pipelines are not yet fully established. Possible applications to data-assimilation-based diagnostics remain as a direction for future work.
