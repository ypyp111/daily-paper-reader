Abstract. This paper establishes an approximation theorem for randomized neural networks (RaNNs) whose hidden-layer parameters are uniformly sampled from a prescribed bounded domain. Our analysis shows that, for RaNNs of the form ∑ 

> i

Wiσ(Ai, b i), the size of the sampling domain required to achieve optimal approximation is intrinsically linked to the smoothness of the target function and the number of neurons. Motivated by this theoretical insight, we integrate a partition of unity (PoU) with RaNNs to develop an adaptive physics-informed randomized neural network (PI-RaNN) method for solving partial differential equations with limited local regularity. The proposed adaptive strategy refines the PoU based on a posteriori error indicators, enabling the network to efficiently capture localized solution features. Numerical experiments validate the theoretical results and demonstrate the strong approximation capabilities of RaNNs, confirming the effectiveness of the adaptive PIRaNN method on a range of benchmark problems.

1. Introduction. In recent years, neural networks have garnered significant at-tention as a promising tool for solving partial differential equations (PDEs). Unlike traditional numerical methods—such as the finite element and finite difference meth-ods—which rely on spatial discretization over computational meshes, neural network-based approaches seek to approximate PDE solutions directly by training networks with strong universal approximation capabilities, thereby eliminating the need for mesh generation. Furthermore, these methods can effectively mitigate the curse of di-mensionality often encountered in conventional mesh-based schemes. As a result, a va-riety of neural network frameworks have been developed, including Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) [29], DeepONet [27], and the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) [24], demonstrating considerable potential in overcoming the limitations of classical numerical techniques. Theoretical research has extensively demonstrated the powerful function approx-imation capabilities of neural networks. For instance, constructive proofs of universal approximation theorems for deep neural networks under various activation functions have been provided in [17, 7, 26]. For shallow neural networks with simpler architec-tures, Ellacott [13] showed that any function defined on a compact set can be approxi-mated provided the activation function is non-polynomial. In a seminal work, Barron [2] employed statistical arguments to derive an approximation rate of O(N −1/2) for shallow networks with sigmoidal activation, where N denotes the number of neurons. Notably, this rate is independent of the input dimension d, offering theoretical sup-port for circumventing the curse of dimensionality. This result was later refined by Klusowski and Barron [23], who improved the convergence rate to O(N − 1

Despite the profound approximation capacity of neural networks, identifying the optimal approximating function within the ansatz space remains a significant chal-lenge when solving PDEs. This difficulty arises from the nonlinear nature of activation functions, which renders the search for the best network approximation equivalent to solving a high-dimensional non-convex optimization problem. In practice, optimiza-tion over neural networks is typically performed using algorithms such as Adam or L-BFGS. However, these methods generally converge to local minima rather than the global optimum, and their convergence rate can be heavily influenced by the smooth-ness of the target function. To address these challenges, various strategies have been proposed. These include introducing scaling parameters into activation functions to accelerate convergence [20], adaptively reweighting the loss function to balance gradi-ent components during back-propagation [35, 36], employing higher-order optimizers to improve training efficiency [34, 30], and applying the extended variable technique to promote the learning of smoother function approximations [18, 3]. Due to the challenges associated with training deep neural networks, randomized neural networks (RaNNs) have attracted increasing interest for solving PDEs. In such architectures, the internal parameters (e.g., weights and biases) of the hidden layers are randomly initialized according to a prescribed probability distribution and remain fixed throughout training. Only the weights of the output layer are trainable, thereby reducing the original nonlinear, non-convex optimization problem to a linear least-squares problem for linear PDEs. This particular randomized model is widely known as Extreme Learning Machines (ELMs) [19]. Building upon both the Physics-Informed Neural Networks framework and the ELM methodology, Dwivedi and Srinivasan sub-sequently proposed the Physics-Informed Extreme Learning Machine (PIELM) [12]. To further improve accuracy and efficiency, Dong and Li introduced the local Ex-treme Learning Machine (locELM) [9] based on a domain decomposition strategy. Similarly, Chen et al. [5] developed a random feature method by combining ran-domized networks with a partition of unity (PoU). The theoretical approximation properties of these randomized architectures have been analyzed in several recent works [16, 17, 8, 28, 25]. Since the internal parameters of RaNNs are fixed and sampled from a prede-termined distribution, classical approximation theorems for general shallow neural networks are not directly applicable. In this work, motivated by the analysis in [31], where approximation rates were established for shallow networks with polynomially-decaying non-sigmoidal activations, we extend the definition of the Barron spectral space [32, 31] to a generalized Barron spectral space. We prove that functions belong-ing to this space can be approximated by RaNNs whose parameters are generated via uniform sampling from a bounded domain, and we establish a corresponding conver-gence rate in the Sobolev space W k,p for 2 ≤ p < ∞. Theoretical analysis indicates that, to achieve the optimal approximation, the size of the sampling domain for the inner-layer parameters of the RaNNs should scale as O (N 1/s ), where s denotes the smoothness index of the target function and N is the number of neurons (see Sec-ADAPTIVE PIRANNS FOR SOLVING PDES 3tion 3). This scaling exposes an intrinsic dependence between the required sampling domain and the regularity of the target function: for a fixed number of neurons, less smooth functions require a larger domain to attain better approximation. In other words, the optimal parameter sampling strategy is strongly influenced by the smoothness of the function being approximated. Leveraging the established relationship between the parameter sampling range and the target function’s smoothness, we construct a PoU over the physical domain of the PDEs. The parameters of the RaNNs are first generated on a reference element and are then mapped to each physical subdomain via an affine transformation. This construction effectively couples the range of the sampled parameters to the local size of the PoU elements. In particular, a finer partition (smaller element size) corresponds to a locally enlarged effective sampling domain for the RaNNs parameters within that element. Motivated by strategies widely used in adaptive finite element methods [10, 4, 21], we employ residual-based a posteriori error estimates to identify which elements require refinement. This leads to an adaptive PIRaNNs framework, where the network parameter distribution and the physical discretization are co-adapted to efficiently capture solution features. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces the basic framework of RaNNs and PIRaNNs, followed by the definition of the proposed gen-eralized Barron spectral spaces. Section 3 presents a convergence analysis in Sobolev spaces for the approximation of functions in the generalized Barron spectral space by RaNNs. Section 4 details an adaptive PIRaNNs algorithm. Numerical experiments in Section 5 support the theoretical analysis of Sections 3, demonstrating that the adaptive PIRaNNs achieves robust approximation. Concluding remarks are provided in Section 6.

Remark 3.10. The approximation theorems established in Theorems 3.7 and 3.9 exhibit a complex dependency on multiple parameters, such as the decay rate r (As-sumption 3.2) of the activation function and the smoothness of the target function u,which is characterized by the generalized Barron spectral norm (2.8). Taking p = 2 and letting η → ∞ (i.e., assuming that u is sufficiently smooth and the activation function σ decays rapidly), we obtain the following asymptotic rates: Theorem 3.7: O(N −1/2) and Theorem 3.9: O(N −1/2−1/(d+1) ).

This result demonstrates that the obtained rates match the optimal approximation rates established in the seminal works of Barron and Xu et al. for SLFNs (see [31]). 16 RAN BI AND WEIBING DENG

Remark 3.11. Theorems 3.7 and 3.9 explain the relationship between the sam-pling domain Gξ (M ) × Gs(M ) and the target function u for achieving optimal ap-proximation when using RaNNs. Taking Theorem 3.7 as an example, the optimal rate is attained when

Remark 4.4. It should be emphasized that Adaptive PIRaNNs differ fundamen-tally from adaptive finite element methods in that continual mesh refinement does not indefinitely reduce the approximation error. Beyond a certain refinement threshold, further subdivision of the mesh ceases to improve accuracy, as the local parameter sampling region has already reached its optimal coverage. 6. Conclusion. In this paper, we have developed a comprehensive theoretical and algorithmic framework for solving PDEs using RaNNs. We introduced a gener-alized Barron spectral space Bk,p s (Ω) to characterize functions that can be efficiently approximated by RaNNs whose hidden-layer parameters are uniformly sampled from a bounded domain. For functions in this space, we established explicit convergence rates in Sobolev norms, revealing a fundamental relationship between the required sampling range and the smoothness of the target function. Specifically, we showed that less smooth functions necessitate a larger sampling domain to achieve optimal approximation, a result that provides theoretical guidance for parameter selection in practice. Motivated by this insight, we integrated a PoU with RaNNs to develop an adap-tive PIRaNN method. By linking the parameter sampling range to the local element size via affine mappings, the PoU framework effectively translates the challenge of generating appropriate basis functions into the more tractable task of designing a solution-adaptive partition. We further incorporated a posteriori error estimates and the D¨ orfler marking strategy to drive adaptive refinement, enabling the network to automatically concentrate computational resources in regions where the solution ex-hibits limited regularity. A series of numerical experiments validated both the theoretical analysis and the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results confirmed that the convergence rate of RaNNs depends critically on the sampling range relative to the smoothness of the solution, and that the adaptive PIRaNN method successfully cap-tures localized features such as singularities and shocks. Notably, the method main-tains consistent convergence rates across L2 and H1 norms, in contrast to traditional finite element methods which often exhibit order reduction in derivative approxima-tions. Extensions to the viscous Burgers’ equation further demonstrated the applica-bility of the framework to nonlinear, time-dependent problems. Several directions warrant future investigation. First, while the present work fo-cuses on uniform sampling, exploring more sophisticated sampling strategies—such as importance sampling or adaptive parameter distributions—could further improve convergence rates. Second, the theoretical analysis could be extended to deeper archi-tectures and more general activation functions. Third, the integration of the adaptive PIRaNN framework with domain decomposition and parallel computing strategies holds promise for large-scale, high-dimensional problems. Finally, a systematic study of optimal penalty parameter selection and its impact on convergence would enhance the practical robustness of the method. In summary, this work provides a rigorous foundation for understanding and en-hancing the approximation capabilities of randomized neural networks in the context of PDEs. The proposed adaptive PIRaNN method offers a principled, theoretically grounded approach to solving problems with limited solution regularity, bridging the ADAPTIVE PIRANNS FOR SOLVING PDES 29 gap between classical numerical analysis and modern machine learning techniques.
