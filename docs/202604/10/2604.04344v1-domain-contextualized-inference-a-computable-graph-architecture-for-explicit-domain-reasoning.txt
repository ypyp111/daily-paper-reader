Abstract 

We establish a computation-substrate-agnostic inference architecture in which domain—the semantic context governing each reasoning operation—is an explicit first-class parameter rather than an implicit annotation or external condition. This design decision, grounded in the representational semantics of the Domain-Contextualized Concept Graph (CDC) framework (Li & Wang, 2026), yields computational properties that have not been systematically characterized: domain-scoped pruning that reduces per-query search space from O(N) to O(N/K), substrate-independent execution over symbolic, neural, vector, and hybrid computational substrates, and transparent inference chains in which every step carries its evaluative context. We do not claim new logical expressiveness; the contribution is architectural. This paper formalizes the computational theory of this architecture across five dimensions: (1) a five-layer computational architecture with well-defined inter-layer interfaces; (2) three domain computation modes with full operational semantics—chain indexing as CSP arc-consistency, path traversal as Kleisli composition in the Domain Context Monad, and vector-guided computation as a substrate transition to the neural interface; (3) a substrate-agnostic inference interface whose three operations—Query, Extend, Bridge—admit symbolic, neural, vector, and hybrid implementations with explicit semantic guarantees at each level; (4) reliability conditions C1–C4 with explicit assumptions, and three failure mode classes with detection and mitigation criteria; (5) validation through a PHQ-9 clinical reasoning case study demonstrating that the theoretical mechanisms correspond to identifiable computational operations in a real-world inference task. The representational foundation is due to Li & Wang (2026). The computational theory—operational semantics, complexity bounds, monad structure, substrate transitions, and boundary conditions—is the contribution of this paper. 

Keywords: inference engines, domain-contextualized reasoning, computable graphs, neuro-symbolic systems, knowledge representation, Kleisli composition, constraint satisfaction 

# 1 Introduction 

1.1 Domain as a Computational Parameter 

We treat domain—the semantic context within which a reasoning operation is scoped—as an explicit computational parameter rather than a mere metadata attribute. This is a departure from standard practice in formal reasoning systems, where domain context is embedded in external query logic, application code, or implicit ontological commitments; it is not encoded in the reasoning unit itself. The consequences of this departure are the subject of this paper. Human reasoning is irreducibly contextual. The concept "atom" means an indivisible particle in Dalton’s chemistry, a quantum field excitation in modern physics, and an uninterruptible operation 1

> arXiv:2604.04344v1 [cs.AI] 6 Apr 2026

in concurrent programming. This is not ambiguity—it is how cognition works. Concepts derive meaning from the frames within which they are understood (Fillmore, 1982; Gärdenfors, 2000). Formal reasoning systems have largely ignored this fact. In standard first-order logic, a predicate symptom_of(headache, migraine) carries no contextual qualification. In RDF knowledge graphs (Hogan et al., 2021), the triple (headache, symptom_of, migraine) applies globally. In Prolog, rules fire without regard to semantic scope. Domain context exists, but it lives outside the reasoning structure—in query conditions, in application logic, in the programmer’s intentions. It is implicit. When domain is implicit, the system cannot directly exploit it for pruning. Every query must consider all facts in the knowledge base. Cross-domain reasoning requires manual ontology alignment. Personalized or context-sensitive reasoning requires bespoke engineering for each new context. These are not expressiveness limitations—they are organizational limitations. The information required for scoped reasoning exists, but the architecture does not expose it to the inference engine at the operational level. 

1.2 This Paper’s Scope 

The Domain-Contextualized Concept Graph (CDC) framework (Li & Wang, 2026) establishes the representational foundation: a four-tuple <concept, relation@domain, concept’> in which domain is a modal world constraint rather than a metadata annotation, grounded in Kripke-style possible-world semantics (Kripke, 1963). That work defines the structural vocabulary—twenty-plus relation predicates with specified algebraic properties—and demonstrates representational consequences including a priori disambiguation and cross-domain structural transfer. The core representational unit is: 

> tau = <c, r@d, c '>

where c, c’ are concept nodes, r is a relation predicate, and d is a domain specification. The @ operator is not a namespace separator—it is a semantic scope operator that parameterizes the meaning of r by d. The computational semantics of this structure remain uncharacterized in prior work: how reasoning propagates through the five-layer architecture, what the complexity bounds are for core operations, what monad structure underlies multi-hop domain traversal, under what conditions the system is reliable, and how it achieves substrate independence. This paper provides that characterization. It presupposes the representational semantics of Li & Wang (2026) and builds the computational theory on that foundation. We do not claim that CDC introduces new logical expressiveness beyond what is achievable in existing systems. Our claim is narrower and more precise: making domain an explicit com-putational parameter changes how reasoning is organized and executed, enabling computational properties—domain-scoped pruning, substrate independence, transparent inference traces—that require substantial external engineering when domain remains implicit. The contribution is architec-tural, not logical.

# 2 Background and Related Work 

2.1 The Reasoning Systems Landscape 

Reasoning systems occupy a design space defined by three competing properties: expressiveness, computational tractability, and semantic transparency. No existing system achieves all three. 3First-order logic and Prolog. First-order logic is Turing-complete and highly expressive, but satisfiability is undecidable in general. Prolog restricts to Horn clauses, achieving semi-decidability with SLD-resolution, but provides no mechanism for domain-scoped reasoning. A Prolog predicate r(X, Y) has a single global interpretation; there is no native construct for expressing that r means different things in different semantic contexts. 

Description logics and OWL. Description logics achieve decidability by restricting quantifi-cation and constructors. OWL ontologies provide rich taxonomic reasoning with complexity ranging from P to 2EXPTIME depending on the profile. However, domain context is implicit: a class Atom in an ontology has a fixed interpretation, and representing the same concept under multiple contextual framings requires either ontology alignment or URI disambiguation—both costly. 

Constraint satisfaction systems. CSP frameworks are highly efficient for combinatorial search, with arc consistency achievable in O(ed 2) for e constraints over d-valued variables. However, CSP does not provide a semantic layer: constraints are syntactic conditions, not meaningful relations between conceptual entities. 

Graph neural networks. GNNs, including Relational GCN (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018), achieve strong generalization over graph-structured knowledge through message passing. R-GCN parameterizes message passing by relation type, learning separate weight matrices W_r per relation. However, domain context is absent: W_r is global, and there is no mechanism for domain-conditioned semantic variation. The reasoning process is opaque by design. 

Neuro-symbolic systems. Recent neuro-symbolic approaches (Garcez & Lamb, 2023; Mao et al., 2019; Yi et al., 2018) seek to combine the interpretability of symbolic reasoning with the generalization of neural networks. These systems typically maintain separate symbolic and neural components that interact at defined interfaces. CDC’s substrate-agnostic interface provides a natural architecture for this integration: the same knowledge base can be executed symbolically for auditability or neurally for generalization, with explicit characterization of the semantic tradeoffs at each substrate. 

Contextualized knowledge systems. Bozzato et al.’s (2018) Contextualized Knowledge Repositories (CKR) and Giunchiglia and Ghidini’s (2001) Local Model Semantics provide context-indexed knowledge bases with inheritance. McCarthy’s (1993) formalization of context as a first-class object in logic and Klarman and Gutiérrez-Basulto’s (2016) description logics with contextual parameters further address formal treatments of context. CDC’s computational contribution relative to these systems is the domain-explicit architecture at the tuple level—domain is a structural component of the four-tuple arity, not an external index—and the explicit cross-domain bridge operations with characterized computational complexity. 

Knowledge graph embedding. TransE (Bordes et al., 2013), RotatE (Sun et al., 2019), and ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016) embed knowledge graph entities and relations in vector spaces for link prediction. These methods are relation-global: the embedding of a relation r is fixed regardless of domain. CDC’s W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d parameterization conditions relation embeddings on domain, enabling domain-specific semantic variation that global embeddings cannot represent. 

The gap. None of these systems treats domain as an explicit computational parameter at the level of the reasoning unit. CDC occupies a previously uncharacterized position in this design space: it achieves domain-scoped reasoning with bounded decidability, transparent inference chains, and—as we establish in Section 5—substrate independence. 42.2 CDC Representational Foundation 

We briefly summarize the representational structure established by Li & Wang (2026), which serves as the formal object of our computational analysis. A CDC triple is a four-tuple τ = ⟨c, r, c ′, d ⟩ where c ∈ C (source concept), r ∈ R (relation predicate), c′ ∈ C (target concept), and d ∈ D (domain specification). Domain specifications are structured strings with recursive composition: 

> d := dimension | dimension@d

Examples: ’Physics’, ’Physics@Quantum’, ’HighSchool@Math@Calculus’. The framework defines a vocabulary of relation predicates organized into structural (is_a, part_of, has_attribute), logical (requires, cause_of, enables, contrasts_with), cross-domain (analogous_to, fuses_with), and temporal (evolves_to) categories, each with specified algebraic properties including transitivity, symmetry, and monotonicity under domain inheritance. The modal semantics: each domain d defines a possible world w_d in a Kripke frame 

⟨W, R _acc, V ⟩ (Kripke, 1963). The assertion r(c, c ′, d ) is read as □_d r (c, c ′)—the relation holds necessarily within world w_d. This interpretation has computational consequences for how inheritance (Layer 3) and cross-domain reasoning (Layer 4) must be implemented. A reference implementation in Prolog is provided in Li & Wang (2026). Our analysis takes this structure as given and characterizes its computational properties. 

2.3 Cognitive and Linguistic Foundations 

The motivation for domain-explicit reasoning is grounded in cognitive science. Fillmore’s frame semantics (1982) establishes that word meanings are interpreted relative to conceptual frames—structured knowledge about typical situations. Gärdenfors’ conceptual spaces (2000) models concepts as regions in multidimensional quality spaces, where similarity and categorization are frame-dependent. Barsalou (1982) demonstrates empirically that human categorization adapts systematically to situational context. These findings converge on a single claim: there is no context-independent meaning. CDC operationalizes this claim computationally by making the contextual frame—the domain—an explicit parameter of every reasoning operation. 

# 3 The Five-Layer Computational Architecture 

We characterize CDC reasoning as a five-layer architecture. Each layer has a distinct computational role, and the layers interact through well-defined interfaces. 

3.1 Overview 

The layers are not independent modules but components of a unified computation. A single reasoning step may traverse multiple layers: a query enters at L2, triggers a domain inheritance check at L1 via L3, finds a cross-domain bridge at L4, and resolves the applicable inference rules by consulting L5. 5Layer Name Computational Role Key Operation              

> L1 Domain Lattice Indexing and pruning Partial order traversal L2 Fiber Concept Graphs Intra-domain reasoning SLD-resolution L3 Reindexing Functors Cross-layer knowledge in-heritance Monotone pullback L4 Cross-Fiber Bridges Inter-domain reasoning channels Morphism / Coproduct L5 Meta-Layer Self-describing inference rules Reflective query

3.2 Layer 1: The Domain Lattice 

The domain lattice D is a partially ordered set where the ordering relation ≤ reflects domain specificity:    

> 'Physics@Quantum '<= 'Physics '<= TOP

More specific domains are lower in the lattice; the universal domain ⊤ is the top element. The domain lattice serves as the indexing structure for all CDC reasoning. Its primary computa-tional role is pruning : when a query is issued with domain d, the search space is restricted to the fiber F(d) associated with that domain. Facts outside F(d) are not evaluated. This is the CDC analog of arc consistency in CSP. The domain string d acts as a constraint that eliminates infeasible variable assignments before search begins. The complexity reduction is significant: in a knowledge base of N triples distributed across K domains of average size N/K, domain pruning reduces per-query search space from O(N) to O(N/K). The domain lattice also defines the inheritance structure exploited by Layer 3. 

3.3 Layer 2: Fiber Concept Graphs 

For each domain d in D, the fiber F(d) is a directed graph whose nodes are concepts and whose edges are instances of relation predicates scoped to d: 

> F(d) = { <c, r, c '> | r(c, c ', d) }

Reasoning within a fiber proceeds by SLD-resolution over the Horn clause representation of CDC triples. Each relation type imposes algebraic constraints on the fiber graph: 

• is_a@d and requires@d induce partial orders (transitive, asymmetric) 

• contrasts_with@d and analogous_to@d induce symmetric relations 

• has_attribute@d is neither transitive nor symmetric The fiber is the primary locus of CDC computation. Most reasoning operations—fact lookup, transitive closure, prerequisite chain computation—execute entirely within a single fiber. 6Key property. Facts in different fibers are semantically independent. The same concept node c may have different type memberships, different attributes, and different prerequisite relations in different fibers. This is the Domain Separation property established by Li & Wang (2026): 

r(c, c ′_1, d _1) and r(c, c ′_2, d _2) with c′_1̸ = c′_2 and d_1̸ = d_2 are consistent. 

3.4 Layer 3: Reindexing Functors 

Layer 3 handles knowledge inheritance along the domain lattice. When a query in fiber F(d_child) cannot be resolved, the system may consult the parent fiber F(d_parent) where d_parent >= d_child. The inheritance mechanism is not unconditional. We distinguish two classes of predicates: 

Monotone predicates: The truth of r(c, c ′, d _parent ) implies the truth of r(c, c ′, d _child )

for all d_child ≤ d_parent . Taxonomic relations (is_a) and prerequisite relations (requires) are monotone: if c is a subtype of c′ in Physics, it remains a subtype in Physics@Quantum. 

Non-monotone predicates: The truth in a parent domain does not propagate to child domains. Oppositional relations (contrasts_with) and conflict relations (conflicts_with) are non-monotone: what contrasts in a general domain may not contrast in a specialized subdomain. The reindexing functor F (d_parent → d_child ) performs selective pullback: it transfers facts along monotone predicates while blocking non-monotone predicates. This prevents semantic contamination while enabling knowledge reuse. 

Note. Building on the representational foundation of Li & Wang (2026), we introduce the reindexing functor as a computational mechanism that operationalizes inheritance along the domain lattice. Without explicit reindexing rules, child-domain queries cannot access parent-domain knowledge, resulting in isolated knowledge silos despite the hierarchical domain structure. The reindexing functor resolves this incompleteness and is a primary computational contribution of the present paper. 

3.5 Layer 4: Cross-Fiber Bridges 

Layer 4 provides two operations that connect distinct fibers: 

analogous_to@D_1 ↔D_2. This predicate establishes a structural correspondence between concepts in different fibers. Formally, it is a partial morphism between fiber graphs: a mapping 

ϕ : F (D_1) → F (D_2) that preserves some relational structure. The analogical bridge allows inference chains to transfer across domain boundaries. Example:   

> analogous_to(neural_network, brain, 'CS@ML ','Biology@Neuroscience ')

This does not assert identity or logical equivalence. It asserts structural similarity sufficient to support analogical inference: properties known of the brain under neuroscience may be proposed (not asserted) as hypotheses about neural networks under CS@ML. 

fuses_with@D_1+ D_2. This operation constructs a new domain D_new as the coproduct of D_1 and D_2, and asserts that a new concept emerges from the conceptual integration of existing concepts across the merged domain. Example: 7fuses_with(user_experience, technical_feasibility, product_design, 'UX+Engineering ')

Unlike analogous_to, fuses_with modifies the domain lattice: D_new = D_1 + D_2 is added as a new node. This is a generative operation that can, in principle, produce unbounded domain growth—a failure mode addressed in Section 7. 

3.6 Layer 5: The Meta-Layer 

Layer 5 is the self-descriptive layer of CDC. Relation predicates themselves are represented as concept nodes and described using CDC triples:    

> is_a(requires, TransitiveRelation, 'Logic ')has_attribute(requires, monotone, 'Logic ')contrasts_with(requires, enables, 'Logic ')

The meta-layer serves two computational functions. First, it drives the reindexing functor in Layer 3: the system consults L5 to determine whether a given predicate is monotone before applying inheritance rules. Second, it enables runtime rule adaptation: if the meta-layer description of a predicate changes, the inference behavior changes without modifying the object-level knowledge base. The meta-layer instantiates a reflective reasoning architecture: CDC can reason about its own reasoning rules. This is analogous to Prolog’s meta-interpreter pattern but integrated into the knowledge structure rather than implemented in external code. 

# 4 Domain Computation Modes 

The domain parameter d can be computed in three distinct modes, each with different operational semantics. 

4.1 Mode 1: Chain Indexing 

In chain indexing, the domain string is used directly as a prefix filter. A query with domain ’Physics@Quantum’ matches facts whose domain string starts with ’Physics@Quantum’, and by the reindexing rules of Layer 3, may also consult ’Physics’ and ⊤.

> def query(concept, relation, domain_prefix): return [e for e in kb if e.concept == concept and e.relation == relation and e.domain.startswith(domain_prefix)]

This is a CSP arc-consistency operation. The domain string acts as a constraint that eliminates irrelevant variable bindings before search. Complexity: O(k) where k = |F(d)|, the size of the target fiber. 8The key property is that domain pruning is applied before reasoning, not after. This is the computational basis for the 50 × speedup reported by Li & Wang (2026) for a 100K-triple knowledge base distributed across 50 domains. 

4.2 Mode 2: Path Traversal as Kleisli Composition 

In path traversal, concept nodes and domain nodes alternate in a traversal path: 

> c1 ->[r1@d1]-> c2 ->[r2@d2]-> c3 -> ...

The path itself constitutes the reasoning trace. Each step applies a relation in a specific domain context; the domain may change between steps, triggering Layer 3 reindexing or Layer 4 bridge traversal. 

Kleisli structure. Path traversal has a precise categorical interpretation as Kleisli composition in the Domain Context Monad (Moggi, 1991). Define the monad T as:  

> T(C) = P(C x D) -- the powerset of concept-domain pairs

A single reasoning step is a Kleisli arrow:  

> f_{r,d} : C -> T(C) f_{r,d}(c) = { (c ', d ') | r(c, c ', d) holds, d 'computed by reindexing from d }

Kleisli composition ▷ sequences two such arrows: 

> (f_{r1,d1} triangleright f_{r2,d2})(c) = UNION_{(c2, d2 ') in f_{r1,d1}(c)} f_{r2,d2 '}(c2)

A multi-hop reasoning chain c_1 → [r_1@ d_1] c_2 → [r_2@ d_2] c_3 is the Kleisli composition: 

> f_{r1,d1} triangleright (bridge: d1->d2) triangleright f_{r2,d2}

where the bridge step invokes the Layer 4 morphism when the domain changes between hops. 

Monad laws. The Domain Context Monad satisfies the standard monad laws: 

• Left identity: return (c) ▷ f = f (c) — starting from a single concept and applying f gives f ’s result 

• Right identity: f ▷ return = f — composing with the trivial step is identity 

• Associativity: (f ▷ g ) ▷ h = f ▷ (g ▷ h ) — the order of composing steps does not affect the result 9These laws guarantee that multi-hop reasoning chains can be decomposed and recomposed without changing their semantics, and that the reasoning trace is well-defined regardless of how intermediate steps are grouped. 

Operational consequence. The Kleisli structure is what makes path traversal strictly more expressive computationally than Mode 1: it supports domain-changing paths where each hop may operate in a different fiber, connected by reindexing or bridge traversal. The original Prolog implementation of Li & Wang (2026) approximates this through depth-first search but does not explicitly characterize the monad structure, which means it lacks the composability guarantees that the Kleisli formulation provides. 

Complexity. A path of length L across domains d_1, ..., d_L has complexity O(L . max_i |F(d_i)|). With reindexing at each step, this becomes O(L . depth(D) . max_i |F(d_i)|) where depth(D) is the maximum depth of the domain lattice. 

4.3 Mode 3: Vector-Guided Computation 

In vector-guided computation, domain strings are embedded into a vector space, and domain relationships are computed by vector similarity rather than string matching.  

> h_d = embed(d) # domain embedding similarity(d1, d2) = cos(h_d1, h_d2)

Characterization. Mode 3 is not a third independent logical mode parallel to Modes 1 and 2. It is a substrate transition: it moves domain computation from the symbolic layer (string matching, lattice traversal) to the neural substrate (embedding similarity), enabling two operations that symbolic modes cannot support without manual specification: 

Automatic bridge discovery. If sim (h_D_1, h _D_2 ) > θ , an analogous_to bridge between fibers F(D_1) and F(D_2) is automatically activated. This removes the requirement for manually specified cross-domain bridges. 

Graded domain membership. A concept may belong to a domain with a degree rather than binary membership, enabling soft domain boundaries and fuzzy inheritance. 

CDC-GNN instantiation. Mode 3 enables the CDC-GNN variant, in which the message passing formula becomes:  

> h_c '= sigma( SUM_{r,d} mask_d * W_{r,d} * h_neighbor ) W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d

where mask_d is the domain membership indicator (a differentiable version of Mode 1 pruning), and W_{r,d} is the tensor product of relation and domain embeddings. This parameterization is the key distinction from R-GCN (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018), which uses W_r—a relation-only weight matrix. CDC-GNN’s W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d conditions the transformation matrix jointly on relation and domain, enabling is_a@Physics and is_a@Biology to learn distinct semantic transformations. 

Semantic status. Results from Mode 3 are proposals, not assertions. Automatically discovered bridges require validation before entering the symbolic layer. The threshold θ controls the precision-recall tradeoff: higher θ produces fewer, more reliable bridges. 10 Scope. The formal operational semantics of Mode 3 within the symbolic framework—including the precise conditions under which vector-discovered bridges are sound with respect to the modal semantics of Li & Wang (2026)—constitute an open problem identified in Section 9.1. Within this paper, Mode 3 is characterized as a substrate transition rather than a logically independent mode, and its computational properties are analyzed at the substrate level in Section 5. 

# 5 The Substrate-Agnostic Inference Interface 

A central claim of this paper is that CDC functions as a computation-substrate-agnostic inference interface: the same knowledge structure can be executed over different computational substrates without modifying the knowledge base. 

5.1 The Interface Structure 

The CDC inference interface is defined by three operations: 

Query(c, r, d) → {c’}: Given a source concept, relation, and domain, return the set of target concepts satisfying the relation in the domain. 

Extend(c, r, d, c’) : Assert a new CDC triple into the knowledge base. 

Bridge(D_1, D_2) → phi: Return the cross-domain morphism between two fibers, if one exists. These operations are substrate-independent: their input/output types are defined in terms of CDC concepts, relations, and domains, not in terms of any particular execution mechanism. 

Extensibility note. The four-tuple ⟨c, r @d, c ′⟩ defines the minimal inference unit. Additional parameters—temporal indices, session identifiers, confidence weights, state annotations—can be incorporated at the application layer without modifying the interface. The semantic scope operator 

r@d remains the atomic unit of domain-constrained inference; supplementary parameters are handled outside it. The interface is backward-compatible: systems that ignore additional parameters recover standard CDC behavior. The four-tuple is a floor, not a ceiling. 

5.2 Four Substrate Implementations 

Symbolic substrate (Prolog). Query resolves by SLD-resolution over Horn clause representations of CDC triples. Reliability: complete over the knowledge base, no hallucination. Limitation: cannot handle ambiguous natural language input; knowledge base must be explicitly populated. 

> query(C, R, D, C2) :- call(R, C, C2, D).

Neural substrate (GNN). Query resolves by message passing with domain-conditioned weight matrices W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d. Reliability: probabilistic output, generalization across unseen concepts. Limitation: opaque reasoning, hallucination risk. 

> h_c_prime = sigma(sum(mask_d * W_r_d @ h_neighbor for r, d in active_edges)) # where W_r_d = outer_product(h_r, h_d)

11 Vector substrate. Query resolves by approximate nearest-neighbor search in domain-projected embedding space. Reliability: soft matching, handles linguistic variation. Limitation: semantic precision is approximate. 

Hybrid substrate. The natural language input is processed by a neural component; the output is verified against the symbolic substrate. This is the architecture validated in the Communications Medicine study (Prenosil et al., 2025): GPT-4 (neural) extracts candidate facts; Plato-3 (symbolic) verifies them against medical rules. 

> Input -> Neural(extract candidate facts) -> Symbolic(verify against CDC rules) -> Output (verified) or Human Review (discrepancy)

5.3 Substrate Selection Criteria 

The choice of substrate is determined by the requirements of the deployment context:      

> Requirement Preferred Substrate
> Auditability required Symbolic Ambiguous natural language in-put Neural or Hybrid Cross-domain bridge discovery Vector Zero hallucination constraint Symbolic or Hybrid Large-scale pattern learning Neural Regulatory compliance (EU AI Act) Symbolic or Hybrid

The substrate-agnostic interface means that these choices can be made at deployment time, not at knowledge-base construction time. A knowledge base built for symbolic execution can be reused for neural execution without modification. 

5.4 Semantic Preservation Across Substrates 

A substrate transition preserves CDC semantics if the three interface operations return equivalent results across substrates, up to the precision guarantees of each substrate. 

Symbolic to neural: The symbolic knowledge base can be used as training supervision for the neural substrate. Facts in the knowledge base provide positive examples; the closed-world assumption provides negative examples. With sufficient training, the neural substrate approximates the symbolic substrate’s query results. 

Neural to symbolic: High-confidence neural predictions can be reified as new CDC triples in the symbolic knowledge base. This is the "neural discovery rule" pattern in the Omni Medical framework: neural pattern detection generates candidate rules that, after expert validation, are added to the symbolic layer. 

The asymmetry: The symbolic-to-neural direction is a relaxation (trading precision for coverage). The neural-to-symbolic direction is a strengthening (trading coverage for precision). Both directions are semantically coherent within the CDC interface. 12 Note. A rigorous formalization of semantic preservation across substrates—particularly the conditions under which neural approximation is sound with respect to the modal semantics of Li & Wang (2026)—requires a formal theory of approximate knowledge base equivalence that is beyond the scope of this paper. Section 9.1 identifies this as an open problem. 

# 6 Core Algorithms and Complexity Analysis 

6.1 Domain-Aware Transitive Closure 

Input: Concept c, transitive relation r, domain d. Output: All concepts reachable from c via r within the domain fiber F(d), with reindexing along the domain lattice.  

> function transitive_closure(c, r, d): visited = {} result = [] queue = [c] while queue is not empty: current = queue.pop() if current in visited: continue visited.add(current) # L2: Query current fiber direct = query(current, r, d) # L3: Reindexing—consult parent domains if r is monotone if is_monotone(r): # resolved via L5 meta-layer query for d_parent in parents(d): direct += query(current, r, d_parent) filtered to concepts in F(d) # L4: Bridge traversal—follow analogous_to edges for (current, c_bridge, d, d_target) in bridges(current, d): bridged = query(c_bridge, r, d_target) for b in bridged: mark_as_hypothesis(b, derivation_depth=1) direct += bridged for target in direct: if target not in visited: result.append(target) queue.append(target) return result

Complexity. The base traversal within a single fiber is O(|V_d| + |E_d|) with adjacency indexing, where V_d and E_d are the vertices and edges in the target fiber F(d). Worst-case O(k 2)under naive scanning without indexing. With reindexing across L levels of the domain lattice, this becomes O(L . (|V_d| + |E_d|)). When L4 bridge traversal is enabled, let B denote the number of active bridges from domain d to other domains; each bridge triggers a query in a foreign fiber. The total complexity is therefore O((L + B) . (|V_d| + |E_d|)). Note that bridged results are marked as hypotheses (not assertions) to prevent the analogical drift failure mode described in Section 7.2. 13 Note on is_monotone resolution. The predicate is_monotone(r) is not a hardcoded property but a runtime query against the L5 meta-layer: has_attribute(r, monotone, ’Logic’). If the meta-layer is modified during reasoning (e.g., a predicate’s monotonicity status is revised), the behavior of the reindexing step changes accordingly. Under condition C3 (meta-layer boundedness), this lookup is O(1) with indexing; without C3, it may require a traversal of the meta-layer graph. 

Note on domain distribution: The expected query scope |F(d)| depends on the distribution of facts across domains. Under uniform distribution across K domains, |F (d)| ≈ N/K . In practice, knowledge bases often exhibit skewed distributions; the relevant bound is O(|F(d)|) for the specific domain queried. 

6.2 Bidirectional Fixed-Point Iteration 

When R is represented as a CDC node (Layer 5), concept embeddings h_c and relation embeddings h_r are mutually dependent. Fixed-point iteration computes the stable embedding:   

> function fixed_point_iteration(G, epsilon): initialize h_c, h_r randomly repeat: # Update concept embeddings using current relation embeddings for each concept c: h_c_new = aggregate({W_{r,d} ·h_neighbor for (c, r, c ', d) in G}) # where W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d # Update relation embeddings using updated concept embeddings for each relation r: h_r_new = aggregate({h_c_source + h_c_target for (c, r, c ', d) in G}) delta = max(||h_c_new - h_c||, ||h_r_new - h_r||) h_c, h_r = h_c_new, h_r_new until $\delta < \varepsilon$ return h_c, h_r

Convergence. This algorithm applies to the neural substrate (Section 5.2) during embedding computation—it is the mechanism by which concept and relation embeddings are jointly learned or updated, not a general-purpose inference procedure. Convergence is not guaranteed in general. If the joint update operator T : ( h_c, h _r) → (h_c_new, h _r_new ) is a contraction mapping—that is, if the operator norm of W_{r,d} is bounded such that the spectral radius of the global update is strictly less than 1—then convergence follows from the Banach fixed-point theorem. In practice, this condition can be enforced via spectral normalization of W_{r,d} during training. Without this constraint, the iteration may oscillate or diverge. For inference on the neural substrate with fixed (pre-trained) parameters, a single forward pass suffices and the convergence question does not arise. 

Structural bound on spectral radius. Because W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d is a rank-1 matrix (the outer product of two vectors), its operator norm equals ||h_r|| . ||h_d||. Spectral normalization of h_r and h_d to unit norm therefore guarantees ||W_{r,d}||_2 = 1, and with a non-expansive aggregation function (e.g., mean pooling with appropriate scaling), the contraction condition is 14 satisfiable. This structural property of the tensor product parameterization—absent in standard GNN weight matrices—makes the convergence condition checkable directly from the embedding norms rather than requiring full spectral analysis of the weight matrix. 

Complexity per iteration: O(|E| · d2) where |E| is the number of edges and d is the embedding dimension. Number of iterations to convergence under the contraction condition: 

O(log(1 /ε )/ log(1 /λ )) where λ < 1 is the contraction factor. 

6.3 Cross-Domain Bridge Discovery 

Input: Two domain fibers F(D_1), F(D_2), similarity threshold θ. Output: Set of analogous_to pairs {(c_1, c_2)} with c_1 in F(D_1), c_2 in F(D_2). 

> function discover_bridges(D1, D2, theta): bridges = [] for c1 in F(D1): for c2 in F(D2): sim = cosine(embed(c1, D1), embed(c2, D2)) if sim > theta: bridges.append(analogous_to(c1, c2, D1, D2)) return bridges

Complexity: O(|F(D_1)| . |F(D_2)| . d) = O(k_1 . k_2 . d) where d is the embedding dimension, accounting for cosine similarity computation. For auto-bridging over all domain pairs: O(|D| 2 . k_bar 2 . d) where k_bar is average fiber size. 

Semantic note: Automatically discovered bridges are proposals, not assertions. They require validation before being added to the symbolic layer. The threshold θ controls the precision-recall tradeoff: higher θ produces fewer, more reliable bridges. 

# 7 Reliability Analysis 

7.1 Reliability Conditions 

CDC reasoning is sound and complete with respect to the knowledge base under the following conditions: 

C1 (Domain lattice finiteness). The domain lattice D has a finite maximum depth. This bounds the reindexing traversal in Layer 3 and prevents infinite inheritance chains. 

C2 (Predicate monotonicity specification). For each predicate r used in cross-layer inheritance, its monotonicity status is explicitly declared in the meta-layer: has_attribute(r, monotone, ’Logic’). Monotonicity is meta-specified rather than automatically verified; incorrect specification may yield unsound inheritance. The system trusts these declarations and does not independently verify them. 

C3 (Meta-layer boundedness). The set of predicates described in the meta-layer is finite and stable. If new predicates are continuously added, the meta-layer query may not terminate. 

C4 (Acyclicity for transitive predicates). For predicates r that are both transitive and used 15 in transitive closure computation, the fiber graph F(d) must be acyclic with respect to r. Cycles cause non-termination in Algorithm 6.1. 

7.2 Failure Modes 

Failure Mode 1: Domain proliferation via fuses_with. 

fuses_with generates new domain nodes. If applied iteratively: 

> fuses_with(D1, D2) -> D3 fuses_with(D3, D4) -> D5 ...

the domain lattice grows without bound, violating C1. This is a halting problem in general. 

Detection: Monitor |D| during reasoning. If |D| exceeds a threshold, halt and require human review. 

Mitigation: Require explicit authorization for fuses_with operations. Alternatively, define a domain algebra with a closure condition that bounds the number of generated domains. 

Failure Mode 2: Cyclic prerequisites. 

If requires(A, B, d) and requires(B, A, d) both hold, Algorithm 6.1 enters an infinite loop. 

Detection: Check acyclicity of the requires graph in F(d) before executing transitive closure. Standard cycle detection runs in O(|V| + |E|). 

Mitigation: Reject or flag cyclic requires assertions at knowledge base construction time. 

Failure Mode 3: Analogical drift via transitive bridge chaining. 

If analogous_to bridges are transitively chained: 

> analogous_to(A, B, D1, D2) analogous_to(B, C, D2, D3) -> analogous_to(A, C, D1, D3)?

the resulting bridge may not preserve structural similarity, introducing semantic drift. 

Detection: Track bridge derivation depth. Bridges derived by chaining more than one step should be flagged as hypotheses. 

Mitigation: Do not automatically assert transitively derived bridges. Require explicit validation for each bridge, regardless of how it was discovered. 

7.3 Comparison with Classical Systems 

Decidability under C1-C4. Under conditions C1-C4, the CDC query language is decidable: every well-formed query terminates and returns a definite answer. This follows from the finiteness of the domain lattice (C1), the finiteness of each fiber (implicit in the finite knowledge base), acyclicity for transitive predicates (C4), and the bounded meta-layer (C3). This stands in contrast to FOL (undecidable in general) and Prolog (semi-decidable via SLD-resolution, with non-termination possible for recursive predicates). Description logics achieve decidability by restricting logical constructors; CDC achieves decidability by restricting the domain structure while preserving full relational expressiveness within each fiber. 16 Property FOL Description Logic Prolog Multi-Context Systems CDC

The decidability of CDC under C1-C4 is therefore not a limitation but a design feature: it guarantees that the inference engine terminates, which is a prerequisite for deployment in safety-critical applications such as clinical reasoning. 

# 8 Case Study: PHQ-9 as a Reasoning Engine Test 

8.1 Motivation 

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) is a standardized instrument for depression screening. It consists of nine items corresponding to DSM-IV diagnostic criteria, each scored 0-3 by frequency, with total scores mapping to severity levels and clinical action thresholds. We use PHQ-9 not as an application demonstration but as a test case for the computational mechanisms described in Sections 3-7. PHQ-9 has several properties that make it suitable for this purpose: it has a well-defined logical structure (diagnostic criteria are explicit), it has known reliability properties (inter-rater reliability is established), and it has clear failure modes (under-reporting due to stigma, over-reporting due to distress). These properties allow us to verify that the CDC reasoning mechanisms behave as theoretically predicted. 

8.2 CDC Representation of PHQ-9 

The PHQ-9 structure maps naturally to the three-layer CDC representation: 

# Fact layer: patient utterances cdc_fact(P001, 'lost_interest_in_activities ', '@Psychology@PHQ9 ', 'Anhedonia '). cdc_fact(P001, 'poor_sleep_recently ', '@Psychology@PHQ9 ', 'SleepDisturbance '). # Rule layer: diagnostic criteria cdc_rule( 'Anhedonia ', '@Psychology@PHQ9@Item1 ', [ symptom_list: ["no longer enjoys previous activities", "cannot get motivated at work", "unable to experience pleasure"], frequency_scale: {0: "not at all", 1: "several days", 2: "more than half the days", 3: "nearly every day"} ]). # Action layer: clinical process cdc_action(P001, 'PHQ9 ', 'r-score@Psychology@PHQ9 ', 14). 

17 cdc_action(P001, 'PHQ9 ', 'r-severity@Psychology@PHQ9 ', 'moderate '). cdc_action(P001, 'SuicidalIdeation ', 'r-alert@Psychology@PHQ9 ', 'high '). 

The domain hierarchy @Psychology@PHQ9@Item1 instantiates a three-level fiber: the clinical domain (Psychology), the instrument domain (PHQ9), and the item domain (Item1). Each level has distinct reasoning rules. 

8.3 Verification of Computational Mechanisms 

Mode 1 (Chain indexing) verification. A query for requires chains within @Psychology@PHQ9 retrieves only PHQ9-relevant prerequisites, not general psychological prerequisites. The CSP pruning reduces the search space from the full psychology knowledge base to the nine-item PHQ9 scope. 

Mode 2 (Path traversal / Kleisli composition) verification. The full assessment path instantiates a Kleisli chain: 

> patient utterance -> cdc_fact -> cdc_rule matching -> cdc_action (score) -> cdc_action (severity) -> cdc_action (alert, if SuicidalIdeation detected)

Each arrow is a Kleisli step f_{r,d}: the source concept (e.g., SuicidalIdeation) is mapped to a target concept (e.g., high-alert) within a specified domain (@Psychology@PHQ9). The chain is composable: intermediate results feed into subsequent steps without domain leakage. The complete reasoning chain is preserved and auditable, consistent with the monad structure established in Section 4.2. 

Algorithm 6.1 (Transitive closure) verification. PHQ9 item dependencies (e.g., Suici-dalIdeation requires assessment of all other items) are computed by transitive closure within the PHQ9 fiber. Acyclicity condition C4 holds: PHQ9 items do not have circular dependencies. 

Failure mode verification. Item 9 (suicidal ideation) triggers an alert that bypasses the normal scoring path. This is modeled as a conflict_with relation that activates the alert pathway. The system correctly identifies this as a non-monotone condition (an alert in the PHQ9 domain does not propagate to the general Psychology domain). 

8.4 The Silent Assessment Extension 

The standard PHQ-9 requires explicit patient cooperation. The CDC framework enables a computa-tionally natural extension: silent assessment , in which PHQ-9 scoring proceeds continuously over conversational input without explicit questionnaire administration. In this extension, every patient utterance is processed through the cdc_fact layer, mapped to PHQ-9 dimensions via the cdc_rule layer, and accumulated in the cdc_action layer. The scoring computation runs incrementally. This extension introduces two computational problems not present in standard PHQ-9: 

Problem 1: Utterance confidence. The reliability of a cdc_fact assertion derived from conversational input is lower than one derived from explicit questionnaire response. We model this as a confidence weight w in [0,1] on each cdc_fact, propagated through scoring: 18 score(item) = SUM frequency(symptom) * confidence(utterance) for symptoms mapped to item 

Problem 2: Inconsistency detection. Conversational input may contain inconsistent signals: a patient may report high energy in one utterance and fatigue in another. Standard PHQ-9 does not encounter this because each item is assessed once. Silent assessment must detect and resolve inconsistencies. We model three types of inconsistency: 

• Logical inconsistency: contradictory symptom assertions within a domain (e.g., both Sleep-Disturbance and NormalSleep asserted for the same item period) 

• State inconsistency: temporal variation in symptom reports (e.g., high distress followed by apparent normalcy) 

• Cognitive inconsistency: mismatch between expressed affect and reported symptoms Each type has a distinct detection algorithm and resolution strategy within the CDC framework. These form the computational foundation for the dynamic assessment research agenda discussed in Section 9. 

# 9 Discussion 

9.1 Theoretical Extensions 

Probabilistic CDC. The current framework admits a natural probabilistic extension: attach confidence values to CDC triples and propagate uncertainty through reasoning chains. This would require defining probability distributions over fiber graphs and characterizing the propagation semantics of each relation type under uncertainty. 

Temporal CDC. The evolves_to predicate provides a foundation for temporal reasoning, but a complete temporal extension requires a notion of time-indexed fibers: F(d, t) rather than F(d). This would enable reasoning about knowledge that changes over time while preserving historical states. 

Domain algebra formalization. The domain lattice currently relies on string-based prefix matching, which lacks formal algebraic semantics. A complete domain algebra would specify the meet, join, and complement operations on domains, providing a mathematical foundation for the reindexing functor and establishing when domain computations are sound. This formalization would also address the compositionality of fuses_with and the equivalence conditions between domain specifications. 

Topological and group-theoretic analysis. The domain lattice, as a partially ordered set, carries a natural topology (the Alexandrov topology). The three domain operations—chain indexing, path traversal, and vector-guided computation—can be characterized as continuous maps in this topology. The group-theoretic structure of cross-domain morphisms (analogous_to as group homomorphisms, fuses_with as coproduct) provides a richer algebraic framework for analyzing the compositionality of CDC reasoning. These analyses constitute the theoretical extensions referenced in the contributions. 19 Cross-substrate semantic equivalence. A rigorous formalization of the conditions under which neural and vector substrate results are sound with respect to the modal semantics of Li & Wang (2026) is an open problem. The informal characterization in Section 5.4 establishes the directions of approximation; the precise conditions require a formal theory of approximate knowledge base equivalence. 

9.2 Application Extensions 

Silent assessment and dynamic questionnaires. Section 8.4 introduces the computational problems of silent assessment. The full research program includes: confidence-weighted scoring, multi-type inconsistency detection, individual baseline modeling, trigger event identification, and adaptive questionnaire generation. Each problem has a well-defined formulation within the CDC framework. 

Cross-institutional knowledge propagation. The substrate-agnostic interface enables a federated knowledge architecture: institutions maintain local symbolic knowledge bases, share validated cdc_rule assertions, and receive updates without sharing patient data. The computational properties of this architecture—convergence time, consistency maintenance, conflict resolution—are open research problems. 

LLM integration interfaces. The CDC framework provides a natural interface for constraining LLM outputs: generated text is processed through the cdc_fact layer, validated against the cdc_rule layer, and either passed or flagged for human review. The formal characterization of this pipeline—including precision/recall tradeoffs at each layer—extends the analysis in Prenosil et al. (2025) to the general CDC framework. 

9.3 Limitations 

Scale validation. The computational analysis in this paper is theoretical. Empirical valida-tion at scale—knowledge bases of 10 6+ triples, hundreds of domains, real-time query loads—has not been performed. The complexity bounds are asymptotic; practical performance depends on implementation details not analyzed here. 

Domain proliferation control. Failure Mode 1 (unbounded domain growth via fuses_with) has no complete solution in the current framework. The mitigation strategies proposed in Section 7.2 are heuristic. A complete solution would require a formal domain algebra with a computable closure condition. 

Cross-substrate semantic equivalence. Section 5.4 characterizes substrate transitions informally. A rigorous formalization of semantic preservation across substrates—particularly the neural-to-symbolic direction—requires a formal theory of approximate knowledge base equivalence that is beyond the scope of this paper. 

Human-in-the-loop validation bottleneck. Several CDC operations—automatic bridge dis-covery (Section 6.3), fuses_with domain generation (Section 3.5), and neural-to-symbolic reification (Section 5.4)—require human validation before their outputs can be trusted as assertions rather than hypotheses. As the CDC graph scales to tens of thousands of nodes and hundreds of domains, the volume of proposed analogous_to and fuses_with operations may exceed human review capacity. Prioritization heuristics (e.g., ranking proposals by structural similarity score or by downstream impact on existing inference chains) can reduce the review burden, but a complete solution to the validation bottleneck—one that maintains semantic quality without requiring exhaustive human review—remains an open problem. 20 10 Conclusion 

We have presented the computational theory of Domain-Contextualized Concept Graphs: a five-layer inference architecture in which domain is a first-class computational parameter, characterized by three computation modes with operational semantics, a substrate-agnostic interface with four substrate implementations, and reliability conditions with explicit failure mode analysis. The central theoretical contribution is the characterization of multi-hop domain reasoning as Kleisli composition in the Domain Context Monad (Section 4.2). This formalization establishes that path traversal in CDC is not ad hoc graph search but a compositional operation with the associativity, identity, and composability guarantees of monad structure. The structural consequence—that W_{r,d} = h_r * h_d conditions neural message passing on both relation and domain—distinguishes CDC-GNN from R-GCN and other relation-parameterized architectures. The five-layer architecture provides a vocabulary for understanding how domain-explicit reasoning works at each computational level. The reliability conditions and failure mode analysis establish the boundaries within which CDC reasoning is sound, and identify the open problems that remain. The PHQ-9 case study demonstrates that these mechanisms are not merely theoretical: they correspond to identifiable computational operations in a real-world clinical reasoning task, and they reveal new research problems—silent assessment, inconsistency detection, dynamic questionnaire generation—that are computationally natural within the CDC framework but invisible within standard questionnaire-based assessment. The representational foundation of CDC is due to Li & Wang (2026). The computational theory developed here—operational semantics for all three domain computation modes, monad structure for path traversal, tensor product parameterization for neural substrate, and boundary conditions for reliable reasoning—establishes what that foundation enables as a principled inference infrastructure.
