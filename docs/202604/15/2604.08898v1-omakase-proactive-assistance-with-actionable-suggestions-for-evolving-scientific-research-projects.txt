# Abstract 

As AI agents become increasingly capable of complex knowledge tasks, the lack of context limits their capability to proactively rea-son about a user’s latent needs throughout a long evolving project. In scientific research, many researchers still manually query a deep research system and compress their rich project contexts into short,

# 1 Introduction 

AI agents can now complete complex tasks across domains, ranging from coding [ 4, 8 , 40 ] to design [ 3] to scientific discovery [ 14 , 20 ,34 ]. These advanced agents have shown high-level autonomy in completing complex tasks by breaking them down into smaller steps, and synthesizing all the intermediate results into a coherent output. However, as their capabilities rapidly grow, user interactions with them remain largely the same traditional request-response paradigm. Most agent systems only react to explicit instructions from users before taking action, either in single-turn queries or multi-turn conversations [ 2, 4, 40 ]. The vision of proactive agents that can take initiative to assist users is still not fully realized [ 17 ,39 ]; most prior work on proactive agents primarily investigated proactive interactions within short conversation sessions instead of long-horizon interactions [ 43 ]. For example, asking clarification questions [ 41 ], getting the user’s attention for confirming high-stake steps or fixing agent-failures [ 13 , 38 ], or chiming in a multi-user conversation [33, 55, 56]. In this paper, we explore an alternative interaction paradigm of supporting proactivity over long-horizon interactions with AI agents. More specifically, we study this in the context of designing a research support agent that observes a researcher’s evolving project document, and proactively provides literature-grounded sugges-tions that are contextualized to the rich project context. Literature-based research support is well-suited for this exploration due to the increased user interests in leveraging LLMs as research tools [ 30 , 36 ] and the rich prior work on building literature-based re-search support capabilities [5, 22, 28]. The two most common user interaction paradigms for literature-based research support are 1) Querying a deep research system that leverages LLM’s reasoning powers to synthesize many retrieved documents; and 2) Recommender systems that proactively send paper recommendations to their users based on a list of seed papers. Deep research systems (e.g., [49]) have the potential of leverag-ing LLMs to understand the user’s rich ongoing context. However, the dominant request-response paradigm can be prohibitively la-borious for users in two ways. First, it can be effortful to compress 

the rich context of a complex project down to detailed instructions. Recent findings indicate that, while users increasingly add more “Complex Contextual Narratives” to their queries to a literature review AI agent [ 15 ], providing context to an AI agent could be more effortful [ 43 ]. Second, the lengthy and comprehensive out-put of LLMs can be overwhelming for users, requiring substantial cognitive effort to turn them into concrete actionable suggestions— that is, suggestions that propose a specific course of action such as citing a paper for a claim, adopting a baseline, or revising a hypothesis [10, 21, 23, 52]. In contrast, paper recommender systems proactively send users recommendations of papers similar to a user’s curated seed papers. While this proactive approach alleviates the burden of constant query formulation, paper recommender systems also have limita-tions. First, the static lists of seed papers can quickly go stale as the project’s direction and user interests shift [ 57 ]. Prior work has showed that users rarely update their preferences on recommenda-tion systems, leading to stagnated feeds with similar and repetitive recommendations, causing user frustration and abandonment [ 57 ]. Even when a recommended paper is highly useful for a project, how 

it is useful may be buried in a monotonous list of recommendations and the full text of paper, requiring high effort for researchers to realize their usefulness [ 22 ]. For example, a paper might inform a researcher on how to design their user study, but this can easily be overlooked when receiving a paper recommendation email that typically lists only the titles and abstracts of the recommended papers. These evidences point to a future where agents observe and lever-age a user’s actively changing rich context to generate suggestions that are highly relevant and actionable to its users [17, 39]. In this work we explore Omakase , a system that observes a research project document, tracks the state of a project, formu-lates interesting and open questions, performs background queries, and expresses the results specifically in the context of the project. Instead of having users provide context at the moment of agent invocation, our approach leverages existing project documents. 

Omakase regularly checks for changes in the user’s project doc-uments, issues timely literature questions based on the project’s state to a literature deep research system, and derives actionable suggestions from deep research reports. This approach resolves the problem of stale context often found in recommendation systems, requiring almost no extra effort from the user. Concretely, this paper makes the following contributions: 

• Through an iterative design process spanning 15 months, we built three versions of a proactive research assistant, as-sessed each one with users (Table 1), revised the design, and iterated. These experiments yielded the following insights: 

• Cost of context engineering: Researchers disliked having to invest time and effort crafting the context of their project as background for an agent. Instead, they wanted the proactive assistant to monitor their existing artifacts and infer this context (Section 3.3.3). 

• Cost of contextualization: Instead of a long deep-research style report, users wanted specific, actionable suggestions on how to improve their projects (Section 3.3.2). 

• The final iteration of our system, Omakase , proactively generated agent tasks that participants found timely to their project context (Section 5.4.2), and included tasks they themselves might not have thought of without the system (Section 5.4.4). 

• These tasks led to suggestions that the participants rated as significantly more actionable , relevant , and timely when users spends similar amount of effort in the baseline condi-tion of reading top sections from a deep research system’s output (Section 5.4.1).

# 2 Related work 

Our work draws on two bodies of prior research: (1) proactive AI agents and the types of context they leverage, and (2) literature-based research support tools. We review each below and highlight where existing systems fall short of providing proactive, project-aware assistance. 

# 2.1 Context for proactive AI Agents 

Research on conversational AI agents has investigated how proac-tive agents might interact with users. A body of work has focused on identifying the right moment for an agent to chime in during conversation [ 31 , 32 , 41 ]. For example, Liu et al. introduce inner thoughts, a mechanism that helps an AI agent determine when to chime in [ 32 ]. Other works explore how conversational agents might ask clarifying questions from users to resolve ambiguity and improve performance [ 12 , 18 , 26 , 29 , 53 , 59 ]. The context of these agents and models is mostly conversation history for synchronous interventions. Other proactive agents embedded in domain-specific tools such as coding [ 9, 37 , 45 ], searching information [ 16 ] and writing [ 5 , 25 ,35 ] also have limited contexts in terms of scope and time. Some focus more on the appropriate time to offer users assistance without unnecessary interruptions [ 35 , 45 ]. Systems like Morae determine the decision points and proactively provide relevant task context for users to make informed decisions [ 42 ]. However, these sys-tems focus mostly on synchronous interactions where the users engage actively with the system. The context they used was task-specific and mostly lasted for a short session. Omakase ’s context, in contrast, is a long-horizon document for asynchronous proactive interactions. On the other extreme, there are systems that consume a com-prehensive long-horizon multi-modal context [ 47 , 48 , 58 ]. GUMBO observes a series of unstructured screenshots of a user over time to model users’ profiles and provide proactive personalized sup-port [ 47 ]. Shaikh et al. also introduce LongNAP, a model that predicts user next actions from user multi-modal interaction his-tory [ 48 ]. While showing potential in its flexibility, these approaches depend on interaction data which might not give a complete picture of a research project with multiple collaborators contributing from various sources. Further, there is a privacy concern when granting an agent access to all of a user’s activities. Omakase leverages ex-isting research artifacts that are more reliable than user interaction history. No prior work has investigated a system that observes a user’s documents for a particular project throughout its lifetime. 

# 2.2 Literature-based Research Support Tools 

There are multiple RAG-based LLM tools designed to support vari-ous research tasks from ideation [ 44 , 46 ], literature understanding and synthesis [13, 49] to paper critique [1]. While useful for their tasks, the scope of context employed by these tools is quite limited. Recent work on paper recommenda-tions has explored how to contextualize recommended papers to a user’s context. PaperWeaver uses an LLM pipeline to generate explanations of how a recommended paper is related to a user’s paper collection [ 28 ]. Kang et al. surface relevance between a rec-ommended paper and a user’s publication and interaction history using a knowledge graph [ 22 ]. The paper collection still needs to be curated manually and the generated explanations are topical instead of actionable suggestions related to a project. Some other tools do not require manual paper curation but have limited context. PaperPing chimes in with related papers during a Slack conver-sation; however, like other conversational AI agents, its context is limited to prior discussions [ 55 ]. MyScholarQA focuses more on profiling the user based on their publication history [ 6 ], which could be out of sync with their current research projects accord-ing to our formative study (Section 3). Unlike these systems that focus on a specific research activity, our work considers the con-text of the whole research project where literature needs shift over time [19, 51]. 

# 3 Formative Study with a Technology Probe 

We built an initial technology probe that periodically generated paper recommendations using an LLM based on user’s research interests document . The recommendations were delivered via email to 28 participants (4 female and 24 male; NLP, ML, HCI, and CV re-searchers) over an eight-week period. Using this technology probe, we explored the following questions: 

• Q1: What types of literature-based information do researchers want from a proactive system? 

• Q2: Would researchers maintain a free-form document as the primary input to such a system? 

• Q3: How well could an LLM-based pipeline generate useful recommendations from this richer context? 

# 3.1 Technology Probe: Living Research Interests Document 

The first iteration of our system is a technology probe consisted of three parts: (1) a shared Google Doc as a research interests document 

that can be freely edited by the participants; (2) a paper recom-mendations pipeline that periodically read the research interests document, retrieved papers from Semantic Scholar API [ 24 , 50 ] and an open-sourced deep research system [ 49 ], and generated brief explanations of their relevance to the user interests; and (3) recom-mendation emails delivered twice a week containing the paper rec-ommendations, from which users could add the paper back to their research interests document, asked an AI assistant about them, or

gave feedback through the emails. Through the deployment study, we iterated the system based on user’s feedback. 

3.1.1 Research interests document. This served as a lightweight shared memory between the user and the system: users maintained the document as personal notes about their current interests, while the system used it as the source of context for generating recom-mendations. Users could describe each topic however they wanted (e.g., sentences, bullet lists, links to another documents). The only constraint imposed was that they kept different topics as their own separate sections and added a few relevant papers to each topic. To address the cold-start problem, we seeded each document with the user’s five most recent publications under a default “My papers” topic. 

3.1.2 Paper recommendations pipeline. Based on the research inter-est document, the system generate paper recommendations through an LLM-powered pipeline with the following steps: (1) Topic and paper extraction The pipeline fetched the lat-est content of the user’s document and used an LLM to extract the following for each topic: title, description, and associated papers. 1 We also allowed participants to include links to other Google Docs, and follow those links to extract additional topics from them. This allowed us to tap into existing documents for their real research projects. (2) Paper retrieval. The system retrieved paper recommenda-tion using two methods. First, it used the seed papers to find their recent citations and similar papers using the Semantic Scholar APIs [ 24 ]. Second, based on the topic descriptions and notes from the user, the system generated natural lan-guage questions to query an open-sourced deep research system and extracted papers from its inline references [ 49 ]. Finally, we pooled papers from both methods and ranked them using a recent paper embedding technique [ 50 ], keep-ing recommend ones that were most similar to the seed papers. (3) Explanation generation. For each selected paper, an LLM generated a brief (2–3 sentence) explanation of why the paper was relevant to the user’s topic. The prompts took the recommended paper’s title and abstract and description of the topic as input (Appendix B.6). If it cited any of the seed papers, the citing sentence were also included (Appen-dix B.4). If the paper was from a deep research output, the question was provided as context (Appendix B.5). 

3.1.3 Recommendation emails. The system delivered recommenda-tion emails (Figure 2) twice a week, depending on the availability of highly relevant papers from recent publications. Each email covered up to two topics, each containing up to two recommended papers. For each recommended paper, the email displayed: 

• The paper title linked to the corresponding paper details page on Semantic Scholar.

# 3.2 Procedure 

Participants were informed that they would be receiving research update emails based on their research documents and they could opt out of the study if they wished. We observed their interactions with the research interests document and the emails from February 3rd, 2025 to March 30th, 2025. During the 8-week observation period, the system sent 383 total emails to 28 participants (DP1–DP28). Over the observation period, we tracked each participant’s in-teraction with the emails and their research interests document. Specifically, we tracked the following events: 

• Open paper : how many times a participant clicked a paper link in the email.

# 3.3 Findings 

Overall, the level of engagement was comparable to prior paper rec-ommendation systems in the literature. Each participant received 9–17 emails, and the median click-through rate (CTR) was 9.0%. For context, the CTR from a prior work on paper recommendation systems was 4.5–5.8% [ 22 ]. Twelve of 28 participants did not trigger any tracked event, though interviews revealed that most had still opened and read the emails. Half of the participants made edits to their interest documents during the 8-week deployment period. With sufficient engagement, we focused our analysis on qualita-tive insights from the interviews [ 7 ], with a goal of guiding the design of the next iteration of our system. We reached out to all 28 participants and 13 responded in a timely manner to be interviewed. 

3.3.1 Researchers found the recommendation emails helpful (Q3). 

Eight (of 13) participants indicated that the suggestions were help-ful. DP2, DP6, DP17 particularly liked the explanation of paper relevance as they told them what to pay attention to. DP15 spot a few papers that were useful as a citation in their related work. Some participants with no logged click from the email said that they did read the emails (DP7, DP21). DP7 noted that the recommendations were more “directly relevant” than another paper recommendation service they were using. Deep research system was able to find more relevant papers compared to citation- and embedding-based approaches [ 28 ]. DP21 also said that they found the recommen-dations from leveraging the deep research system “starting to be genuinely useful.” These results shows high opportunities in lever-aging existing deep research system for finding highly relevant paper recommendations, combining richer context in an interest document to generate better contextualized explanations. 

3.3.2 Participants wanted actionable insights for their active projects over general interests (Q1). We collected feedback from six partici-pants with low engagement. Participants mentioned that they had read the emails and found the recommendations to be generally interesting, but did not take any actions because they were also not “super relevant” to the problem they are solving with their active projects (DP23, DP26). Contrastly, participants who engaged deeply with the technology probe pointed to how their recommendations were tied to their ongoing projects. Several participants described two tiers of relevance: papers that are “super relevant” to a current project—which they would add back to their document—and papers of general interest that they might skim or ignore (DP1, DP26). Some participants (10 out of these 14 that edited their documents) organized their research interests document using active projects on top of topical areas (DP1). DP1 specifically wanted a “scoop alert” for new papers that could affect their current project. Further, beyond discovering relevant papers, participants wanted the system to deliver information they could act upon. The expla-nation from the probe did not tell them what to do with the infor-mation and could have missed important details that they would be interested in at the time. DP7 wanted something “specific and actionable” that could enable them to “close the decision loop”. For example when they would like to see baseline candidates during their experimental design phase. DP23’s comment reflected similar sentiment: “[I want the system to tell] me some information on how to proceed on the project that I am working on.” These results suggest participants desire literature-based support to provide actionable insights over information that are of high-level interests. 

3.3.3 Maintaining a dedicated research interests document is burden-some (Q2). We used Google Doc as the primary means for users to communicate their interests to the system. Participants were split: half of the 28 participants edited their research interests document during the 8-week deployment. Some participants found having an ongoing research interests document helpful for “organizing ongoing thoughts and threads” (DP2) or maintaining related work (DP20). Others, like DP23 stated that they were “too busy and unor-ganized to put things from [their] head to a maintained document.” DP17 noted that “it’s a hassle to go back and update the doc.” Leveraging recent publications to improve cold-start was also insufficient. Multiple participants removed them because it was an “eclectic collection of things” (DP17) that no longer reflected their shifting interests (DP3). These observations suggest that while a dedicated document can be valuable, expecting researchers to maintain one specifically for the sake of getting recommendations is unrealistic. One opportunity emerged from the interviews: the system could draw context from documents researchers already maintain, such as project briefs, weekly meeting slides, or shared notes (DP3, DP7). DP7 suggested that for meeting logs the system should “just look at the recent one,” highlighting that the system should handle doc-ument staleness gracefully rather than depending on the user’s manual curation. 

# 3.4 Design goals 

Based on insights from the formative study, we formulate the design goals for our proactive literature support system: 

• DG1 : Help users discover literature relevant to their active research projects instead of general interests. 

• DG2 : Suggest timely and actionable suggestions instead of individual paper recommendations. 

• DG3 : Leverage existing project documents so that users do not need to spend additional effort curating their interests. 

# 4 Omakase 

Guided by our design goals, we developed Omakase , a proactive agent that monitors researchers’ project documents and delivers ac-tionable, literature-based suggestions contextualized to the project. Instead of asking users to constantly curate up-to-date context, 

Omakase uses existing research documents that researchers al-ready maintain —such as meeting notes or paper drafts (DG3). It then infers the project’s specific information needs to surface rele-vant suggestions (DG1) that are timely and actionable (DG2).

# 4.1 Example User Scenario 

4.1.1 Project creation and document upload. An HCI researcher starts a new research project on “multi-agent ideation” with a few research collaborators in her organization. She has already done some literature review in the space but still wants to keep an eye open for relevant work specifically for this project. She adds her Google Doc to a new project on Omakase , and grants it read permission.

Omakase correctly inferred information about the project. She opens the Project details page (Fig. 6). # 4.2 Generation Pipeline Iteration 1 

Once a project document was added to Omakase , it took the follow-ing steps to generate actionable suggestions based on the project context: 

Step 1: Document Preprocessing. Omakase retrieved a document (supported document types are Google Doc, Google Sheet, and Google Slides) from the link provided by the user. It parsed the document into markdown format and extracted a list of potential papers mentioned in the document (Appendix D.2). It then retrieved the potential papers metadata (full title) and filtered only those with corresponding papers. Omakase inserted the papers’ metadata (Semantic Scholar corpus id and title) to the parsed document. 

Step 2: Infer project state and generate questions. From the docu-ment with papers’ metadata, Omakase inferred the project state with reasoning. It also extracts relevant questions that could pro-vide information useful for the researcher in the current stage of the project. The prompt provides descriptions of questions a user might be interested in at different stages. It also specifies the model to rank the questions based on relevance and timeliness (Appendix D.3). 

Step 3: Issue questions to a deep research system. Omakase took top questions from the previous step and issued each question to a deep research system [ 49 ]. Once the answers were all retrieved the system forwarded the answers to the next step in the pipeline. 

Step 4: Generate contextualized suggestions. Lastly, the sugges-tions were extracted from deep research responses and within the same prompt ranked by expected usefulness, importance, and rel-evance to the project phase (Appendix D.4). Each suggestion con-sisted of a text description, a list of mentioned papers, and relevant information from the paper. 

# 4.3 Generation Pipeline Iteration 1 Evaluation 

To evaluate our initial pipeline, we conducted a study where re-searchers evaluated the pipeline outputs of their actual project documents. We also gathered researchers’ opinions on the cases where project-targeted actionable suggestions were helpful. 

4.3.1 Participants. We asked 10 research scientists (PP1–PP10) associated with our institution to participate in our study. The participants have different levels of research experience: 2 Ph.D. students, 2 post-doctoral researchers, 1 university professor, and 5 industrial researchers. Their research areas are HCI, NLP, and ML. Each participant provided documents from their ongoing research projects as input to evaluate the pipeline. Seven of the participants also participated in the recommendation emails study. 

4.3.2 Procedure. We collected 12 research documents from 11 projects. Seven documents were planning and presentation docu-ments that got updated regularly (e.g., for weekly meetings). Two were paper drafts in LaTeX. Three were project proposals or sketches. For each document, we generated the outputs with our pipeline. Each output contained the inferred project state, rationale for the project state inference, and a list of 3-11 questions with 3-8 sug-gestions per questions. We saved the output of the pipeline in a separate document. Participants were provided with output document(s) associated with their project(s). If a participant belonged to multiple research projects on our list, they could give feedback on as many documents as they liked. We asked participants to spend 15-20 minutes reading through the suggestions and provided feedback on the pipeline output and their preferences on receiving updates from the system (see Appendix F for the full questions list). 

4.3.3 Findings. 

The inferred project state is generally correct. In general, our pipeline inferred the correct state from regularly updated project documents (e.g., meeting notes or weekly presentations) or project proposal in the beginning state. Six researchers indicated that the inferred project state were correct while four others stated the project state were "close to correct" (PP1) or "Not perfect, but pretty good" (PP4). Two participants (PP8, PP10) in particular added paper drafts instead of project documents to Omakase also noted that the inferred project states to be generally correct. 

Project document-grounded questions and suggestions offers useful information beyond paper recommendations. In general, participants found useful information in generated suggestions. For example, PP1 found some "really good ideas or suggestions and worth taking a look at". Some also found the suggestions surfaces things they "might not have thought about" even after completing their own literature review (PP2). PP2 and PP8 commented that these ques-tions and suggestions could surface blindspots in the project. In general, participants seeing suggestions based on "[their] meeting notes and researchers notes... could be useful" (PP10). Some participants noted that the suggestions are more specific and better contextualized than paper recommendations feed, help-ing them to "know what to pay attention to" (PP6). PP9 commented that the suggestions are not redundant with the existing recom-mendations. While PP5 "like the document/project-specific nature of this [approach]". 

Researchers want control over the generation and delivery of sug-gestions. Participants wanted to steer the output by getting more control of the generation process or giving feedback to the system. For example, PP7 wanted to be able to select the questions before issuing them to a deep research system. Participants in general wanted to be able to add their own questions (PP5, PP7, PP8). PP1 wanted to give feedback to the system on the quality of the sug-gestions. PP3 said that they wanted to edit inferred information and even provided more information to steer the output to fit their needs as their "project doc was not the most comprehensive... it’s kind of impossible for the system to know what [they were] work-ing on" (PP3). Unlike real collaborators, the system could not have had background knowledge from past in-person discussion. Five participants commented that the amount of information presented was too much to view at once. None of them finished reading all suggestions in allotted time. For example, PP7 only got through 2 questions out of 7. However, some had higher informa-tion overload threshold than others. overwhelming". On the other hand, PP8 said they were fine with the current amount of suggestions as long as they were diverse enough. Participants also expressed that they wanted control over the timing of suggestion delivery. While some participants would like to receive the suggestions weekly (PP7) or bi-weekly (PP2, PP4), some preferred suggestions being delivered on demand (PP3, PP8, PP9). Some were also interested in the system delivering sugges-tions as the user makes edits or "when something changes in the environment that affects [their] project" (e.g., new relevant papers got published) (PP8). The preferred timing varied — and even contradicted. Some wanted suggestions early during brainstorming (PP3), while oth-ers explicitly did not want them during ideation when they were already compiling information (PP10). PP10 stated they could "get excited about" receiving suggestions during paper write-up instead. Similarly, some wanted more suggestions at later stages (PP9), while others valued them most during active thinking or paper writing (PP7). This suggests that delivery timing cannot be one-size-fits-all and likely needs to adapt to both project phase and individual preference. 

Other improvement opportunities. Beyond already-mentioned issues, we identified the following opportunities to improve the pipeline and eventual system design: 

• Better output generation. Some (PP3, PP6, PP7) noted that the questions and suggestions, while generally helpful, sometimes had convoluted phrasing that were hard to parse ("word salad", PP6). PP5 commented that some generated questions should be broken down into sub-questions. This is a common issue with LLM text generation addressable with tighter prompting and post-processing. 

• Awareness of the time of generation. Some participants wanted to put higher weight on suggestions related to recent actions. They flagged that the output suggestions showed that Omakase did not know that some tasks had already been completed or discarded as directions changed. PP3 noted that the pipeline should be able to infer that their formative had already concluded after 2 months "un-less I’m that slow" (PP3). PP7 also noted that the output over-indexed on an abandoned early direction. 

• Suggestions needed to be properly contextualized. PP3 and PP5 wished the suggestions were more contextualized. Particularly, PP3 wanted the system to explain "where the suggestions were derived". While some other wanted the ex-planation next to the relevant sections to its corresponding original project documents (e.g., as a comment). 

• Make sure suggestions are non-redundant. Some sug-gestions can be redundant with what they already know (PP2), redundant with other suggestions (PP8) or what they are already planning to do (PP3, PP7). While some take this as a signal of knowing the pipeline could be useful, it could be annoying. Further improvement could focus on ensuring all suggestions do not repeat what was already planned in the documents, selecting a set of diverse suggestions and giving users a lightweight way to tell the system what they already know or are planning to do to avoid redundancy. In summary, the evaluation showed that participants were inter-ested in receiving project-document-based suggestions, considered them useful, specific and actionable. The pipeline could infer project state and information need (questions) from active documents that reflected the current state of the project. Participants stated that they want more control over the generation and delivery of sugges-tions. We took their feedback to improve the pipeline and design of Omakase .

# 4.4 Generation Pipeline Iteration 2 

Based on the findings above, we made the following improvements to both the generation pipeline and the interface of the final system. 

4.4.1 Pipeline improvements. Based on the findings from the pipeline evaluation (Section 4.3), we made several improvements to the suggestion generation pipeline addressing the four key areas par-ticipants identified: time awareness, output quality, information overload, and redundancy. The final pipeline of Omakase uses Opus 4.6 for all LLM requests in the pipeline (Appendix C). 

Time-aware generation. PP3 and PP7 noted that the initial pipeline sometimes generated suggestions that overindexed past informa-tion. We traced part of this to a document parser bug that dropped date information, causing the model to treat all content as cur-rent. After fixing the parser, we also instruct the model to discard open loops that have had no follow-up activity for several weeks (Appendix C.4). 

Improved output clarity. Participants reported that some gen-erated questions and suggestions had convoluted phrasing (PP3, PP6, PP7) and that compound questions should be broken into sub-questions (PP5). We updated the underlying model from O3 to Opus 4.6, which produced clearer and more concise text. We also refined the question generation prompt to avoid complicated questions (Appendix C.4). 

Ranking and selection to manage information overload. None of the participants in the pipeline evaluation finished reading all gen-erated suggestions within the allotted time, and five explicitly com-mented that the volume was overwhelming. To address this, we added two explicit ranking stages to the pipeline. First, we added a question selection step that selects a diverse subset of the top ques-tions (Appendix C.5). Second, we added a suggestion selection step that selects the top suggestions based on relevance, actionability, and diversity (Appendix C.7). 

Non-redundancy. Participants noted that some suggestions re-peated what they already knew or planned to do (PP2, PP3, PP7), or overlapped with other suggestions (PP8). The suggestion ranking prompt (Appendix C.7) now explicitly instructs the model to penal-ize suggestions that are redundant with the document’s existing plans or with other selected suggestions. Additionally, the system tracks which suggestions a user has previously seen and excludes them from future batches. 

4.4.2 Document viewer. To better contextualized the suggestions (PP3, PP5), Omakase identifies which part of the document is most 

> 8Omakase : proactive assistance with actionable suggestions for evolving scientific research projects

relevant to a suggestion (Appendix C.8). The user can view docu-ment section relevant to a suggestion by clicking on the suggestion in the document view (Fig. 1B) to see relevant highlighted text. 

4.4.3 Updates mechanism and control. To send participants proac-tive updates, a background process runs at a configurable interval. For each project, it compares the linked document’s last-modified timestamp and the current inferred project state against those recorded at the last pipeline run. If the document has changed or the project state has shifted, the system triggers an update work-flow that infers the project state and generates new questions to issue them together with tracked questions to a deep research sys-tem. The suggestion generation for new questions went as usual. For tracked questions, Omakase compares the new answer with the old answer and only extracts suggestions if there are substantial differences (Appendix C.9). 

Omakase gives user control over the timing of update delivery. User can specify how frequent they want Omakase to check for update (from daily to never) (Fig. 6). They can also manually request new suggestions. 

# 5 Omakase ’s evaluation 

We conducted a controlled evaluation of Omakase ’s Generation Pipeline Iteration 2, focusing on the quality of the resulting sugges-tions, the timeliness of the generated questions and the overall user experience using Omakase ’s interface. We asked the following: 

• RQ1 : Can Omakase understand the document to generate timely questions that reflect the current state of the project? 

• RQ2 : How relevant, timely, and actionable do researchers find Omakase ’s suggestions compared to directly reading the answers from a deep research system? 

• RQ3 : Does Omakase help participants discover unexpected information needs that they had not considered? 

• RQ4 : How effortful is it to triage the suggestions? For RQ1, we used Omakase to generate two sets of questions: The first set was based on the up-to-date version of participants’ project documents, and the second set was based on an earlier version of the same documents. We then asked participants to compare the two sets of question while being blind to which condition each came from to see if the first set is more timely. For RQ2, we compared 

Omakase ’s suggestions with output of the same deep research report that was used to generate the suggestions, similar to common literature review practices. For RQ3-4, we report qualitative data from post-survey and interview. 

# 5.1 Participants 

We recruited 11 researchers (SP1–SP11) via social media and aca-demic mailing lists. The participants consisted of 8 Ph.D. students, 1 pre-doctoral researcher, 1 post-doctoral researcher, and 1 research scientist. Two participants had 6-10 years of research experience while the rest has 2-5 years of research experience. None of the par-ticipants had participated in the formative study or prior pipeline evaluation. 

# 5.2 Study protocol 

The study consisted of a pre-study preparation step, followed by an asynchronous evaluation of the system’s output and an interview. 

5.2.1 Pre-study: document submissions. Each participant submitted two copies of their documents from an active research project–a current version and a past snapshot from the document’s history. For each version, we generated questions that result in 12 deep search reports and 12 Omakase suggestions. 

5.2.2 Output evaluation. The output evaluation form had three tasks. 

Task 1: Evaluate questions (current vs past). Participants received the shuffled list of 23–30 questions, blinded to conditions. For each question, they specified how much they agreed with the statement 

“This question is relevant to my project and is a priority to address at its current stage.” on a 7-point Likert scale (1=“strongly disagree”, 7=“strongly agree”). If a question is not relevant to project at any point in time, they flagged that question to catch the case where the system just generated random questions. 

Task 2: Evaluate responses ( Omakase ’s suggestions vs deep research answer). Participants evaluated 12 sets of responses to questions generated from their current document. Because answers from a deep research system can be much longer than a suggestion, we asked participants to select two topics (sections) from each answer that they thought would be most useful. Each set of responses there-fore consisted of Omakase ’s suggestions and two selected sections from a deep research answer for the same question. We use these two sections to construct two comparison baselines: Equal effort ,where we compare against the section the participant expected to be most useful, and Double deep research effort , where we com-pare against whichever was rated higher on a given dimension (see below), requiring participants to have read both. Participants evalu-ated each response in randomized order, blind to condition, rating their agreement on a 7-point Likert scale (1=“strongly disagree”, 7=“strongly agree”) on three dimensions— relevance , actionability ,and timeliness :

• Relevance: This response is relevant to my project. 

• Actionability: This response suggests concrete actions I can take (e.g., cite for a claim, adopt a baseline, update a hypothesis). 

• Timeliness: This response is appropriate for my current stage of work. 

Task 3: Survey response. Participants shared their opinions about the questions and the responses in relation to their project. Specifi-cally, we asked if the output helped them think about new ideas or questions (RQ3) and how easy it was to triage the responses (RQ4). See the full list of the survey questions at Appendix G. 

5.2.3 Interview. We conducted a semi-structured interview with participants. We asked follow-up clarification questions from their evaluation responses. Participants were also presented with Omakase 

for their current document. We observed how they interacted with the system and asked for their impressions of the system.

# 6 Discussion and Future Work 6.1 Granting AI agents access to project document 

There are risks in granting an AI agent access to an ongoing research documents and artifacts [ 54 ]. When we were recruiting participants for the system evaluation, some interested researchers mentioned that their organization prohibited them from sharing their research documents externally. DP16 from the formative study said they did not want to grant an AI agent access to their working research document, citing privacy as the main reason. Indeed, privacy is one barrier to a proactive context-aware agent like Apple’s Knowledge Navigator [39]. One common feature suggestion for Omakase is to add the capability for an agent to edit or comment on the document directly. However, users might have different preferences in giving comment or write access to their project document to an AI agent and the preferences also depend on the usage context. For example, a user can feel comfortable having an agent editing their document to add a paper to a literature review list but not editing their paper draft. More investigation is needed to understand how to manage AI agents’ access to different artifacts in various use cases. 

# 6.2 Multiple sources of project context 

In this paper, we only explored project context from a single docu-ment as an initial exploration step. In practice, a research project can have many artifacts (e.g., project briefs, weekly presentations, paper drafts, experimental results, code, etc.). Multiple sources of 

> 11

Siangliulue et al. 

project context can give an agent a more complete picture but in-crease complexity. Future work can explore how AI agents reason about what the user needs from multiple, potentially conflicting, sources. 

# 6.3 Over-reliance on AI 

Omakase ’s suggestions are designed to be easy to triage. Mean-while, there is concern that using LLM tools could lead to overre-liance on AI [ 54 ]. Prior work has shown that AI models are often perceived as unbiased entities [ 11 ]. The risk is that researchers could accept suggestions from the system without verifying them. One way to mitigate this is by providing evidence links to AI-generated text, making it easier to verify [27]. 

# 7 Limitations 

While Omakase was designed for long-horizon interactions over the course of a project’s lifetime, our evaluation of the system did not cover this aspect. Our user study’s attempt to address this issue with two different versions of the document had limited implica-tions. A longer deployment study could provide a more complete picture about the effectiveness of the system. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We presented Omakase , a proactive research assistant that monitors a researcher’s evolving project documents and generates literature-grounded, actionable suggestions without requiring explicit queries or manual context curation. Our evaluations showed that Omakase 

generated suggestions that were significantly more relevant, ac-tionable, and timely than reading equivalent sections of a literature deep research output. Participants also reported that the system sur-faced new ideas, relevant communities, and potential weaknesses they had not previously considered, while remaining easy to triage. These findings demonstrate the viability of leveraging existing project artifacts as living context for proactive AI assistance.
