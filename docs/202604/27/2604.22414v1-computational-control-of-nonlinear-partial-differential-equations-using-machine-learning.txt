COMPUTATIONAL CONTROL OF NONLINEAR PARTIAL DIFFERENTIAL EQUATIONS USING MACHINE LEARNING 

MAXIMILIAN KURBANOV, MINH-NHAT PHUNG, AND MINH-BINH TRAN 

Abstract. The numerical reconstruction of controls for nonlinear partial differential equations remains a challenging and relatively underdeveloped problem, despite the extensive literature on control theory. While recent works have introduced constructive approaches for semilinear wave and heat equations, the design of reliable computational methods for approximating control functions continues to raise significant analytical and numerical difficulties. In this work, we propose a novel framework based on physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) for the approximation of controls in nonlinear PDE settings. We develop an approach that incorporates the governing equations, boundary conditions, and control mechanisms di-rectly into the learning process. In addition, we provide a convergence analysis of the proposed method and support the theoretical findings with numerical experiments demonstrating good performance. The resulting framework offers a flexible computational tool for approximating control func-tions from partial observations and provides a promising direction for the computational treat-ment of control reconstruction problems. Moreover, it can be applied to a broader class of problems, beyond the control of nonlinear PDEs.

1. Introduction 

The control theory for nonlinear partial differential equations has been the subject of sus-tained and intensive investigation over the past several decades; we refer, for instance, to [1, 2, 3] and the extensive bibliography therein. Despite this substantial body of work, the literature remains comparatively sparse regarding the numerical reconstruction of controls for nonlinear systems. In particular, although a variety of controllability results, including local, global, ap-proximate, and null controllability, have been established under diverse structural assumptions, significantly less is understood about how to compute accurate numerical approximations of the control that reliably approximate the underlying control functions. This issue is not merely technical. The intrinsic nonlinearity of the dynamics typically pre-cludes superposition principles and often leads to instabilities or lack of compactness, thereby complicating both analytical and numerical reconstruction procedures. Consequently, the prob-lem of finding reliable and accurate numerical approximations for control reconstruction remains challenging, especially in ensuring stability, consistency, and convergence of the proposed meth-ods within nonlinear regimes. Such a problem appears to have been investigated only relatively recently, starting with the initial contribution [4]. Since then, it has begun to develop as an emerging research direction, although it remains far from being fully established and is still comparatively unexplored. In particular, constructive and least-squares approaches have been proposed for semilinear wave and heat equations, including both distributed and boundary control problems, as well as space‚Äď time methods for the approximation of controls; see [5, 6, 7, 8, 9]. These contributions underline both the difficulty and the importance of obtaining precise numerical approximations of the controls from a computational perspective in nonlinear PDE settings. It is also worth noting that classical numerical methods for PDEs may face increasing com-putational challenges in high-dimensional settings, a phenomenon commonly referred to as the curse of dimensionality. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) are neural-network-based methods for solving differential equations by incorporating the governing equations into the training loss. Intro-duced in [10, 11, 12], PINNs approximate the solution with a neural network trained using data, boundary conditions, and PDE residuals computed via automatic differentiation. They have become a central tool in scientific machine learning, with applications to forward and inverse problems, particularly in data-sparse settings [12]. From a computational perspective, significant efforts have focused on improving robustness and accuracy, addressing issues such as optimization difficulties and loss imbalance [13]. Notable developments include conservative formulations, domain decomposition methods, and adaptive strategies [14, 15, 16]. On the the-oretical side, convergence and generalization properties have begun to be understood for certain classes of PDEs [17, 18]. We also refer to the recent surveys [19, 20] for further developments and perspectives. Although PINN-based methodologies have recently been applied to optimal deterministic control problems [21, 22, 23, 24] as well as stochastic control settings [25, 26], our objective here is to develop a new PINN-based approach, referred to as WeightedPINNs , tailored to the MACHINE LEARNING FOR CONTROL PDES 3

computation of reliable and accurate numerical approximations for the aforementioned con-trol reconstruction problem (initiated in [4]). In particular, such approaches are well suited to high-dimensional control PDEs, where classical numerical methods often encounter significant computational challenges associated with the curse of dimensionality. The central idea is to introduce adaptive weights directly into the PINN formulation. The WeightedPINN approach provides several structural improvements over standard PINN methodologies. These advan-tages stem from incorporating weighting functions at the level of the differential operator and boundary terms, rather than treating them as fixed coefficients in the loss function (see also Subsection 2.3 for an illustration with explanation). 

(i) Adaptive balancing of differential operators. In standard PINNs, all terms of the PDE residual are treated uniformly, which often leads to imbalance during training, particularly for nonlinear or stiff problems. In contrast, WeightedPINN introduces space‚Äďtime dependent weights acting multiplicatively on each operator component. This enables dynamic rebalanc-ing between the time derivative, diffusion, nonlinearity, and control terms, thereby mitigating gradient imbalance and improving both stability and efficiency of the optimization. 

(ii) Space‚Äďtime localization. Since the weights depend on space and time, the method can automatically emphasize regions where the approximation error is larger or where the solution exhibits complex behavior, such as near the control region or nonlinear transition zones. This induces a natural localization mechanism that is absent in standard PINNs and is particularly beneficial for control reconstruction. 

(iii) Min‚Äďmax structure and robustness. The formulation can be viewed as a min‚Äďmax problem in which the weights act as adversarial variables, amplifying residuals in poorly approximated regions, while the primary network minimizes these amplified errors. This leads to a robust training procedure that avoids fitting only averaged residuals and instead promotes a more uniform accuracy across the domain. 

(iv) Controlled deviation from the original model. An additional neural penalty enforces the weights to remain close to unity, ensuring that the learned operator does not deviate significantly from the original PDE. This prevents degenerate behaviors and provides a balance between flexibility and fidelity to the physical model. 

(v) Balanced treatment of constraints. The weighted formulation extends naturally to boundary, initial, and terminal conditions, allowing each component, including derivatives, to be scaled in-dependently. This prevents certain constraints from dominating the loss and ensures a balanced enforcement of all conditions, which is crucial in controllability problems. 

(vi) Implicit preconditioning. From an analytical viewpoint, the weights can be interpreted as a nonlinear preconditioning of the differential operator. This improves the conditioning of the optimization problem and reduces stiffness. Overall, our new WeightedPINN approach combines adaptivity, robustness, and analytical structure, making it well suited for the challenging task of control reconstruction in nonlin-ear PDEs. Moreover, its applicability is not limited to the control of nonlinear PDEs; it can also be used to address a wider range of related problems. The structure of the Weighted-PINN method is introduced in Section 2. Its theoretical analysis is presented in Section 3, and 4 MAXIMILIAN KURBANOV, MINH-NHAT PHUNG, AND MINH-BINH TRAN 

numerical results are reported in Section 4. In particular, the experiments in Section 4 demon-strate that WeightedPINN outperforms the standard PINN approach for the control problems considered.
