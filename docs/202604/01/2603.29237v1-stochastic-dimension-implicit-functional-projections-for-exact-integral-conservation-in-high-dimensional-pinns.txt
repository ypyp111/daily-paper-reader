Abstract. Enforcing exact macroscopic conservation laws, such as total mass and energy, in continuous neural partial differential equation (PDE) solvers poses significant computational chal-lenges, particularly in high-dimensional domains. First, explicit discrete projections for global spatial integrals typically require deterministic quadrature over uniform grids, which scales poorly with spa-tial dimension and restricts the mesh-free formulation of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). Second, evaluating high-order spatial differential operators incurs substantial memory overhead dur-ing reverse-mode automatic differentiation. Furthermore, generic implicit optimization layers often lack theoretical convergence guarantees when applied to the non-convex manifolds associated with quadratic energy conservation. To address these issues, we propose the Stochastic Dimension Implicit Functional Projection (SDIFP) framework. Rather than explicitly projecting discrete spatial vec-tors, SDIFP applies a global affine transformation directly to the continuous network output. This formulation yields closed-form algebraic solutions for non-convex integral constraints using detached Monte Carlo (MC) quadrature, thereby bypassing spatial grid dependencies. For scalable backprop-agation, we introduce a doubly-stochastic unbiased gradient estimator (DS-UGE). By decoupling the spatial mini-batch sampling used for implicit functional gradients from the stochastic subsampling of the differential operator terms, the DS-UGE bounds the optimization memory complexity from 

O(M × NL) to O(N × |I| ). Consequently, SDIFP mitigates overfitting to mini-batch sampling vari-ance, preserves the mathematical regularity of the approximated solutions, and maintains standard 

O(1) point-wise inference efficiency, providing a scalable and mesh-free approach for solving exactly conservative high-dimensional PDEs. 

Key words. Physics-informed neural networks, exact conservation, dual curse of dimensionality, implicit functional projection, non-convex constraints.

1. Introduction. Recently, neural network-based approaches have emerged as a promising computational paradigm for solving and modeling partial differential equa-tions (PDEs) [5, 30]. Among these, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) [43] have gained widespread adoption by parameterizing the PDE solution continuously, requiring minimal labeled data, and operating entirely in a mesh-free manner. Con-ventionally, the loss function of a PINN enforces the residuals of the governing PDEs alongside initial and boundary conditions. This introduces a soft constraint on the physical formulation, penalizing predictions that deviate from the underlying equa-tions. While this soft penalty affords necessary flexibility during optimization, it fun-damentally permits the learned continuous approximations to violate strict physical principles [44]. In many complex physical and engineering systems, exact adherence to macro-scopic conservation laws—such as the conservation of mass, momentum, and energy— is not merely a desirable regularization technique, but a strict prerequisite for physical fidelity and long-term numerical stability. Treating these global integral quantities as soft constraints inherently alters the solution space regardless of penalty weights, fre-quently leading to nonphysical artifacts such as artificial dissipation or spurious energy accumulation. Consequently, there is a pressing need to impose hard constraints that mathematically guarantee the neural network outputs obey global integral invariants by design, independent of the optimization dynamics or data distribution.

Enforcing exact global integral constraints within a continuous, mesh-free deep learning framework, however, presents significant mathematical and computational challenges. Recent efforts, such as PINN-proj [2], attempt to guarantee integral con-servation by analytically projecting the network’s discrete spatial outputs onto a con-servation manifold. Yet, evaluating these global integrals analytically forces such methods to rely on deterministic quadrature rules over fixed, uniform spatial meshes [29]. This compromises the elegant mesh-free nature of PINNs and exacerbates the curse of dimensionality for high-dimensional PDEs. Furthermore, replacing uniform grids with stochastic Monte Carlo (MC) collocation points introduces a fundamental statistical inconsistency: forcing a randomly sampled mini-batch to exactly satisfy a macroscopic global constant coerces the neural network to overfit the statistical sam-pling variance. This inadvertently induces high-frequency spurious spatial oscillations, severely degrading the regularity of the approximated PDE solution. As an alternative to explicitly discrete projections, state-of-the-art differentiable optimization layers, such as Πnet [21], leverage operator splitting algorithms (e.g., Douglas-Rachford) [12] and the implicit function theorem (IFT) [13] to project neural network outputs onto feasible sets. While highly effective for generic parametric constrained optimization, these implicit solvers rigorously require the constraint sets to be convex. In physical systems, however, energy conservation frequently entails a global quadratic integral (e.g., R

u2 dx = C), which topologically defines a hyper-ellipsoid—a strictly non-convex manifold. Applying standard operator splitting to such non-convex integral constraints generally leads to algorithmic divergence and lacks theoretical convergence guarantees. Moreover, performing point-wise discrete projections tightly couples all spatial collocation points during backpropagation [19], which inflates computational complexity and negates the inherent O(1) point-wise evaluation efficiency of standard PINNs. Scaling exactly conservative neural PDE solvers to high dimensions also creates a computational bottleneck in evaluating high-order spatial differential operators. The memory footprint required for reverse-mode automatic differentiation across all spa-tial dimensions scales prohibitively [55], frequently causing out-of-memory failures on modern accelerators even for minimal batch sizes. Several efforts have been proposed to mitigate this differential curse of dimensionality. Stochastic Dimension Gradient Descent (SDGD) [24] randomizes over the input dimensions where in each iteration, the partial derivatives are only calculated for a minibatch of sampled dimensions with back-propagation. In [25, 32, 27], the classical technique of Hutchinson Trace Es-timator (HTE) [28] is used to estimate the trace of Hessian or Jacobian to inputs. Others choose to bypass AD completely to reduce the complexity of computation. In [39], the finite difference method is used for estimating the Hessian trace. Ran-domized smoothing [23, 26] uses the expectation over Gaussian random variable as ansatz, so that its derivatives can be expressed as another expectation Gaussian ran-dom variable via Stein’s identity [48]. More recently, Stochastic Taylor Derivative Estimator (STDE) [46] was proposed to efficiently amortize arbitrary differential op-erators, though its accuracy still depends on discretization or smoothing parameters. Nevertheless, none of these methods possess inherent mechanisms to enforce exact global physical invariants such as integral conservation laws. To fundamentally resolve these theoretical bottlenecks and simultaneously over-come the dual curse of dimensionality (grid-dependent integral projections and memory-intensive differential operators), we propose the Stochastic Dimension Implicit Functional 

Projection ( SDIFP ) framework. Instead of projecting a high-dimensional discrete vector of spatial evaluations, SDIFP projects the continuous functional parameters STOCHASTIC DIMENSION IMPLICIT FUNCTIONAL PROJECTIONS 3via a global affine transformation ˜ u(x) = α · uraw (x; θ) + β. This paradigm shift decouples the spatial complexity from the constraint satisfaction verification, reduc-ing an otherwise intractable infinite-dimensional non-convex projection into a robust, low-dimensional algebraic root-finding system. Concurrently, to evaluate the govern-ing equations efficiently, this continuous projection is seamlessly synergized with a stochastic dimension-sampling mechanism. Our primary contributions are summarized as follows: 

• Exact mesh-free conservation via detached MC quadrature: We evaluate global integrals utilizing large-scale Monte Carlo (MC) sequences strictly detached from the automatic differentiation (AD) computational graph. This mitigates the integral curse of dimensionality associated with grid-based quadrature, bypass-ing the reliance on fixed uniform meshes without incurring prohibitive memory overhead. 

• Analytical resolution of non-convex constraints: By projecting in the affine functional space rather than the discrete Euclidean space, we derive exact, closed-form algebraic solutions for simultaneously conserving linear (mass) and strictly non-convex quadratic (energy) integrals. This effectively circumvents the theoret-ical convexity prerequisites of generic implicit solvers. 

• Double-stochastic unbiased gradient estimator: To backpropagate through both the non-convex projection layer and the high-dimensional differential oper-ators, we formulate an orthogonal dual-decoupling estimator. By combining a spatial mini-batch evaluation for implicit functional gradients with a stochastic sampling of the PDE’s differential dimensions, we reduce the overall optimiza-tion memory complexity from an intractable O(M × d) to an optimally bounded 

O(N × | I|) without sacrificing mathematical unbiasedness. 

• Preservation of solution regularity and O(1) inference: By isolating the global integration evaluation from the localized gradient updates, SDIFP prevents the network from overfitting to mini-batch sampling variance. Furthermore, fixing the scalar projection parameters post-training preserves the canonical point-wise evaluation efficiency of standard PINNs. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 3, we review the theoretical bottlenecks of explicit discrete projections and high-dimensional operators. In Section 4, we detail the proposed SDIFP framework, including the derivation of functional projection roots and the double-stochastic unbiased gradient estimator. In Section 5, we present numerical experiments demonstrating the accuracy, efficiency, and exact conservation properties of the proposed method in high-dimensional sys-tems. Concluding remarks are provided in Section 6. 

2. Related Work. Soft Constraints on PINNs. Soft penalty terms for con-straint violations in the objective function are one approach to incorporate constraints in neural networks [36], and have been adopted to solve parametric constrained op-timization problems [51], and partial differential equations through physics-informed neural networks [15, 43]. A soft constraint can be used to enforce the conservation law in a PINN by adding a loss term that penalizes the difference between the conserved quantity in the current prediction and the ground truth [47]. Training can either be done entirely with this modified loss function [16], or in two stages where the modified loss function is used in a second stage of training [34]. Creating soft constraints in this way can also be used to enforce conservation of flux between two neighboring discrete subdomains [29]. Despite their ability to handle general constraints, these approaches offer no guarantees at inference time. Beside requiring manual tuning of the penalty 4 Z. LIANG 

parameters [42], which is challenging yet critical for good performance, the use of soft constraints is discouraged for the following reasons. First, the structure of the con-straints set can be exploited to design more efficient algorithms; see, e.g., the simplex algorithm [11]. Second, treating constraints softly may significantly alter the problem solution regardless of tuning [22]. Third, certain constrained optimization problems (e.g., linear programs) may not admit a solution at all when constraints are treated softly [3]. 

Hard Constraints on PINNs. Hard constraints have been used in PINNs to strictly enforce initial and boundary conditions [54]. KKT-hPINN uses KKT con-ditions to create an untrainable projection that conserves a desired quantity, but is limited to linear constraints [7]. To circumvent the shortcomings of soft constraints, hard-constrained neural networks aim to enforce constraints on the NN output by design. [18] address linear homogeneous inequality constraints by parameterizing the feasible set. RAYEN [50] enforces convex constraints by scaling the line segment be-tween infeasible points and a fixed point in the feasible set’s interior. These methods enjoy rapid inference but require expensive offline preprocessing and are not applicable to input-dependent constraints. [37] propose a closed-form expression for polyhedral constraints and employ CVXPYlayers [1] for general convex sets. [10] orthogonally project the NN output or intermediate layers using Dykstra’s algorithm [4], but rely on loop unrolling. DC3 [14] introduces an equality completion and inequality correc-tion procedure. [33] impose non-linear equality constraints by recursively linearizing them. Lagrangian and augmented Lagrangian approaches [17, 40] are considered for general non-convex constraints, with drawbacks similar to soft constraints. [31] con-sider a probabilistic sampling approach. LinSATNet [53] imposes non-negative linear constraints, relaxed by GLinSAT [56] which requires bounded constraints [49]. Aprojection-based method applies multiple Newton iterations after each training step to guarantee conservation [6]. AT-PINN-HC [8] introduces hard-constraint strategies with various auxiliary functions (polynomial, trigonometric, exponential, logarithmic) for structural vibration problems, achieving error reductions of one to four orders of magnitude. The HCP-PINN [52] extends hard constraint methods to strongly nonlin-ear PDEs, using a hard constraint projection layer to project solutions to a hyperplane that admits only exact solutions to the governing equations. 

Learning Conservation Laws. Conservation laws can be mapped to hard con-straints by incorporating them directly into the structure of neural networks. The Hamiltonian [20] or Lagrangian [9] describe how total energy is conserved in a sys-tem, and can be parametrized by a neural network so that its quantities are always conserved. Hard constraints can also be created by parameterizing a divergence-free vector field that corresponds to the continuity equation [45]. This divergence-free assumption, however, limits these neural conservation laws to forms of conserved sys-tems, whereas PINNs can solve and model non-conservative systems with sources, sinks, or boundary flux. Neural operators have also been applied to enforce conser-vation using the continuity equation [35], and a differential projection layer can be incorporated to learn the governing PDE and enforce it as a hard constraint [38].

4. Methodology. In this section, we formulate the mathematical framework of the SDIFP. To establish a rigorous foundation, we first critically examine the math-ematical structures of existing constraint enforcement paradigms—namely, explicit STOCHASTIC DIMENSION IMPLICIT FUNCTIONAL PROJECTIONS 7discrete projections (e.g., PINN-proj [2]) and implicit optimization layers (e.g., Πnet [21]). By diagnosing their theoretical bottlenecks regarding grid dependency and topo-logical non-convexity, we motivate a transition from discrete Euclidean projections to continuous affine functional projections. Specifically, rather than constraining a high-dimensional vector of spatial evaluations, SDIFP applies a global affine transformation ˜u(x) = α · uraw (x; θ) + β directly to the network’s continuous outputs. This spatial decoupling reduces an intractable infinite-dimensional non-convex projection into a robust, low-dimensional algebraic root-finding system, providing an exact, mesh-free resolution that cleanly circumvents the dual curse of dimensionality. Furthermore, to ensure scalable backpropagation without degrading solution regularity, this contin-uous projection is seamlessly synergized with a double-stochastic unbiased gradient estimator.

[Compatibility with Boundary Conditions] The fundamental global affine mapping proposed in the SDIFP framework, ˜ u(x, t ) = α(t)uraw (x, t ; θ) + β(t), inher-ently preserves homogeneous Neumann ( ∂n ˜u = α∂ nuraw = 0) and periodic boundary conditions, provided the base network uraw analytically or numerically satisfies them. Extending this continuous functional projection to seam-lessly integrate exact Dirichlet constraints—for instance, by augmenting the ansatz with a spatial distance function or mask, ˜ u(x, t ) = α(t)uraw (x, t ; θ) + β(t)ϕ(x), where

Remark. [Numerical Stability] To prevent numerical singularities (division by zero) during the evaluation of α∗ and its implicit gradients (e.g., when the network ini-tializes to a nearly flat field, yielding σ2(t; θ) → 0), a minor numerical relaxation max( σ2(t; θ), ϵ ) with ϵ = 10 −8 is applied in practical implementations. Building on these scaling diagnostics, the first experiment evaluates the structural robustness of projection methods against spatial discretization topologies. Explicit projections mathematically rely on a constant volumetric element ∆ x, necessitating uniform grids. To expose the grid-dependency paradox, we evaluate the frameworks on a moderate-dimensional (10D) Sine-Gordon equation under two spatial sampling regimes: a deterministic uniform Eulerian grid and a fully unstructured Monte Carlo (MC) random point cloud. The numerical results empirically validate the theoretical bottlenecks diagnosed in Section 3. Even at d = 10, constructing a minimal uniform grid (e.g., 10 points per axis, yielding 10 10 points) for PINN-proj triggers an immediate out-of-memory (OOM) failure, exemplifying the integral curse of dimensionality. When forcibly subjected to random MC sampling to avert memory explosion, PINN-proj’s analytical guarantees completely collapse, resulting in severe conservation violations due to heterogeneous volumetric weights and degraded solution accuracy due to statistical variance over-22 Z. LIANG 

fitting. Furthermore, soft constraints (PINN-SC) persistently violate the physical invariants regardless of the sampling strategy, yielding macroscopic errors bounded between 10 −3 and 10 −1.In profound contrast, SDIFP successfully decouples the spatial geometry from the constraint satisfaction. Utilizing detached MC quadrature, SDIFP robustly enforces both linear and non-convex quadratic integral constraints up to single-precision ma-chine epsilon ( ∼ O (10 −6)) under entirely unstructured random MC sampling, thus establishing an unconditionally mesh-free exact conservation paradigm. By physically isolating the macroscopic constraints from the local stochastic iterations, SDIFP pre-vents the network from overfitting to localized sampling variance, consistently yielding the lowest continuous solution errors. 

5.4. Scalability to high-dimensional problems. Having established exact and mesh-free integral conservation, we proceed to evaluate SDIFP’s scalability against the differential curse of dimensionality in ultra-high-dimensional settings. We focus on the highly anisotropic Sine-Gordon equation, aiming to simultaneously enforce the exact conservation of the global energy and solve the complex PDE. 

6. Conclusions. This paper proposes SDIFP, a framework that simultaneously addresses the mesh-dependency bottleneck and the memory scalability barrier in ex-actly conservative neural PDE solvers. Existing approaches to exact invariant enforce-ment fall into two categories: methods that rely on fixed uniform grids and therefore lose the mesh-free property of PINNs, and stochastic sampling methods that lack structural mechanisms for strict conservation and resort to soft penalty regulariza-tion. SDIFP overcomes these limitations through two key innovations. First, by refor-mulating the projection as a continuous affine transformation of the network output, the enforcement of nonlinear integral invariants reduces to solving a low-dimensional algebraic system for the scalar coefficients α∗ and β∗, completely decoupling the con-straint from the spatial discretization. This enables the use of detached quasi-Monte Carlo quadrature, achieving exact conservation on arbitrary unstructured point sets with no mesh requirement. Second, to mitigate the O(M × NL) memory cost of backpropagating through large differential operators, the DS-UGE estimator is intro-duced. By subsampling the operator index set into disjoint forward and backward subsets J and I, the reverse-mode AD graph is drastically truncated, reducing the overall memory complexity to O(max( |I|, |J|) · |B res |). Combined with detached MC quadrature, this allows exactly conservative PINNs to scale to 100 , 000-dimensional nonlinear systems on a single GPU. Future work includes extending the functional projection framework to handle localized point-wise constraints such as entropy inequalities, and integrating the DS-UGE estimator into neural operator architectures for learning exactly conservative infinite-dimensional operators.
