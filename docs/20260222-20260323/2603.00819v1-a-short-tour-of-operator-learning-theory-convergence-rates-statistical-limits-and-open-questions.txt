# A short tour of operator learning theory: Convergence rates, statistical limits, and open questions 

Simone Brugiapaglia [0000 −0003 −1927 −8232 ] ,Nicola Rares Franco [0000 −0002 −4980 −5606 ] ,Nicholas H. Nelsen [0000 −0002 −8328 −1199 ]

Abstract This paper surveys recent developments at the intersection of operator learning, statistical learning theory, and approximation theory. First, it reviews error bounds for empirical risk minimization with a focus on holomorphic operators and neural network approximations. Next, it illustrates fundamental performance limits in terms of sample size by adopting a minimax perspective and considering various notions of regularity beyond holomorphy. The paper ends with a discussion on the interplay between these two perspectives and related open questions. 

Key words: operator learning, empirical risk minimization, minimax rates, neural networks, error bounds 

## 1 Introduction 

Since their first introduction, our theoretical understanding of deep neural networks and related architectures has increased remarkably. Already in the 1990s, Cybenko [12] and Hornik [19] proved several foundational results, referred to as universal approximation 

theorems, which established the density of neural networks in classical function spaces such as 𝐶 𝑘 and 𝐿 𝑝 spaces. Relatedly, Chen and Chen [9] extended these arguments to the case of nonlinear operators between function spaces by introducing a neural network-based architecture later referred to as DeepONet [27]. These findings about existence [23] were later refined with results on model ex-pressivity . This established a connection between architectural complexity and model

Outline. The paper is organized as follows. §2 presents two sample complexity results for supervised training of neural operators using empirical risk minimization (ERM) [18], whereas §3 comments on the optimality of these results from the minimax per-spective [22, Chp. 9]. §4 summarizes the findings and collects open problems. 

## 2 Error bounds for empirical risk minimization 

In this section, we overview two recent results on operator learning that focus on ERM via deep learning, namely [35] and [4]. Both contributions address the case of holomorphic operators, thus requiring strong regularity assumptions. This choice is supported by several reasons. First, holomorphy emerges naturally in the study of solution operators of parameter-dependent partial differential equations (PDEs) [10, 11], where operator learning plays a central role. Second, the rich mathematical structure of holomorphic operators enables a more refined analysis of both analytical and statistical aspects, offering a strong foundation for future research. In this regard, it is noteworthy that the learning problem associated with holomorphic operators can be approached using quite different mathematical techniques; as we shall see, the two contributions summarized hereafter provide a clear illustration of such versatility. The former, [35], leverages the theory of empirical processes , whereas the latter, [4], is grounded in the theory of compressed sensing .For ease of exposition and in an attempt to unify the setting and notation, we adopt a different presentation of the results than the one by the original authors. Clearly, this process inevitably results in a partial loss of generality. The interested reader is invited to consult the original works for additional details and more general formulations of the results showcased here. Throughout §2, we consider the following assumptions.

Discussion. We conclude this section by illustrating some key differences between Thms. 1 and 2. To start, Thm. 1 considers the case of data perturbed by i.i.d.—potentially unbounded—statistical noise, whereas Thm. 2 focuses on bounded perturbations. Con-sequently, the two theorems end up establishing different error rates. Specifically, in the first result a nonparametric rate is obtained that is at best of order 𝑛 −1/2. In the second result, a faster-than-Monte Carlo rate is possible when 𝛾 and 𝜈 in (6a)–(6b) are large enough and the noise magnitude satsifies 𝜎 ≤ 𝑐 ˜𝑛 − ( 1/ 𝑝 −1/2) . Moreover, in contrast to Thm. 1, Thm. 2 establishes a result in “high probability.” This means that the probabil-ity of the event that the error bound holds is arbitrarily high but fixed (e.g., 0 .99). The dependence of the bounds on such constants is explicitly tracked in [4]. Another key difference concerns the network class 𝒩 . In Thm. 1, 𝒩 contains fully trainable, unconstrained MLPs. In contrast, the class 𝒩 in Thm. 2 consists of “handcrafted” architectures. We note that this gap is partially addressed by [4, Thm. 3.2], which establishes the existence of uncountably many minimizers able to achieve error bounds analogous to (7) when 𝒩 contains fully trainable MLPs. Finally, the two classes of holomorphic operators considered in Thms. 1 and 2 differ. In Thm. 1, it is sufficient for 𝒢 ◦ ℰ−1

## 4 Conclusions and open problems 

This paper reviews recent progress in the theory of operator learning by presenting two convergence analyses of ERM for holomorphic operators (Thms. 1 and 2 in §2) and by surveying multiple results about minimax sample complexity rates (Thms. 3–6 in §3). Several open problems naturally arise from this paper. Based on the results presented in §2, it is natural to ask whether faster-than-Monte Carlo rates are possible when considering ERM with fully trainable networks , especially in the absence of noise. Currently, an answer is elusive because the arguments used to prove Thms. 1 and 2 do not easily extend to such a setting. In fact, the technical approach underlying Thm. 1 is likely to be suboptimal when 𝜎 = 0; conversely, the construction considered in Thm. 2 is still not general enough to accommodate fully trainable architectures. Notably, these issues are not intrinsic to operator learning, but rather constitute an open question even in finite dimensions. A remaining question is whether the nonparametric rate in Thm. 1 (which is at best 𝑛 −1/2) is an artifact of the statistical learning analysis in the noisy setting, or a genuine minimax barrier. One interesting future research direction in minimax analysis concerns the random noise setting from §3. For classical function approximation, it is known that 𝑠 𝑛 (𝐾 )𝑋 

from (8) and ˜ 𝑠 𝜂 𝑛 (𝐾 ; 𝜎 )𝑋 from (11) behave quite differently as a function of 𝑛 , although they do agree in the limit 𝜎 → 0 [14]. An open problem is to obtain a similar noise level aware characterization of minimax rates for operator learning. Another important challenge is to identify classes of operators that are practically relevant in scientific applications, but still enjoy algebraic minimax sample complexity. Collectively, these considerations open up several promising research directions at the interface of the topics in §2 and §3. Moreover, is ERM optimal in the minimax sense of (8) for target classes beyond holomorphic operators? Other sets of interest could include 𝐶 ∞ operators or those arising from specific parametric PDE problems. In addition, as suggested by the results in §3, an interesting alternative could be to consider architecture-oriented operator classes, in contrast to those based on more classical regularity assumptions.
