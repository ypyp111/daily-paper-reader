Abstract 

We present methods for constructing Taylor series surrogate mod-els for covariance preconditioned high dimensional mappings that de-pend implicitly on the solution of a system of nonlinear equations, e.g., the solution of a partial differential equation. Taylor series are tradi-tionally considered intractable for such mappings because the deriva-tive tensors are enormous, and are only accessible through ‚Äúprobing‚ÄĚ (contraction of the tensor with vectors in all but one index). We overcome these challenges using a ‚ÄúTucker tensor train Taylor series‚ÄĚ (T4S) surrogate model, in which each derivative tensor is approxi-mated by a Tucker decomposition composed with a tensor train. After an initial dimension reduction, Tucker tensor trains are fit to direction-ally symmetric tensor probes using Riemannian manifold optimization within a rank continuation scheme. The optimization is enabled by fast sweeping methods for applying the Riemannian Jacobian (the Ja-cobian for the Tucker tensor train fitting problem) and its transpose to vectors. We justify the T4S model theoretically, and provide nu-merical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

Moreover, the low order terms themselves are subject to approximation error, and this error can overwhelm any potential gains from including higher order terms. While there is no universal best order, our practical experience suggests that a modest order of k = 3 or k = 4 often offers a favorable balance between accuracy and computational expense.

X[i1, . . . , i d] denotes the ( i1, . . . , i d)-entry of a d-dimensional array X.An array entry can be obtained by probing the array with a set of ap-propriately chosen unit basis vectors, similar to how a matrix entry can be obtained from matrix vector multiplication. One evaluates the tensor probe, then selects a single entry from the resulting vector. However, this procedure is inefficient if no other entries from this probe are required. Consequently, conventional tensor approximation methods that require sampling scattered tensor entries are unsuitable for approximating the derivative tensors here. The procedure for probing derivatives of an implicitly defined map, de-tailed in Section 3, involves solving many equations which are linearized ver-sions of the state equation, (2). The number of equations that must be solved 9and the cost to assemble the right hand sides for each equation depend on the amount of repetition in the vectors xi used to probe the derivative tensor. Probes with more repetition are cheaper than probes with less repetition. We learn the T4S model from directionally symmetric probes (probes with max-imal repetition) because they are far cheaper to compute than directionally asymmetric probes. Traditionally, surrogate models are trained on function evaluations at many distinct points. In contrast, our approach trains the surrogate us-ing many derivative probes at a single point. Accuracy is improved not by adding more samples, but by probing the derivative tensor in more random directions, and incorporating data from higher derivative orders. Computing these derivative probes is often cheaper than evaluating the function at new points, since function evaluation may require a nonlinear solve if the state equation is nonlinear (via, e.g., Newton or fixed point iteration), while deriva-tive probes only require linear solves. These linear systems share the same coefficient matrix and differ only in their right hand sides, allowing solvers or preconditioners (often the most expensive components) to be built once and reused across all systems, orders, and probing directions. These advantages have been noted in [85], which trains surrogate models using first derivative information, and our previous work, [7], which uses directionally asymmetric higher order derivative probes. Here, we use directionally symmetric probes, which are even cheaper to compute. In essence, our Taylor series approach sacrifices global accuracy in exchange for cheaper training data. 

## 1.3 Overview of the paper 

Section 2 (Tensor background): We summarize necessary background on arrays, multilinear functions, and the correspondences between them. We also define tensor trains and Tucker tensor trains. 

Section 3 (Derivative probing): We review the computational procedure for probing derivative tensors, and discuss how the cost to compute probes depends on their directional symmetry. Directionally symmetric probes are much cheaper than directionally asymmetric probes. 

Section 4 (Fitting the model): We present methods for constructing the T4S model from random directionally symmetric derivative tensor probes. First, a coarse dimension reduction is performed to reduce the problem 10 from infinite (or very large) dimensions to moderate dimensions, then derivative tensors are fit with Tucker tensor trains using Riemannian manifold optimization methods embedded within a rank continuation scheme. 

Section 5 (Riemannian Jacobian): We propose fast sweeping methods for applying the Riemannian Jacobian (the Jacobian for the Tucker tensor train fitting problem) and its transpose to vectors. This enables efficient application of the Riemannian manifold optimization methods from Section 4. Also, simplifications of the proposed sweeping meth-ods may be used to compute the gradient and perform Gauss-Newton Hessian vector products required by corewise (non-manifold) gradient-based optimization methods. 

Section 6 (Error bounds): We prove that the derivative tensors Dj f (0) may be approximated by the proposed Tucker tensor train decomposi-tion, and establish upper bounds on the Tucker and tensor train ranks in terms of the induced (operator) norm of Dj q(őł0) and the spectral properties of C.

Section 7 (Numerical experiments): We present numerical results that illustrate our methods. 

Appendix A: We discuss Tucker tensor trains in more detail. We note the connection between Tucker tensor trains and low rank matrix unfold-ings and matricizations of a tensor, and present the T3-SVD algorithm, which generalizes the TT-SVD algorithm for tensor trains. We also dis-cuss the structure of the manifold of fixed rank Tucker tensor trains and its tangent space, and present efficient algorithms for working with tangent vectors. 

Appendix B: We provide proofs of results that are stated but not proven in the body of the paper.

Note that algebraic polarization and multilinear symmetry of derivatives imply that the set of all directionally symmetric probes of Dj q completely determines Dj q.25 4 Fitting the model 

This section describes the construction of Tucker tensor trains Tj to ap-proximate higher-order derivatives Dj f (0). First, derivative-based dimen-sion reduction is performed to reduce the infinite dimensional (or very high dimensional) Hilbert spaces X and Y to moderate dimensional Euclidean spaces RN and RM (Section 4.1). This dimension reduction is based on a tensor version of randomized sketching which uses directionally symmetric probes of the derivative tensors to construct orthonormal bases U and V for reduced subspaces of X and Y , respectively. Next, the derivative tensors in the reduced spaces are approximated by Tucker tensor trains Tj . Each Tj is trained using directionally symmetric for-ward and reverse derivative tensor probes on random vectors (Section 4.2). The fitting problem is formulated as a nonlinear least squares problem on the manifold of fixed-rank Tucker tensor trains. We present two methods for solving the fixed rank optimization problem: in Section 4.3.1, we present a trust region Riemannian Gauss-Newton method (TR-RMGN), and in Sec-tion 4.3.2, we present a stochastic manifold gradient descent method with Cauchy step lengths (MC-SGD). The TR-RMGN method inherits the theo-retical guarantees of trust region Riemannian manifold Newton-type meth-ods, which are considered the gold standard among classical methods for solving optimization problems of this form. The MC-SGD method, while lacking the theoretical foundations of the TR-RMGN method, still performs well and is much cheaper per iteration. After solving the optimization problem with a fixed set of Tucker and tensor train ranks, the ranks are increased and the optimization problem is solved again: fit Tj ‚Üí increase ranks ‚Üí fit Tj ‚Üí increase ranks ‚Üí . . . . 

Ranks are increased in a manner designed to maintain balanced edge condi-tion numbers across all edges in the tensor network (Section 4.4). The final tensor train is chosen based on validation performance.

Alternating least squares Alternating least squares (ALS) methods [49, 54, 96] cycle through the tensor cores, fitting one at a time while keeping the others fixed. This process repeats until convergence. Performance typically improves when the fixed cores are orthogonalized relative to the core being updated, as in (69) and (70). In our case, the optimization problem for a single core becomes a linear least squares subproblem which may be solved via conjugate gradients (CG), yielding the ALS-CG method. Full ALS, in which the normal equations for each subproblem are solved exactly using dense linear algebra methods, is typically intractible for large scale problems 35 due to time and memory considerations. Hessian-vector products for the ALS-CG Hessian are computed similarly to those for the TR-RMGN Hessian (Section 5), except that certain terms are set to zero. This makes ALS-CG only slightly cheaper per Hessian-vector product, so the per-iteration CG cost is comparable to TR-RMGN. However, while ALS-CG updates one core at a time, TR-RMGN updates all cores simultaneously. 

Riemannian manifold optimization Alternative Riemannian manifold approaches include Riemannian gradient descent [83] as well as Riemannian manifold nonlinear conjugate gradient methods [66, 100][106, Chapter 9]. Riemannian manifold Newton-type (RMN) methods have been widely ap-plied to low rank matrix completion and related matrix optimization prob-lems [19, 60], which can be interpreted as a type of tensor network optimiza-tion in the special case where the tensor is two-dimensional. RMN methods have also been applied to fitting canonical polyadic (sum of rank-1) tensor decompositions [20]. Works that propose using RMN methods for tensor network optimization include [76] for Tucker tensors, [24, 82, 95, 106] for tensor trains, and [113] for hierarchical Tucker tensors (a generalization of a tensor trains and Tucker tensor trains in which the tensor network has a tree topology). The primary disadvantage of Riemannian manifold methods (including ours) is the requirement to perform retractions at every iteration. The re-traction step requires sweeping orthogonalization and singular value decom-positions, which may be expensive, especially for tensor networks with large ranks. Modern improved methods for tensor network retraction, such as the randomized rounding procedure for tensor trains in [4], have the potential to reduce this cost. Another difficulty with applying RMN methods for tensor network op-timization is efficient computation of Riemannian manifold Hessian vector products. A promising direction in this regard is the work of [82], which pro-poses a method for computing Riemannian manifold Hessian vector products with automatic differentiation in the tensor train context. In our work, we leverage a problem specific method for efficiently computing Gauss-Newton Hessian vector products, presented in Section 5. 36 4.4 Rank continuation 

Starting with small Tucker ranks n and tensor train ranks r, we solve opti-mization problem (24) using the method described in either Section 4.3.1 or Section 4.3.2. The ranks are then incrementally increased, and the optimiza-tion problem is solved again, at each step using the previous solution as the initial guess. We reserve a fraction of the probes (e.g., 20%) as validation data. This validation set is excluded from the nonlinear least-squares fitting problem (22) and is used solely to select the optimal Tucker tensor train ranks. The generalization error, evaluated on the validation data, typically decreases as the ranks increase and subsequently either stabilizes, or increases due to overfitting. We quantify how overdetermined the model is by computing the ratio of the data dimension to the dimension of Mn,r. The rank contin-uation terminates when this ratio exceeds a user-defined threshold. Upon termination, we return the Tucker tensor train with the ranks that achieved the smallest generalization error across all prior iterations.

4.4.2 Initial guess with new ranks 

After determining the updated ranks n‚Ä≤ and r‚Ä≤, we pad the cores of T with zeros so that their dimensions match the new ranks, without changing the represented tensor T . The resulting zero-padded Tucker tensor train is then used as the initial guess for solving nonlinear optimization problem (22) in the next iteration. This initial Tucker tensor train is necessarily degenerate, since the matrix unfoldings and matricizations of T still have their original ranks, r and n.

4.4.3 Alternative approaches for rank adaptivity 

Our method for increasing ranks is inspired by the adaptive rank ALS method for tensor completion in [50]. There, tensor train ranks are continually in-creased and decreased within a regularized ALS scheme. The goal is to maintain a fixed number of ‚Äúminor‚ÄĚ singular values for each of the tensor‚Äôs matrix unfoldings, where ‚Äúminor‚ÄĚ means that the singular value is below a certain threshold which depends on the regularization parameter. Another adaptive rank technique is the modified ALS (MALS) method of [55], which adjusts tensor train ranks by temporarily merging two consecutive 3-tensor cores into one larger 4-tensor. The enlarged tensor is updated via least squares, after which it is factored with a truncated SVD to recover two 3-tensor cores, possibly with an updated rank along the shared edge. In the context of tensor trains for time dependent PDEs, [39] proposes adjusting ranks based on the component of the velocity vector that is per-pendicular to the tangent space. An analogous idea in our setting would be to adapt the rank based on the magnitude of the component of the gradient that is perpendicular to the tangent space. Many standard optimization algorithms must treat the cores of the Tucker tensor train (( Ui)di=1 , (Gi)di=1 ) as independent optimization variables. For in-stance, the popular stochastic method ADAM adjusts each gradient entry based on its variance. This approach is inappropriate from the manifold op-timization perspective, where the optimization variable is the (intractable) full tensor, and the Tucker tensor train cores merely provide a computational representation. Individual core entries of the Riemannian manifold gradient representation have no intrinsic meaning and vary depending on the repre-sentation of the base point for the tangent space. Similarly, classical quasi-Newton methods like L-BFGS rely on forming linear combinations of past gradients. In manifold optimization, this corre-sponds to adding vectors from different tangent spaces, an ill-defined oper-ation (but see [100] for a method addressing this in the manifold nonlinear conjugate-gradient setting). We therefore desire efficient sweeping methods for applying the corewise (non-manifold) Jacobian and its transpose to vectors. Fortunately, such non-manifold sweeping methods are easily obtained as simplifications of the man-ifold sweeping methods already presented. Since a tensor network is multilinear in its cores, perturbing the cores of a tensor train a small amount yields a sum of tensor trains. Each term in the sum is the same as the original tensor train, except for one core which is replaced by its perturbation. The form of the sum is identical to sum (71) which defines a tangent vector, except with the following substitutions: 

Pi ‚Üí Gi, Qi ‚Üí Gi, Oi ‚Üí Gi,

and with the basis cores Ui no longer required to be orthogonal. Therefore, if we make these substitutions in all formulas in Section 5.2.3 and Section 5.2.4, then z computed in Section 5.2.3 represents the application of the corewise Jacobian to the corewise tangent vector (( őīUi), (őīGi))). # 6 Error bounds 

In this section, we prove that Dj f (0) may be approximated by a Tucker tensor train, and provide upper bounds on the Tucker and tensor train ranks 49 based on the induced norm of Dj q(őł0) and the spectral properties of C. The basic argument goes as follows. First, we show that finite dimensional tensors that are preconditioned by matrices with decaying eigenvalues in all but one index may be approximated by tensor trains (Proposition 2). This is done with a peeling process that constructs tensor train cores one at a time. The crux of the argument is the proof of Lemma 3, which shows how to peel off one core. Second, we show that approximately symmetric tensor trains may be approximated by Tucker tensor trains (Lemma 5). Lemma 5 is combined with Proposition 2 to show that finite dimensional symmetric preconditioned tensors may be approximated by Tucker tensor trains (Proposition 6). Third, we generalize the results in Proposition 6 from finite dimensions to infinite dimensions in Lemma 7 and Theorem 8, which shows that our pre-conditioned derivative tensors can be approximated by Tucker tensor trains. Finally, in Corollary 9 we combine Theorem 8 with a standard hyperbolic cross result to provide upper bounds on the Tucker and tensor train ranks in the case where the eigenvalues of C decay according to a power law.

# 7 Numerical experiments 

We present three sets of numerical results to illustrate our methods. In Section 7.1, we use TR-RMGN and MC-SGD to fit symmetric random pre-conditioned tensors with Tucker tensor trains. For these random tensors, we 63 can compare our methods to a T3-SVD benchmark, which is quasi-optimal. Then, in Section 7.2, we use our complete method (using MC-SGD for the fitting step) to form T4S approximations of mappings that depend implicitly on the solution of a Poisson PDE. We present two Poisson PDE examples. The first example, in Section 7.2.1, illustrates T4S approximation of a map-ping for which parameter, őł, is normally distributed. The second example, in Section 7.2.2, illustrates a mapping for which őł is logistically distributed. The first example contains the bulk of our numerical results, while the second example is presented to demonstrate how one might construct T4S models if 

őł is not normally distributed.

## A.3 Connection with matrix unfoldings and matriciza-tions 

The ranks of Tucker, tensor train, and Tucker tensor train decompositions of a tensor are determined by the ranks of the matricizations and matrix unfoldings of the tensor. For a tensor T ‚ąą RN1√ó¬∑¬∑¬∑√ó Nd , let ni denote the rank of its ith matricization and ri the rank of its ith matrix unfolding, and define the rank vectors n = ( n1, . . . , n d), r = (1 , r 1, . . . , r d‚ąí1, 1). The tensor T admits a low-rank Tucker decomposition if and only if its matricizations are low rank, and the minimal Tucker ranks are n. Similarly, T admits a low-rank tensor train decomposition if and only if its matrix unfoldings are low rank, with minimal TT-ranks r. Finally, T admits a Tucker tensor train decomposition if and only if both its matricizations and matrix unfoldings are low rank, in which case the minimal Tucker and TT-ranks are n and r, respectively.
