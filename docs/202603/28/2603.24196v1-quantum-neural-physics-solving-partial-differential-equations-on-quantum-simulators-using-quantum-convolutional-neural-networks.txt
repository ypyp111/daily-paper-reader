Abstract 

In scientific computing, the formulation of numerical discretisations of partial differential equations (PDEs) as untrained convolutional layers within Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), referred to by some as Neural Physics, has demonstrated good efficiency for executing physics-based solvers on GPUs. However, classical grid-based methods still face computational bottlenecks when solving problems involving billions of degrees of freedom. To address this challenge, this paper proposes a novel framework called “ Quantum Neural Physics ” and develops a Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG). This approach maps analytically-determined stencils of discretised differential operators into parameter-free or untrained quantum convolutional kernels. By leveraging amplitude encoding, the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) technique and the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT), the resulting quantum convolutional operators can be imple-mented using quantum circuits with a circuit depth that scales as O(log K), where K denotes the size of the encoded input block. These quantum operators are embedded into a classical W-Cycle multigrid using a U-Net. This design enables seamless integration of quantum operators within a hierarchical solver whilst retaining the robustness and convergence properties of classical multigrid methods. The proposed Quantum Neural Physics solver is validated on a quantum simulator for series of test cases, including the Poisson equation, transient diffusion equation, convection-diffusion equation and incompressible Navier-Stokes equations. The solutions of the HQC-CNNMG are in close agreement with those from traditional solution methods. This work establishes a mapping from discretised physical equations to logarithmic-scale quantum circuits, providing a new and exploratory path to exponential memory compression and computational acceleration for PDE solvers on future fault-tolerant quantum computers.

1. Introduction 

With the rapid advancement of High-Performance Computing (HPC), scientific computing has be-come an indispensable cornerstone in fields such as physics, fluid dynamics and engineering design [1]. Traditional numerical methods, such as the Finite Difference Method (FDM) and the Finite Element Method (FEM), while possessing rigorous theoretical support for solving Partial Differential Equa-tions (PDEs), are often constrained by computational resource consumption and memory bandwidth bottlenecks when facing simulations with large numbers of degrees of freedom [2, 3]. Concurrently, Artificial Intelligence (AI) and one of its core components, Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), have demonstrated great performance in handling large-scale parallel tasks. In recent years, although data-driven surrogate models [4, 5, 6] can achieve near real-time prediction during the inference phase, they are often limited by the distribution of training data when dealing with high-Reynolds-number flows or problems with severe singularities. Consequently, their numerical accuracy and generalisation capabilities still struggle to match the rigorous standards of traditional numerical solvers [7, 8]. In recent years, researchers have explored the equivalence between numerical discretisations and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in terms of mathematical structure: the local discrete schemes of differential operators can essentially be mapped to convolutional kernels with fixed weights [9, 10, 11, 12]. This emerging paradigm, referred to by some as “Neural Physics” [13], offers a compromise: it retains the platform agnosticism and the high-throughput parallel advantages of deep learning frameworks (such as PyTorch, TensorFlow and JAX) [10, 14] and dedicated AI accel-erators (such as GPUs and TPUs) [9], while ensuring the mathematical precision of discrete schemes by analytically determining convolutional kernel weights [15, 16, 17] rather than acquiring them through training. This approach enables researchers to enjoy the computational dividends brought by AI hardware with the accuracy of traditional numerical methods, achieving simulation efficiencies several times to hundreds of times faster than traditional CPU codes [18]. However, with the slowing of Moore’s Law, relying solely on classical semiconductor architectures for improvements in computational power is unlikely to be successful given the challenges of energy efficiency. Quantum computing, as an emerging computational paradigm, demonstrates potential ex-ponential advantages in processing high-dimensional vector spaces and linear operator computations due to quantum superposition and entanglement properties [19, 20]. Quantum Linear System Algo-rithms [21, 22, 23] and Variational Quantum Algorithms [24] provide new possibilities for solving large-scale PDE systems. This paper aims to integrate the advantages of Neural Physics and Quantum Computing by propos-ing a Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG). Our core idea is to trans-form the concept of Neural Physics (expressing discretisations as convolutions) into a quantum circuit representation, i.e., “ Quantum Neural Physics ”. Unlike typical Physics-Informed Neural Net-works (PINNs) [25] or data-driven models that require optimisation of weights by minimising loss functions, our solver adheres to the governing equations by analytically determining the weights of quantum convolutional kernels. We precisely encode mathematical stencils derived from FDM or FEM discretisations (such as the five-point stencil for the Laplacian operator) into quantum oper-ators. Since the weights are strictly defined by physical laws and numerical methods, and do not undergo stochastic training processes, the numerical convergence order of the HQC-CNNMG solver 2is fully consistent with classical schemes, mathematically guaranteeing the rigour of the solution. Traditional solvers face severe memory bottlenecks and the challenge of scalability (where compu-tational cost grows rapidly with increasing grid resolution and problem size) when handling large numbers of degrees of freedom [26, 27]. Our solver leverages quantum parallelism to address efficiency issues through two primary mechanisms: exponentially compressed representation with amplitude en-coding and logarithmic-complexity operator execution with Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) and the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT). With amplitude encoding [28], n qubits can represent grid data in a 2n-dimensional space. This implies that grids of the order of 10 9 points require only approximately 30 qubits for state storage. The quantum convolutional engine, constructed using LCU and QFT [29], implements each convolution on an encoded K × K grid block with a circuit depth scaling as O(log K), where K denotes the size of the encoded grid block. This “matrix-free” quantum computing mode endows the solver with significant asymptotic advantages when extract-ing global physical quantities or when the operator is used as a subroutine within more complex algorithms. In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: 1. Operator Representation for Quantum Computers : For the first time, we map convo-lutional kernels, and restriction and prolongation operators from multigrid methods into LCU and QFT circuits based on qubits, achieving a logarithmic ( O(log K)) quantum circuit depth. 2. Hybrid Quantum-Classical Multigrid Framework : We construct an integrated HQC-CNNMG solver that utilises classical processors (GPUs or CPUs) for managing the global multilevel scheduling using a U-Net architecture [30], while delegating the most time-consuming calculations of local convolutions ( Ax ) and residual transfer between grids to the quantum engine. 3. Matrix-Free Quantum Implementation : Distinct from traditional quantum linear solvers, this method retains the matrix-free property of Neural Physics, utilising amplitude encoding to drastically compress the storage requirements for high-dimensional physical fields. Our approach not only retains the advantages of Neural Physics (training-free and adherence to the discretised equations), but also holds promise, through exponentional compression, for addressing fluid dynamics problems with large numbers of degrees of freedom, on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices and even future fault-tolerant quantum computers. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section 2 elaborates on the numerical dis-cretisation methods, details the design of quantum circuits to represent convolution, restriction and prolongation operators, and proposes the HQC-CNNMG framework. Section 3 demonstrates the performance of this solver on typical benchmark cases, including the Poisson equation, diffusion equation, convection-diffusion equation and incompressible Navier-Stokes equations. Section 4 pro-vides the conclusions. 

2. Methodology 

This section provides the details behind our proposed Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG Solver) and is divided into three parts. First, considering the operators of the partial differential equations (PDEs), we present their numerical discretisation based on Neural 3Physics, that is, transforming discretised differential operators into parameter-free or untrained convolutional operations. Second, considering the solver, we explain the topological equivalence between the classical Multigrid Method and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN/U-Net). Finally, considering the quantum circuit realisation, we detail the representation of core network layers as a quantum circuit and assemble them into the final HQC-CNNMG solver. 

2.1. Neural Physics and Numerical Discretisation

While traditional Finite Difference Methods (FDM) discretise differential operators using difference schemes, the core idea of Neural Physics is to exactly equate numerical discretisation (such as FDM or Finite Element transfor-mations) to convolutional layer operations in neural networks. This approach not only inherits the rigour and high precision of traditional numerical methods but also seamlessly integrates with the underlying optimisations of modern deep learning frameworks, thereby enjoying the extremely high throughput provided by AI acceleration hardware (e.g., GPUs/TPUs). 2.2. Multigrid Method 

After numerically discretising the diffusion and convection terms, we can efficiently perform the forward calculation of Ax . Whether using explicit matrix methods or matrix-free methods (where convolution offers significant storage advantages), forward calculation can be completed efficiently. However, for steady-state problems or implicit time-stepping solutions (e.g., solving the pressure Poisson equation), the most time-consuming part lies in solving the linear system Ax = b. The Multigrid Method, due to its O(N ) time complexity, holds significant advantages in solving large-scale linear systems. 6The Multigrid Method accelerates the convergence of linear systems by constructing multi-scale grid hierarchies: iterative schemes on fine grids efficiently suppress high-frequency errors, while low-frequency errors (which appear smooth on fine grids) manifest as relatively high-frequency on coarse grids, allowing them to be effectively eliminated there. Its core idea involves transferring residu-als to coarser grids via restriction operators, calculating error corrections on the coarse grids and then interpolating these corrections back to the fine grids via prolongation operators to update the solution. Taking the Geometric Multigrid Method as an example, it involves three basic operations: (i) Smoothing: typically performing several local iterations using linear solvers like Jacobi or Gauss-Seidel; (ii) Restriction: mapping residuals from a given fine grid to a coarser grid; (iii) Prolongation: extrapolating error corrections from the coarse grid to the finer grid. A complete multigrid iteration cycle includes the following steps: 1. Smooth η times on the current grid; 2. Calculate the residual and restrict it to a coarser grid; 3. Smooth η times on the coarse grid; 4. Repeat steps (2) and (3) until the desired coarsest grid is reached; 5. Solve the system on the coarsest grid (exactly or approximately); 6. Prolongate the solution from the coarse grid to the fine grid and correct the fine grid solution; 7. Smooth ϕ times on the fine grid; 8. Repeat steps (6) and (7) until returning layer-by-layer to the finest grid. In this paper, we adopt the W-Cycle multigrid strategy. Unlike the V-Cycle, after prolongating from the coarsest grid to an intermediate grid for the first time, the W-Cycle does not directly continue upward to the finest grid. Instead, it recalculates the residual and restricts it back to the coarsest grid, undergoing a second coarse-grid correction before gradually prolongating to the finest grid. Correspondence between Multigrid Method and U-Net Architecture 

If we re-examine the above process from the perspective of deep learning, we can discover that the multigrid method exhibits a high degree of similarity with the classic U-Net neural network architecture in terms of topological structure and information flow transmission. In our matrix-free solver, this connection becomes particularly intuitive: First, in each grid layer, the Ax forward calculation based on differential operator discretisation (i.e., the smoothing step Sν ) is essentially equivalent to a Convolutional Layer in a neural network [33]. Pre-calculated finite difference or finite element templates serve as convolutional kernels with fixed weights, extracting local features through forward convolution operations (physically manifested as eliminating high-frequency error components). Second, scale conversion operations between grids are completely consistent with feature map scaling in deep learning. For the Restriction operation, we use a 2 × 2 convolutional filter with weights all equal to 14 , which is mathematically equivalent to an Average Pooling or Downsampling layer, serving to aggregate neighbourhood information and reduce resolution. For the Prolongation operation, we use a 2 × 2 convolutional filter with weights all equal to 1, assigning a single point value to the surrounding 4 grid points; this is completely equivalent to Nearest-Neighbour Upsampling or Transposed Convolutional layers in neural networks. Based on this correspondence, seamlessly concatenating multiple multigrid cycles essentially con-structs a deeply nested physical-prior neural network (similar to Nested U-Net [34]). The complex recursive restriction and prolongation paths in the W-Cycle act as Skip Connections and multi-scale feature fusion mechanisms in deep networks. 

2.3. Quantum Circuit Representation 

Quantum computing, leveraging its properties of superposition and entanglement, demonstrates potential surpassing classical computation in specific linear algebra tasks, particularly exhibiting exponential advantages in representation and calculation when handling high-dimensional vectors and sparse structured matrices. Therefore, targeting the core operations of the multigrid method, which include the forward matrix-vector multiplication Ax , restriction (coarsening) and prolon-gation (interpolation), we have designed a hybrid quantum-classical computing framework. This framework maps key operators into efficient quantum circuits and coordinates the global iterative process via classical processors, thereby constructing a hybrid multigrid solver. Specifically, we have implemented three types of specialised quantum operators: the Quantum Convolution Operator (for local Ax computation), the Quantum Restriction Operator and the Quantum Prolongation Op-erator (corresponding to grid coarsening and refinement, respectively). 2.5. Quantum Restriction and Prolongation Circuits 

To achieve cross-scale operations in the multigrid method, we further developed two types of lightweight quantum operators: The Quantum Restriction Operator maps a local 2 × 2 block on the fine grid to a single scalar (i.e., the sum of elements within the block). Its circuit requires only 2 qubits: first, load the block data with amplitude encoding, then apply Hadamard gates to all qubits. At this point, the amplitude of the basis state |00 ⟩ is proportional to the sum of the input vector components. By measuring this component and inferring the original norm, the restriction result can be obtained efficiently, with complexity far lower than classical accumulation. The Quantum Prolongation Operator performs the inverse process: uniformly distributing a scalar value to a 2 × 2 block. The circuit similarly consists of two Hadamard gates used to generate the uniform superposition state | ++ ⟩ = 12 (|00 ⟩ + |01 ⟩ + |10 ⟩ + |11 ⟩). By scaling the amplitude of this state to match the input scalar, constant interpolation is achieved. This operation naturally preserves conservation properties, making it suitable for transferring residuals or errors between coarse and fine grids. In summary, these three types of quantum circuits jointly constitute a hybrid quantum-classical multigrid framework oriented towards PDE solving: the quantum processor handles highly parallel, low-dimensional local operations, while the classical CPU/GPU manages global grid scheduling and iteration control, fully leveraging the advantages of both. 

2.6. Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG) 

To accelerate the practical application of scientific computing on current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices and combining the aforementioned correspondence between the Multigrid 12 Method and Convolutional Neural Networks, we propose a novel Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG). From the macroscopic perspective of deep learning, the HQC-CNNMG framework is not merely an efficient algebraic solver but also a physics-driven Quantum Neural Network (QNN). As described in Section 2.2, the classical Multigrid Method is topologically very similar to the U-Net architecture. In this hybrid framework, we systematically replace the core components of the traditional U-Net with logarithmic-depth quantum circuits: utilising quantum convolution circuits for forward feature extraction, quantum restriction operators for downsampling and quantum prolongation operators for upsampling. Therefore, this framework essentially constructs a physics-encoded Quantum U-Net. Under this architecture, the classical computer (CPU/GPU) is responsible for scheduling the macroscopic computational graph, managing skip connections and orchestrating multi-scale feature flow, while the Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) acts as a highly parallel operator layer, executing the most critical local tensor mappings. Specifically, our implementation includes the following key mechanisms: 

• Quantum Acceleration of Ax : On each grid level, the forward calculation Ax is completed via instantiated quantum convolution circuits. Specifically, the input is divided into K × K

blocks, each executed by a pre-constructed quantum convolution circuit performing a K ×K →

(K − 2) × (K − 2) mapping. This engine is based on amplitude encoding and LCU-based block encoding, realising a quantum circuit with depth O(log K).

• Quantum Restriction and Prolongation: Residual coarsening employs 2 × 2 block summation, while error interpolation uses uniform distribution. Both require only 2 qubits and Hadamard interference, with constant circuit depth. 

• Hybrid Sliding Window and Fallback Mechanism: When input blocks are smaller than K × K

(e.g., near boundaries), the system automatically falls back to classical convolution (SciPy) or classical restriction/prolongation, ensuring algorithmic robustness and completeness. 

• W-Cycle Scheduling: The solver adopts the W-Cycle strategy, performing two recursive cor-rections after solving on the coarse grid to more thoroughly eliminate low-frequency errors. All cross-scale data transfers are completed via the aforementioned quantum or classical operators. The time and space complexity of this framework exhibit a clear hierarchical structure: 

• Single Quantum Call: The circuit depths for convolution, restriction and prolongation are 

O(log K), O(1) and O(1) , respectively, requiring O(log K) qubits; 

• Total Overhead per Grid Level: Assuming Nℓ unknowns at a given level, the number of quantum calls is O(Nℓ/K 2) and the total quantum gate count is O  (Nℓ log K)/K 2;

• Overall Multigrid Complexity: In geometric multigrid, the number of levels scales as O(log N )

and the computational workload per level decreases, the total computational volume remains 

O(N ), consistent with classical multigrid, but the local computational units are replaced by logarithmic-depth quantum circuits. Although this framework does not alter the overall asymptotic complexity of O(N ) under clas-sical scheduling, it provides a highly scalable circuit paradigm for scientific computing on future 13 Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computers (FTQC). Once future quantum hardware supports the paral-lel execution of multiple small quantum circuits or integrates efficient Quantum Random Access Memory (QRAM), this HQC-CNNMG architecture can seamlessly evolve into an end-to-end fully quantum multigrid solver, thereby achieving substantial quantum acceleration in extracting specific fluid observables or in embedded large-scale simulations.

4. Conclusion 

This paper proposes a novel “ Quantum Neural Physics ” methodology and, based on this, constructs a Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN Multigrid Solver (HQC-CNNMG). This framework ingeniously maps the numerical discretisation of partial differential equations (PDEs) to operations in an un-trained Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and further transforms its core operators into quantum circuits based on the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) and the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT). Theoretically, our designed quantum convolutional engine features logarithmic qubit re-quirements and an extremely shallow circuit depth of O(log K), providing a theoretical pathway for achieving exponential state compression and logarithmic acceleration in local tensor mappings for large-scale simulations. To validate the reliability of this framework, we conducted extensive numerical experiments on a quantum simulator. The results demonstrate that embedding quantum circuits into U-Net architec-tures set-up to mimic classical multigrid methods yields excellent solution accuracy and physical fidelity. From fundamental problems such as the Poisson equation and unsteady diffusion equation to complex convection-diffusion issues, and further, to the highly nonlinear incompressible Navier-Stokes equations, the HQC-CNNMG solver consistently achieves stable convergence and accurately reproduces classic fluid dynamics phenomena such as the Kármán vortex street. These findings con-firm the feasibility of integrating quantum computing with artificial intelligence to solve complex PDEs. However, we must objectively acknowledge the current limitations of this work. Since all validation experiments were executed on classical quantum simulators (e.g., PennyLane ), and given that State Preparation (encoding classical data into quantum amplitudes) and final Measurement still incur sig-nificant I/O overhead on current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices, this algorithm has not yet demonstrated end-to-end substantial acceleration through physical experimentation, de-spite its asymptotic time complexity advantages at the circuit level. The core value of this study lies in successfully establishing the full-chain mapping from physical equations to quantum circuits at the algorithmic level and verifying its robust numerical convergence accuracy. In the future, we will explore efficient hybrid classical-quantum solution methods as well as fully quantum solution approaches, aiming to execute large-scale computations with higher qubit utilisa-tion and shallower quantum circuits. Furthermore, we intend to broaden the application boundaries of the Quantum Neural Physics method. In preliminary tests, this solver has been successfully ap-plied to flow simulations of urban micro-scale wind environments; we plan to conduct more in-depth investigations into this complex industrial scenario in subsequent research.
